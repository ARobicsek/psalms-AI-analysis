# Commentary on Psalm 60
---
## Introduction
The heading promises a parade. Joab comes home from the northern wars and strikes Edom — twelve thousand dead in the Valley of Salt — a campaign the Bible cross-files under 2 Samuel 8 and 1 Chronicles 18. Then the poem opens its mouth: אֱלֹקִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ, "O God, You have rejected us." A victory title has been bolted onto a defeat poem. And the title carries one word found nowhere else atop a psalm: לְלַמֵּד, "to teach."
There is exactly one other Davidic composition Scripture tags with that instruction. In 2 Samuel 1:18, David's lament over Saul and Jonathan fallen at Gilboa is introduced וַיֹּאמֶר לְלַמֵּד בְּנֵי־יְהוּדָה קָשֶׁת, "and he ordered the teaching of the Bow to the sons of Judah." Both Davidic "teaching" pieces, then, are songs sung *after* a battlefield disaster. And here is the seam no one stitches: the famous crux in our psalm's verse 6 — Masoretic מִפְּנֵי קֹשֶׁט, "because of truth," against the Greek reading קֶשֶׁת, "bow" (τόξον), a single consonant's difference, ט for ת — sets the word קשת right beside Psalm 60's teaching rubric as well. Whatever the Masoretes finally vocalized, the ancient "bow" reading was never an arbitrary stumble. It may belong to a recognizable Davidic genre: the taught song-of-the-bow over the dead. The lesson of this psalm, in other words, is not how to win. It is how to sing while breached.
**A floor plan.** The poem moves in five beats. A long historical superscription (vv. 1–2) files a victory. A complaint (vv. 3–5) charges God, in a chain of accusing verbs, with the disaster itself. A petition (vv. 6–7) asks for a banner and rescue. A divine oracle (vv. 8–10) breaks in: God speaks in the first person, parceling out the land and reducing the enemy nations to household objects. Then the lament resumes (vv. 11–12) — who will get me into Edom, since You have abandoned us? — before a closing turn to confidence (vv. 13–14): human help is worthless; with God we will do valiantly. The architecture is a promise wrapped inside a complaint: the oracle of dominion sits sandwiched between two identical cries of rejection, זְנַחְתָּנוּ in verse 3 and again in verse 12. The triumph never escapes the brackets of the defeat.
**The grammar of the not-yet.** What holds this together is not theme but tense — and the poet has tuned it with precision. Every act of divine destruction in verses 3–5 is in the perfect, the conjugation of completed action: זְנַחְתָּנוּ פְרַצְתָּנוּ, "You rejected us, You breached us; הִרְעַשְׁתָּה, You made the land quake; הִשְׁקִיתָנוּ, You made us drink." Done, accomplished, already presented as fact. The quoted oracle of verse 8 shifts into first-person resolve: אֶעְלֹזָה אֲחַלְּקָה... אֲמַדֵּד, "I will exult, I will divide... I will measure." Willed, not yet executed. And the final triumph of verse 14 lands in the imperfect: יָבוּס צָרֵינוּ, "He will trample our foes" — future, unaccomplished, hoped. The poem's verb system is itself the argument: the breaking is finished, the promise is spoken, the victory has not come. This is why the psalm can end "confident" while Edom remains unreached in verse 11 and the rejection still stands in verse 12. The grammar never once claims an accomplished win. The open ending is not a failure of nerve; it is built into the conjugations.
Notice, too, what the poem's verbs do to the land. In the complaint, they take it apart: פְּצַמְתָּהּ, "You tore it open" — a verb so rare it occurs nowhere else in the Bible — followed by שְׁבָרֶיהָ... מָטָה, "its fractures... it is collapsing." The earth is a wounded body. In the oracle, the very same God picks up a surveyor's line: אֲחַלְּקָה, "I will apportion, אֲמַדֵּד, I will measure," with the insistent possessive לִי, "mine," ringing through the tribal list. The territory God shattered is precisely the territory God re-parcels. When verse 4 begs רְפָה שְׁבָרֶיהָ, "heal its fractures," and verse 8 answers with the measuring-rod, the healing requested and the surveying granted turn out to be the same gesture from opposite ends. The God who fissured the ground takes up the measuring-line.
And the whole victory-defeat friction the superscription opens is localized, with almost suspicious economy, in a single circling place-name. Edom is *defeated* in the title (v. 2), *claimed* in the oracle — עַל־אֱדוֹם אַשְׁלִיךְ נַעֲלִי, "on Edom I cast my shoe" (v. 10) — and humanly *unreachable* in the lament — מִי נָחַנִי עַד־אֱדוֹם, "who will lead me to Edom?" (v. 11). Conquered, claimed, and inaccessible: the entire crisis of the poem can be tracked through one word, three times.
The human experience here is exact and modern. It is the vertigo of losing while officially winning — of standing inside a catastrophe that the records will later sanitize into a triumph. The Syrian poet Nizar Qabbani caught it precisely after the Arab defeat of 1967, in his *Marginal Notes on the Book of Defeat*:
> إذا خسرنا الحربَ لا غرابهْ
> لأننا ندخُلها..
> بكلِّ ما يملكُ الشرقيُّ من مواهبِ الخطابهْ
> "If we lost the war, it is no surprise, / because we enter it / with all the oratorical talents of the East."
Qabbani sets the official banner against the soldier's wound and names the banner as the cause of the wreckage. Psalm 60 performs a similar collision — third-person victory in the header, first-person plural grief in the body — but draws the opposite conclusion. Where Qabbani indicts the rhetoric, the psalmist indicts, and then appeals to, the hand that struck: same God, with perfect tense for the wound and imperfect for the cure.
This is the discipline the rubric לְלַמֵּד names. The psalm does not teach that defeat was secretly victory, nor that God will surely turn it around tomorrow. It teaches how to hold a spoken promise and an unhealed fracture in the same breath — to keep the oracle of dominion and the cry of abandonment in one poem without letting either dissolve the other. That a psalm could be assigned for memorization on exactly this basis may be the lesson of the genre David founded at Gilboa.
So the one thing to carry away: read the verbs. Psalm 60 ends with יָבוּס, "He will trample" — and that verb is future, not past. The poem never grammatically arrives at its victory. It teaches the harder thing, which is how to stand in confidence inside a defeat that has not yet been reversed.

#### Full psalm
Psalm 60 is not part of the fixed daily, Sabbath, or festival psalmody in any of the major rites. Its accusatory opening and its unresolved lament make it awkward as communal praise — one does not easily daven in the register of "O God, You have rejected us, You have breached us." Its afterlife in the siddur is therefore almost entirely by *fragment*, where single verses are lifted out and redeployed.
#### Key verses
The most widely used line is verse 7, לְמַעַן יֵחָלְצוּן יְדִידֶיךָ הוֹשִׁיעָה יְמִינְךָ וַעֲנֵנִי, "That those whom You love might be rescued, deliver with Your right hand and answer me." In Nusach Sefard (the Hasidic rite) and among Edot HaMizrach, it is appended to the close of the Amidah, inside the expansions of אֱלֹקַי נְצֹר, at weekday Shacharit and Mincha and at Shabbat Musaf and Mincha. It follows directly upon the chain עֲשֵׂה לְמַעַן שְׁמָךְ... עֲשֵׂה לְמַעַן יְמִינָךְ, "act for the sake of Your name... act for the sake of Your right hand." The librarian's puzzle — why *this* verse — has a plain answer: catchword. The preceding plea climaxes on יְמִינָךְ, "Your right hand," and verse 7 supplies הוֹשִׁיעָה יְמִינְךָ, "save with Your right hand." The ימין hook draws the verse into the slot. Significantly, the rite keeps the singular Qere וַעֲנֵנִי, "answer *me*" — the very grammatical seam that makes a national lament-verse usable as one worshipper's private cry, and the basis for its use as a "name-verse" segulah for those whose name begins with ל and ends with י.
Verse 13, הָבָה־לָּנוּ עֶזְרָת מִצָּר, "Grant us aid against the foe," surfaces in the Edot HaMizrach machzor at Neilah, the closing of Yom Kippur, woven into a penitential piyyut: יוֹשֵׁב הַכְּרוּבִים הוֹפִיעָה, הָבָה לָּנוּ עֶזְרַת מִצָּר, הֲיַד ה׳ תִּקְצָר, "Enthroned on the cherubim, appear; grant us aid against the foe — is the LORD's hand too short?" The verse's original military "foe" is reheard at the gates' closing as the accumulated enemy of sin and exile.
Verse 6's נֵס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס, "a banner to be unfurled," migrates into the Rosh Hashanah Musaf piyyutim, where the battlefield ensign becomes a cosmic standard "for upper and lower realms" at the opening of the heavenly books; and the fuller phrase נֵס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס מִפְּנֵי קֹשֶׁט appears in the Hasidic rite's selichot for the fast of the Twentieth of Sivan, where the martyrs themselves become the "banner raised because of truth."

## Verse-by-Verse Commentary
**Verse 1**
לַמְנַצֵּחַ עַל־שׁוּשַׁן עֵדוּת; מִכְתָּם לְדָוִד לְלַמֵּד׃
Three opaque terms are compressed into one heading, and they were already opaque to antiquity. שׁוּשַׁן עֵדוּת, "lily of testimony," defeated even the ancient translators: the Septuagint renders it as if from a root meaning "to alter, change," nothing like a lily. Ibn Ezra and Meiri treat שׁוּשַׁן as a musical instrument or tune-name and עֵדוּת from the root of עֲדִי, "ornament" — Meiri: שושן הוא נגזר... משושנים ועדות הוא מלשון עדי, "shoshan is derived from lilies, and eduth from 'adornment.'" The contrast is worth a half-second: a poem stuffed with breaches, staggering wine, washbasins, and trampling is filed under a floral, delicate-sounding label.
מִכְתָּם is the genuine puzzle, clustering only in Psalms 16 and 56–60. The Hebrew lexicographers tie it to כֶּתֶם, "fine gold" — Rashi quotes כֶּתֶם פָּז... דָּבָר חָמוּד, "fine gold, a precious thing," and Metzudat David glosses the psalm as אהוב לו כעדי, "beloved to him like an ornament." But the Septuagint hears something else entirely: στηλογραφία, "a stele-inscription" — a monument carved in stone for permanence. That is a striking divergence. If the Greek preserves an older sense, this psalm was conceived not merely as a prayer but as a public victory-text, the kind of thing a king inscribes on rock — which only sharpens the irony that its body laments a loss.
The capstone is לְלַמֵּד, "to teach," unique as a psalm-heading and pointing, as the essay argued, straight at 2 Samuel 1:18's לְלַמֵּד בְּנֵי־יְהוּדָה קָשֶׁת. Meiri alone among the classical voices registers the didactic strangeness, citing a midrash that the psalm is "necessary for instruction" precisely because of the legal quandary Joab faced fighting Aram. Metzudat David draws the lesson cleanly: עשוי ללמד דעת את העם איך הכל בא בהשגחה, "made to teach the people how everything comes through Providence."
**Verse 2**
בְּהַצּוֹתוֹ אֶת־אֲרַם נַהֲרַיִם וְאֶת־אֲרַם צוֹבָה; וַיָּשָׁב יוֹאָב וַיַּךְ אֶת־אֱדוֹם בְּגֵיא־מֶלַח שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר אָלֶף׃
The longest historical superscription in the Psalter is also the most embarrassed by its own parallels. Three canonical sources credit the same Edomite slaughter to three different commanders: 2 Samuel 8:13 to David, 1 Chronicles 18:12 to Abishai (וְאַבְשַׁי בֶּן־צְרוּיָה הִכָּה אֶת־אֱדוֹם בְּגֵיא הַמֶּלַח שְׁמוֹנָה עָשָׂר אָלֶף, "Abishai son of Zeruiah struck Edom in the Valley of Salt... eighteen thousand"), and our verse to Joab — and the count splits, 12,000 here against 18,000 there. Rashi harmonizes by arithmetic: אבישי... ששת אלפים, ויואב שנים עשר אלף, "Abishai six thousand, Joab twelve thousand." The very need for such patching hints that the liturgical heading has drifted free of any single event — fitting for a psalm rubbed smooth for reuse.
The opening verb בְּהַצּוֹתוֹ is itself contested. The standard reading takes the root נצה, "to strive, do battle"; Rashi compares בְּהַצּוֹתָם עַל ה׳ (Num 26:9), "when they quarreled against the LORD." But the Septuagint reads ἐμπυρίζω, "to set on fire," implying a root יצת or צות, "to kindle." Two different operations — pitched battle versus incendiary warfare — arising from different root analyses.
The toponym carries its own freight. גֵּיא־מֶלַח, "Valley of Salt," is where salt means desolation and curse (Deut 29:22; the sowing of Shechem with salt in Judg 9:45). It became a recurring placeholder for the Edomite wound: Amaziah fights the same battle in the same valley generations later (2 Kgs 14:7). The friction between this victory header and the lament that follows is the engine of the whole psalm; Qabbani's collision of banner and trauma, quoted in the essay, is its modern cousin.
**Verse 3**
אֱלֹקִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ פְרַצְתָּנוּ; אָנַפְתָּ תְּשׁוֹבֵב לָנוּ׃
Three accusing perfects, then one abrupt plea — and they are bound by sound as much as by sense. The phonetics show zə-naḥ-**TĀ**-nū, fə-rats-**TĀ**-nū hammering on the same suffix, ‑tānû, "[You did it] to us," a grammatical rhyme that will recur at הִשְׁקִיתָנוּ in verse 5 and then return, as the bracket of the whole lament, in זְנַחְתָּנוּ at verse 12. The accusation has an acoustic signature.
זנח is not mild absence; it is deliberate repudiation. The concordance shows it as the standard verb of God's apparent rejection — לָמָה זְנַחְתָּנִי, "why have You rejected me?" (Ps 43:2); לָמָה אֱלֹקִים זָנַחְתָּ לָנֶצַח, "why, O God, have You rejected forever?" (Ps 74:1). פָּרַץ, "to breach," is sharper still: it is the verb of David's own victory at בַּעַל פְּרָצִים, where פָּרַץ ה׳ אֶת־אֹיְבַי לְפָנַי כְּפֶרֶץ מָיִם, "the LORD has burst through my enemies before me like a bursting of waters" (2 Sam 5:20). The same God who once breached David's *enemies* has now breached *us* — Metzudat David: כמו עיר שנפרץ חומתה, "like a city whose wall is breached," through which the war pours from every side.
What no English captures is the closing verb. תְּשׁוֹבֵב, "restore us," uses a rare intensive form where the ordinary causative הָשֵׁב would do. The escalation matters: not "bring us back" but "fully reconstitute us." The same divine energy that broke is conscripted to rebuild thoroughly. Tellingly, no sin is named as cause; the indictment stands raw. Malbim hears the logic of Isaiah 12:1's reversal — אָנַפְתָּ בִּי יָשֹׁב אַפְּךָ וּתְנַחֲמֵנִי, "You were angry with me; let Your anger turn away, and comfort me."
**Verse 4**
הִרְעַשְׁתָּה אֶרֶץ פְּצַמְתָּהּ; רְפָה שְׁבָרֶיהָ כִי־מָטָה׃
Mid-verse, the earthquake becomes a medical chart. God "made the land quake" and tore it open; then the plea is רְפָה שְׁבָרֶיהָ, "heal its fractures" — רפה being a body-doctor's verb, where one would expect חַזֵּק, "fortify," or בָּנָה, "rebuild." The Septuagint confirms the register with ἰάομαι, "to heal." The land is a patient on the table, and the surgeon is the one who wounded it. The figurative tradition makes the move unmistakable: Jeremiah 30:17, וּמִמַּכּוֹתַיִךְ אֶרְפָּאֵךְ, "and from your wounds I will heal you," and Psalm 147:3, הָרֹפֵא לִשְׁבוּרֵי לֵב, "who heals the broken-hearted." The logic is: You broke it, so You heal it.
פְּצַמְתָּהּ, "You split it open," is a genuine hapax legomenon — found nowhere else in the Bible — and the poet seems to have reached for obscurity deliberately, bypassing the available בָּקַע, פָּצַח, or פָּקַע. Rashi confesses the difficulty, citing Dunash that it is Arabic and Moshe Hadarshan that it means "tearing," with the Targumic ופצים for "he cut out windows" (Jer 22:14). The rarity itself marks the rupture as unrepeatable. The closing כִי־מָטָה, "for it is tottering," keeps the land mid-fall, not yet fallen, which is exactly what makes the imperative urgent.
The collapse touches creation-order. Compare Du Fu, the great Tang poet, writing in 757 CE while the An Lushan rebellion had shattered the empire and held him captive in the fallen capital. His *Spring Prospect* opens:
> 國破山河在
> 城春草木深
> "The nation is shattered, but mountains and rivers remain; / city in spring, grass and trees grow deep."
Du Fu draws an ironic line *between* the body politic and the physical earth: the state is broken, yet the land indifferently endures. The psalmist refuses that consolation. For him the two are one — when the kingdom falls, the dirt itself cracks open and totters. Where the Chinese poet finds a stubborn natural permanence beneath political ruin, the Hebrew poet finds the ground giving way underfoot, an ontological emergency that only the Maker of the ground can mend.
**Verse 5**
הִרְאִיתָ עַמְּךָ קָשָׁה; הִשְׁקִיתָנוּ יַיִן תַּרְעֵלָה׃
הִרְאִיתָ adds a cruel optical twist: "You made Your people *see* hardship" — not merely suffer it but witness it. And קָשָׁה, "hard things," is the precise adjective of Egyptian bondage, עֲבֹדָה קָשָׁה (Exod 1:14), planting a quiet Egypt-typology: defeat as renewed slavery.
The wine of staggering belongs to a well-mapped prophetic motif, the cup of divine wrath. תַּרְעֵלָה, "reeling," is rare: Isaiah 51:17, כּוֹס הַתַּרְעֵלָה, "the cup of reeling," and Zechariah 12:2, סַף־רַעַל, "a bowl of reeling." The Septuagint intensifies it to κατάνυξις, "stupor, numbness" — not drunken swaying but full anesthesia. Rashi reads similarly, taking רעל as "envelopment, stopping up: הָאוֹטֵם אֶת הַלֵּב, that stops up the heart." God is the bartender of judgment — כּוֹס בְּיַד־ה׳, "a cup is in the LORD's hand" (Ps 75:9).
But here is the buried point. In the prophets this cup is eventually *passed on* to the nations: גַּם־עָלַיִךְ תַּעֲבָר־כּוֹס תִּשְׁכְּרִי, "to you too the cup shall pass; you shall get drunk," Lamentations says to Edom (4:21); Habakkuk turns it on the oppressor, תִּסּוֹב עָלֶיךָ כּוֹס יְמִין ה׳, "the cup of the LORD's right hand shall come round to you" (2:16). Psalm 60 stands at the painful *earlier* moment — Israel drinks first — and never completes the transfer. The enemy nations in verses 8–10 are degraded as washbasin and sandal-target, but no cup is ever handed to them. The unreversed cup matches the unrealized triumph; the poem withholds the closing reversal it could have asserted.
Wilfred Owen secularized exactly this image of forced intoxication. Writing from the trenches of the First World War, gassed and shell-shocked, he describes his men in *Dulce et Decorum Est*:
> Bent double, like old beggars under sacks... / Men marched asleep. Many had lost their boots, / But limped on, blood-shod. All went lame; all blind; / Drunk with fatigue...
Owen's "drunk with fatigue" is the wine of reeling stripped of God — the staggering arises from sheer abandonment, not from a hand pouring the cup. The psalmist's version is in some ways harder to bear, because the intoxication is administered: there is an agent. Euripides pushes it further still in the *Bacchae* (405 BCE), where Dionysus boasts that he has driven the women of Thebes μανίαις, "with madness," into involuntary frenzy as punishment. Both the Greek tragedian and the Hebrew poet weaponize wine — the usual sign of joy — into an instrument of cognitive fracture struck down from the divine hand. The causative verb הִשְׁקִיתָנוּ, "You made us drink," is Dionysus's boast in Hebrew dress.
**Verse 6**
נָתַתָּה לִּירֵאֶיךָ נֵּס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס; מִפְּנֵי קֹשֶׁט סֶלָה׃
A banner appears — but a banner is a curious choice. The poet did not ask for a shield or a fortress; he asked for a signal-flag, נֵּס, the thing that does not itself protect but gathers, identifies, reorders scattered troops. After the disorientation of the staggering wine, the people need first to be *regrouped* under a sign, not yet defended. The phrase נֵּס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס pairs a noun with a verb from its own root (נסס) — a banner that banners itself.
And the sound is unstable in a way that suits a routed army. The phrase לְהִתְנוֹסֵס invites hearing against נוס, "to flee," and the Septuagint takes the line in that direction: where the Masoretic מִפְּנֵי קֹשֶׁט reads "because of truth," the Greek reads קֶשֶׁת, "bow," yielding "a banner, to flee from before the bow" (φεύγειν ἀπὸ προσώπου τόξου). One consonant, ט versus ת, pivots the line from covenant-theology to battlefield tactics. Syriac and Symmachus back the Masoretic "truth"; and as the essay argued, the קשת reading may preserve the fingerprint of the same taught-song-of-the-bow tradition that gave us 2 Samuel 1:18. The Selah falls right on the crux, marking the pause before the explicit plea of verse 7.
The medieval reading takes a third path. Rashi puns נֵּס into נִסָּיוֹן, "trial: נִסְיוֹנוֹת שֶׁל צָרוֹת, trials of many troubles," given לְהִתְנוֹסֵס, "to be tested" — and מִפְּנֵי קֹשֶׁט as לְקַשֵּׁט מִדּוֹתֶיךָ, "to vindicate Your justice in the world," so that the nations cannot fault God's goodness to a people who have stood the test.
**Verse 7**
לְמַעַן יֵחָלְצוּן יְדִידֶיךָ; הוֹשִׁיעָה יְמִינְךָ וַעֲנֵנִי׃
Across three verses the addressed community is renamed with rising intimacy: עַמְּךָ, "Your people" (v. 5), then יְרֵאֶיךָ, "those who fear You" (v. 6), now יְדִידֶיךָ, "Your beloved." This is love-poetry vocabulary: it names Benjamin in Deuteronomy 33:12, יְדִיד ה׳, and stands inside Nathan's name for Solomon יְדִידְיָה (2 Sam 12:25). The petition narrows from nation to intimates exactly as it turns from complaint to request — and it is this most intimate verse, not the cooler "people" or "fearers," that the liturgy later lifts out as a personal cry.
The verse carries a famous textual seam. The Ketiv is וַעֲנֵנוּ, "answer *us*"; the Qere וַעֲנֵנִי, "answer *me*" — a singular royal "I" puncturing the plural prayer, with everything around it (יֵחָלְצוּן, יְדִידֶיךָ) staying plural. The same variant is preserved at the parallel Psalm 108:7, which suggests genuine ancient uncertainty, not a one-off slip — perhaps the trace of a royal petition adapted for communal use. The Septuagint's singular ἐπάκουσόν μου supports the Qere. And it is precisely this singular trace that lets a national lament become "my" prayer: as noted in the liturgical section, the verse enters the post-Amidah coda by catching on the יְמִינְךָ, "Your right hand," of the preceding pleas, keeping וַעֲנֵנִי so each worshipper can ask God to answer *him*.
**Verse 8**
אֱלֹקִים דִּבֶּר בְּקׇדְשׁוֹ אֶעְלֹזָה; אֲחַלְּקָה שְׁכֶם וְעֵמֶק סֻכּוֹת אֲמַדֵּד׃
The hinge of the psalm, and a deliberate ambiguity sits in its first phrase. דִּבֶּר בְּקׇדְשׁוֹ can mean "spoke in His holiness," an oath sworn on God's own being (compare נִשְׁבַּע בְּקׇדְשׁוֹ, "He swore by His holiness," Ps 89:36), or "spoke in His sanctuary," an oracle delivered in the temple. The ambiguity is productive rather than vexing: if the speech is grounded in God's *holiness* — His very nature — it is the more irrevocable, which is exactly what the rest of the psalm leans on. The medievals split: Ibn Ezra and Radak read בְּרוּחַ קָדְשׁוֹ, "by His holy spirit," prophecy to Samuel that David's kingship would stand.
Then come the first-person resolve forms — אֶעְלֹזָה, "I will exult, אֲחַלְּקָה, I will apportion, אֲמַדֵּד, I will measure" — and a real ambiguity of *voice*. Is God still speaking, exulting over His own land-grant? Or has the king seamlessly taken up the divine speech as his own? The poem fuses them, and that fusion is a theology of kingship: the king's territorial claim simply *is* God's, voiced in one mouth.
The vocabulary is the surveyor's and the conqueror's. חלק is the verb of Joshua's land-allotment (Josh 18–19); אֲמַדֵּד, "I will measure out," makes God a cartographer. שְׁכֶם (west of the Jordan) plus עֵמֶק סֻכּוֹת (east of it) form a pair that spans both sides of the Jordan — and both are Jacob's first settlements on returning from Aram (Gen 33:17–18), which may not be accidental in a psalm about wars with Aram. As the essay argued, this is the structural answer to verse 4: the God who tore the ground (פְּצַמְתָּהּ) now measures it (אֲמַדֵּד); the apportioning *is* the requested healing of its fractures.
**Verse 9**
לִי גִלְעָד וְלִי מְנַשֶּׁה; וְאֶפְרַיִם מָעוֹז רֹאשִׁי יְהוּדָה מְחֹקְקִי׃
The repeated לִי, "mine... mine," presses the claim like a deed of title over the contested Transjordan and north. Then the tribes are mapped onto a warrior's body: אֶפְרַיִם מָעוֹז רֹאשִׁי, literally "Ephraim is the stronghold of my head" — a helmet — and יְהוּדָה מְחֹקְקִי, "Judah my scepter." The pairing encodes a precise theology of the united monarchy: the dominant northern tribe supplies the army's protective strength, the southern royal tribe supplies the ruling staff. The Septuagint confirms the helmet sense with κραταίωσις τῆς κεφαλῆς, "fortification of the head."
מְחֹקְקִי, from חקק, "to engrave, decree," is a direct quotation of Jacob's blessing — לֹא־יָסוּר שֵׁבֶט מִיהוּדָה וּמְחֹקֵק מִבֵּין רַגְלָיו, "the scepter shall not depart from Judah, nor the ruler's staff from between his feet" (Gen 49:10). Rashi makes the link explicit, glossing מְחוֹקֵק as one שֶׁמְּחוֹקֵק וְשׁוֹלֵחַ סְפָרִים וּמְצַוֶּה, "who decrees and sends out documents and commands." The Septuagint amplifies past "lawgiver" to βασιλεύς, "my king."
**Verse 10**
מוֹאָב סִיר רַחְצִי; עַל־אֱדוֹם אַשְׁלִיךְ נַעֲלִי; עָלַי פְּלֶשֶׁת הִתְרוֹעָעִי׃
Three enemies, three escalating gestures of contempt. Moab is reduced to סִיר רַחְצִי, "my washbasin" — the foot-washing pot, a servant's tool holding dirty water, the lowest household utility. Radak is exact: not a cooking pot but כלי שישפוך אדם בו מי רחיצת הכלים, "the vessel into which one pours the dirty rinse-water." A nation famed for its haughtiness becomes the basin for grimy feet.
Edom receives the cast sandal — אַשְׁלִיךְ נַעֲלִי, "I throw my shoe" — and this can be heard as legal language, not mere insult. In Israelite custom the sandal can mark transfer of property: שָׁלַף אִישׁ נַעֲלוֹ וְנָתַן לְרֵעֵהוּ, "a man drew off his sandal and gave it to his fellow" (Ruth 4:7); the same object marks the public renunciation of claim in the levirate law of Deuteronomy 25:9. The Nuzi tablets preserve the cuneiform formula "he lifted his foot from his land and set the foot of [the buyer] in it." To fling one's shoe *onto* a territory can signal a claim of ownership. Malbim catches the master-slave logic: חליצת נעל רבו והולכת כליו לבית המרחץ, "removing the master's sandal and carrying his vessels to the bathhouse" — Moab the basin, Edom the sandal, both signs of servitude.
Philistia gets הִתְרוֹעָעִי, a genuinely two-edged imperative. It can mean "raise a shout *for* me" — a vassal's acclamation — or "raise a war-cry *against* me," received with contempt, as if to say *go ahead and try*. The ambiguity is productive: either way Philistia is cast in a subordinate role, and the Septuagint's ὑποτάσσω, "be subjected," forecloses the double-reading by resolving it. The washbasin's reach into cultural memory is long: Charles Spurgeon preached a whole sermon titled "Moab Is My Washpot," and Stephen Fry borrowed the phrase for the title of his memoir, applying the image of the vessel that holds society's defiled water to the humiliations of his adolescence — three millennia on, the domestic-military metaphor still carries its sting.
**Verse 11**
מִי יֹבִלֵנִי עִיר מָצוֹר; מִי נָחַנִי עַד־אֱדוֹם׃
The jolt is structural. One verse after God flung His sandal *onto* Edom in sovereign possession, the human speaker cannot even get *into* it: מִי נָחַנִי עַד־אֱדוֹם, "who will lead me to Edom?" Divine ownership and human inaccessibility collide in adjacent lines. עִיר מָצוֹר, "the fortified city," is identified by Radak and modern readers alike as Sela/Petra, the rock-hewn Edomite capital that Amaziah later renamed (2 Kgs 14:7) — notoriously difficult to assault because of its natural geography. The oracle declared Edom God's property; experience finds its capital sealed shut. The whole psalm's tension lives in that miniature.
The double מִי, "who?", is the rhetoric of dependence: the implied answer is "no one — only God." And the forms dislocate again. יֹבִלֵנִי, "will bring me," is future-looking; נָחַנִי is formally past. Ibn Ezra reads the whole as collapsed hope: הייתי מחשב מי יובילני... והנה מחשבתי נהפכה, "I kept thinking, who will bring me... and behold my plan was overturned," because — the next verse — "have You not, O God, rejected us?" God was the only possible guide; God's withdrawal has stranded the army at the threshold of its goal.
This is the stall that Chief Joseph of the Nez Perce voiced in his 1877 surrender at the Bear Paw Mountains, having led his people on a fighting retreat of more than a thousand miles only to be cornered short of the Canadian border:
> "I am tired of fighting. Our chiefs are killed... He who led on the young men is dead. It is cold, and we have no blankets... From where the sun now stands I will fight no more forever."
Where the psalmist asks "who will lead me?" and finds the answer in God's absence, Chief Joseph answers the same question with a roll-call of the dead — *there is no one left to lead*. Both texts hinge on the crushing inventory of who is missing at the decisive march. The difference is the direction of the energy: Joseph's repetitions close the door on battle forever; the psalmist's unanswered מִי becomes the lever that pries the next plea out of him.
**Verse 12**
הֲלֹא־אַתָּה אֱלֹקִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ; וְלֹא־תֵצֵא אֱלֹקִים בְּצִבְקוֹתֵינוּ׃
זְנַחְתָּנוּ returns verbatim from verse 3, slamming the bracket shut on the oracle. The הֲלֹא, "have You not...?", is not a real question but a rhetorical affirmation: *indeed You have rejected us*. The promise of dominion (vv. 8–10) sits nested between two identical statements of rejection — and as noted at verse 3, the bracket is reinforced acoustically by the ‑tānû rhyme. The oracle did not erase the abandonment; lived experience has not yet caught up to the divine speech.
The barb is in בְּצִבְקוֹתֵינוּ, "with our armies. צְבָקוֹת, hosts," is the very word in God's warrior-title ה׳ צְבָקוֹת, "LORD of Hosts" — and here is a quiet structural irony worth pausing over. Psalm 60 belongs to the Elohistic Psalter, the block of psalms that largely prefers the name ה׳ and calls God אֱלֹקִים. The Tetragrammaton-bearing title "LORD of *Hosts*" is therefore largely unavailable to this poem. So the *only* צְבָקוֹת in the psalm is human — "*our* hosts" — and that is precisely the company God declines to march with. The redactional name-suppression may sharpen the bitterness of verse 12: the title that would name God as commander of armies is absent exactly where the psalm grieves that God will not join the armies. Holy-war theology (Deut 20; Josh 5–6) assumes the divine warrior goes out at the front; his non-appearance is covenant abandonment itself. Radak softens it by hindsight: God *was* absent earlier, but עתה כשרצית בנו, "now that You have favored us," the victories prove He returned.
**Verse 13**
הָבָה־לָּנוּ עֶזְרָת מִצָּר; וְשָׁוְא תְּשׁוּעַת אָדָם׃
הָבָה, "grant!", is an archaic, emphatic imperative of יהב, the old form heard in Genesis (הָבָה נִבְנֶה, "come, let us build," 11:3) but absent from standard prose — more urgent and primal than the ordinary תֵּן. Its archaism signals a foundational, ground-level appeal.
The verse's force lies in its inverted word order, which throws שָׁוְא, "worthless," to the front: "Worthless — that is what man's deliverance is." And שָׁוְא is a loaded choice. It is the exact word of the Decalogue's prohibition, לֹא תִשָּׂא אֶת־שֵׁם ה׳ אֱלֹקֶיךָ לַשָּׁוְא, "you shall not take the name of the LORD your God in vain" (Exod 20:7). Human military salvation is filed in the same drawer as a false oath — a radical devaluation. The same theology powers Psalm 127:1, שָׁוְא עָמְלוּ בוֹנָיו, "in vain do its builders labor," unless the LORD builds. Note, too, the closing soundplay: the unreachable עִיר מָצוֹר of verse 11 (mā-**TSŌR**) and the besieging foe מִצָּר of verse 13 (mits-**TSĀR**) ring on the same consonants מ-צ-ר, the field of constriction and siege — the fortress one cannot enter and the distress one cannot escape, bound by sound at the psalm's close.
**Verse 14**
בֵּאלֹקִים נַעֲשֶׂה־חָיִל; וְהוּא יָבוּס צָרֵינוּ׃
The communal "we" reasserts itself against the isolated "I" of verse 11: נַעֲשֶׂה, "*we* shall do valiantly." עָשָׂה חַיִל is an old idiom of triumph — וַעֲשֵׂה־חַיִל בְּאֶפְרָתָה, "do valiantly in Ephrathah" (Ruth 4:11); יְמִין ה׳ עֹשָׂה חָיִל, "the LORD's right hand does valiantly" (Ps 118:16). The crucial particle is the בְּ of בֵּאלֹקִים — "*by means of* God," not autonomous human prowess. Agency is shared but never independent. Spurgeon put the balance well: divine working is "not an argument for human inaction, but… the best excitement for courageous effort."
And the final word completes the grammar the whole psalm has been building toward. יָבוּס, "He will trample," is imperfect — future, unaccomplished. בּוּס is no genteel "defeat" but bodily crushing, as in תִּהְיֶה לְמִרְמָס כְּטִיט חוּצוֹת, "she shall be trampled like the mud of the streets" (Mic 7:10). Radak hears it directly: כמו בוסו בטיט חוצות, "like 'trample in the street-mud.'" The Septuagint flattens it to ἐξουδενώσει, "he will bring to nothing," losing the visceral foot. Ibn Ezra reads the verb prophetically — מתנבא ברוח הקדש שהוא ינצח באחרונה, "he prophesies by the holy spirit that He will be victorious *in the end*."
That phrase — *in the end* — is the whole point. The psalm that opened in finished perfects of destruction closes in an unfinished imperfect of victory. It never grammatically arrives. The enemies are still standing; Edom is still unreached; the rejection of verse 12 has not been retracted. What the psalm has taught — the burden of its unique לְלַמֵּד — is not how the story ends but how to stand confident inside the part of it that has not ended yet.---
