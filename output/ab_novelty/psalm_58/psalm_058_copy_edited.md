# Commentary on Psalm 58
---
## Introduction
Psalm 58 carries, in its very first line, an instruction it spends the next eleven verses cheerfully ignoring. The heading reads אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת, "Do not destroy" — and then the poem proceeds to beg God to destroy the wicked in something like seven different ways: smash their teeth, melt them like spilled water, dissolve them like a snail, abort them before they see daylight, whirl them off in a storm. A title that forbids destruction, pasted over a body that pleads for nothing else.
The tidy solution is that אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת is merely a tune name, the opening words of some lost melody, shared with Psalms 57, 59, and 75. But the rabbinic tradition heard something sharper, and to a one they heard the same thing. Rashi, Radak, Meiri, and Metzudat David all anchor this psalm to a single scene: 1 Samuel 26, where David slips into Saul's sleeping camp by night and Abishai whispers, *let me pin him to the ground with one stroke — I won't need a second.* David refuses: אַל־תַּשְׁחִיתֵהוּ, "Do not destroy him" (1 Sam 26:9) — the same root שחת, the same negated command. The man who could have ended his persecutor by his own hand walked off with the king's spear instead.
Read against that night, the paradox quietly resolves. *Do not destroy — by your own hand.* Hand the destruction upward. The superscription and the poem are not at war; they are the same sentence said twice, and the whole psalm lives in the gap between the violence a wronged man could do and the violence he refuses to do, choosing instead to lay the case before a higher bench.
**A lawsuit, tried organ by organ**
The psalm is built as a courtroom proceeding, and it moves cleanly through the four phases of one. Verses 2–3 are the indictment, flung in the second person at corrupt judges. Verses 4–6 present the evidence: the defendants are wicked by birth and deaf by choice. Verses 7–10 are the sentence, a cascade of dissolution-curses. Verses 11–12 are the aftermath — the verdict seen, and the public confession that justice was real after all. The frame is sealed by a single repeated word: בָּאָרֶץ, "in the land / on earth," names the site of their violence in v. 3 and the site of God's judgment in v. 12. The same arena that hosts the crime hosts the sentencing.
But the deeper architecture is anatomical. This psalm prosecutes its case through the body's instruments of communication, and tracks justice as the retuning of each one to honest function. Watch the organs. The **mouth** is supposed to speak צֶדֶק, "justice" (v. 2); instead it speaks כָזָב, "lies" (v. 4), and drips חֵמָה, "venom" (v. 5). The **ear** seals itself shut — the wicked are a deaf cobra that יַאְטֵם אָזְנוֹ, "stops up its ear" (v. 5), so as not to hear (v. 6). And here is the move that organizes everything: because the ear is sealed, persuasion is finished. No charmer's voice can reach a creature that has plugged its own hearing. So when God finally acts, His very first blow lands not on the hands that "deal out violence" in v. 3, but on the **mouth** — הֲרֹס שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ, "smash their teeth in their mouth" (v. 7). The oddly redundant "in their mouth" — where else would teeth be? — flags the target precisely. The organ of venomous speech, unreachable by sound, is destroyed directly.
The verdict then passes to the **eye**. The poet chooses the verb חזה, the prophetic "behold" of Isaiah and Amos, not the everyday רָאָה — and he uses it twice as the axis of the whole judgment. The stillbirth בַּל־חָזוּ שָׁמֶשׁ, "never sees the sun" (v. 9); the righteous man כִּי־חָזָה נָקָם, "when he beholds vengeance" (v. 11). To be cancelled is to never see; to be vindicated is to see. And the case closes where it began — at the mouth — when וְיֹאמַר אָדָם, "humanity will say" (v. 12), and ordinary people, restored to true speech, confess what the corrupt mouths denied: there is justice on earth.
The grammar even keeps time with this. In the face-to-face indictment of vv. 2–3, the verbs carry an old, formal second-person ending — תְּדַבֵּרוּן (`tə-dhabbē-RŪN`), תִּפְעָלוּן, תְּפַלֵּסוּן — the sound of a prosecutor addressing the accused as "you." Once the poem turns from the defendants to God and prays *against* them, it switches to the archaic third-person -מוֹ: לָמוֹ (v. 5), שִׁנֵּימוֹ, פִּימוֹ (v. 7). The shift from *-ūn* to *-mô* is the audible signature of the turn from accusing them to their face to praying about them before the judge.
**The competence that fails at the one moment it matters**
The poet builds his enemies up as masters of calculation precisely so he can watch the calculation collapse. In v. 3 they "weigh out" violence — תְּפַלֵּסוּן, from פלס, the verb of careful, calibrated measurement. In v. 6 they are the מְחֻכָּם, the "expert," the consummate charmer, חוֹבֵר חֲבָרִים, "binder of bindings," a highly emphatic phrase of mastery. And then, in v. 10, they are swept off בְּטֶרֶם יָבִינוּ — "before they discern" what is happening to them. The hyper-competent are caught uncomprehending.
The choice of פלס in v. 3 is sharper still than generic irony. A proverb assigns that very instrument to God: פֶּלֶס וּמֹאזְנֵי מִשְׁפָּט לַה׳, "a just balance and scales belong to the LORD" (Prov 16:11). The corrupt judges have not merely been unfair; they have picked up the LORD's own scale and used it to portion out brutality. Which is why the psalm's closing declaration, אֱלֹקִים שֹׁפְטִים בָּאָרֶץ, "God judges on earth" (v. 12), is not a bland moral tag. It is the instrument repossessed.
W. H. Auden caught the same hollowness in "Law Like Love" (1940): *"Law is as I've told you before, / Law is as you know I suppose, / ... Law is The Law."* The judge defines justice by his own circular authority — exactly the fraud Psalm 58 names when it asks the "mighty ones" whether they *really* speak what is just.
So here is the single thing to carry away. The wicked are punished, in this psalm, by having the right organs silenced and the right organs reopened — the lying mouth smashed, the willing eye permitted at last to see, the human voice restored to honest speech. Justice, for this poet, is not finally a matter of force. It is the re-tuning of the body's instruments of speech, hearing, and sight back to their true use — which is why the case cannot close until somebody, with a clean mouth, can simply say that it is so.

#### Full psalm
Psalm 58 has no place in the fixed daily, Sabbath, or festival liturgy of any major rite. Its unrelenting second-person prosecution and its sevenfold curse make it nearly unusable as communal prayer — one does not easily lead a congregation in "their venom is like the venom of a snake." Its survival in worship is therefore fragmentary and oblique, and the two places it does surface are both telling.
#### Key verses
The fullest appearance is in **Perek Shirah**, the "Chapter of Song," a non-statutory mystical anthology in which each creature of creation is assigned a biblical verse to "sing." It is printed at the back of many siddurim, including the Hasidic (Nusach Sefard) rite, and recited as private devotion, often after the weekday morning service. The snail's song is Psalm 58:9 in full: שַׁבְּלוּל אוֹמֵר כְּמוֹ שַׁבְּלוּל תֶּמֶס יַהֲלֹךְ, "the snail says, 'Like a snail that melts away as it goes.'" Commentators have puzzled over why a contemplative bestiary would hand the humble snail a curse against the wicked rather than a verse of praise. The puzzle dissolves once you notice that שַׁבְּלוּל is a hapax legomenon — Psalm 58:9 is the *only* verse in the entire Hebrew Bible that contains the word "snail." A compiler who wanted the creature to sing a verse that *names* it had, in all of Scripture, exactly one option.
The single verse drawn into statutory prayer is **58:7**, הֲרֹס שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ, "smash their teeth in their mouth." It appears once, in the Ashkenazic Yom Kippur Shacharit, woven into a *selichah* (penitential poem) inside the fourth blessing of the Amidah, punctuated by the refrain חָטָאנוּ, "we have sinned: הֲרֹס שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ וְיֻדְמָמוּ ... לָקַחַת נַפְשִׁי זָמָמוּ: חָטָאנוּ — smash their teeth in their mouth and let them be silenced ... they plotted to take my soul: we have sinned." The placement is quietly profound. Dietrich Bonhoeffer argued that a guilty mortal cannot honestly hurl a divine curse; the imprecation is sayable only by the sinless. The machzor reaches the same conclusion from the other end: it permits the curse against one's enemies *only* when it is wrapped inside the confession of one's own guilt. The wording is adjusted to fit the poem's rhyme (the divine name dropped, a parallel verb וְיֻדְמָמוּ added), the standard midrashic handling of a borrowed verse.

## Psalm 58

1. לַמְנַצֵּ֥חַ אַל־תַּשְׁחֵ֗ת לְדָוִ֥ד מִכְתָּֽם׃‎		For the leader; al tashḥeth. Of David. A michtam.
2. הַאֻמְנָ֗ם אֵ֣לֶם צֶ֭דֶק תְּדַבֵּר֑וּן מֵישָׁרִ֥ים תִּ֝שְׁפְּט֗וּ בְּנֵ֣י אָדָֽם׃‎		O mighty ones,-a do you really decree what is just?
Do you judge mankind with equity?
3. אַף־בְּלֵב֮ עוֹלֹ֢ת תִּפְעָ֫ל֥וּן בָּאָ֡רֶץ חֲמַ֥ס יְ֝דֵיכֶ֗ם תְּפַלֵּסֽוּן׃‎		In your minds you devise wrongdoing in the land;
with your hands you deal out lawlessness.-a
4. זֹ֣רוּ רְשָׁעִ֣ים מֵרָ֑חֶם תָּע֥וּ מִ֝בֶּ֗טֶן דֹּבְרֵ֥י כָזָֽב׃‎		The wicked are defiant from birth;
the liars go astray from the womb.
5. חֲמַת־לָ֗מוֹ כִּדְמ֥וּת חֲמַת־נָחָ֑שׁ כְּמוֹ־פֶ֥תֶן חֵ֝רֵ֗שׁ יַאְטֵ֥ם אׇזְנֽוֹ׃‎		Their venom is like that of a snake,
a deaf viper that stops its ears

or the expert mutterer of spells.

shatter the fangs of lions, O LORD;
8. יִמָּאֲס֣וּ כְמוֹ־מַ֭יִם יִתְהַלְּכוּ־לָ֑מוֹ יִדְרֹ֥ךְ חִ֝צָּ֗ו כְּמ֣וֹ יִתְמֹלָֽלוּ׃‎		let them melt, let them vanish like water;
let Him aim His arrows that they be cut down;
9. כְּמ֣וֹ שַׁ֭בְּלוּל תֶּ֣מֶס יַהֲלֹ֑ךְ נֵ֥פֶל אֵ֝֗שֶׁת בַּל־חָ֥זוּ שָֽׁמֶשׁ׃‎		like a snail that melts away as it moves;-a

## Verse-by-Verse Commentary
**Verse 1**
לַמְנַצֵּ֥חַ אַל־תַּשְׁחֵ֗ת לְדָוִ֥ד מִכְתָּֽם׃
The superscription's central word, אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת, "Do not destroy," does its main work in the essay; one further point is worth adding. The root שחת is the language of total ruin — it describes the pre-flood earth (Gen 6:11) and the wiping-out of Sodom (Gen 18:28). But it also stands at the heart of Isaiah's most merciful image: כַּאֲשֶׁר יִמָּצֵא הַתִּירוֹשׁ בָּאֶשְׁכּוֹל וְאָמַר אַל־תַּשְׁחִיתֵהוּ כִּי בְרָכָה בּוֹ, "as when wine is found in the cluster, and one says, 'Do not destroy it, for a blessing is in it'" (Isa 65:8). The same two words — "do not destroy" — that head a psalm of curses are, elsewhere, a vintner's plea to spare the good grape. The reader who hears that resonance may hear the psalm's restraint built into its title from the start.
מִכְתָּם, "a michtam," is one of the genuinely opaque psalm terms, clustering only in Psalms 16 and 56–60 — the Davidic crisis poems. The competing etymologies are revealing: כֶּתֶם, "gold" (a golden poem); Akkadian *katāmu*, "to cover"; and a writing-related etymology behind the LXX's εἰς στηλογραφίαν, "for a stele inscription." The Greek translator, in other words, already found the word hard enough that he reached for "monument inscription" — an ancient confession of uncertainty. Rashi simply calls it שֵׁם נְעִימוּת שִׁיר, "the name of a song's melody," and declines to guess further. לְדָוִד, "of David," carries the attribution; the medieval commentators read the whole heading through the lens of the Saul narratives.
**Verse 2**
הַאֻמְנָ֗ם אֵ֣לֶם צֶ֭דֶק תְּדַבֵּר֑וּן; מֵישָׁרִ֥ים תִּ֝שְׁפְּט֗וּ בְּנֵ֣י אָדָֽם׃
The opening particle הַאֻמְנָם (`ha-'um-NĀM`), "is it really so?", is no neutral question. It belongs, across the Bible, to adversarial and incredulous mouths: Balak's reproach to Balaam, הַאֻמְנָם לֹא אוּכַל כַּבְּדֶךָ, "can I really not honor you?" (Num 22:37), and Solomon's astonished הַאֻמְנָם יֵשֵׁב אֱלֹקִים עַל־הָאָרֶץ, "will God really dwell on earth?" (1 Kgs 8:27). It opens a prosecution, and it expects a damning answer — which v. 3's אַף, "indeed," promptly supplies.
The second word, אֵלֶם, is the crux of the whole psalm. The unpointed consonants permit several readings. Repointed אֵלִם, it means "gods, mighty ones" — the divine-council beings of בְּנֵי אֵלִים, "sons of gods" (Ps 29:1), making the addressees the corrupt celestial judges of Psalm 82. Left as אֵלֶם it means "silence," producing the bitter paradox that Ibn Ezra's teacher "Rabbi Moshe" preferred: *because you have gone mute (נֶאֱלַמְתֶּם) about justice* — judges struck dumb in the face of wrong. Meiri offers yet another route, connecting it to אוּלָם, the judicial "hall," so that the line addresses "you who sit in the seat of judgment." The Septuagint cut the knot by cutting the word: faced with the hardest term in the psalm, the translator dropped it and smoothed the line into a more ordinary second-person question — εἰ ἀληθῶς ἄρα δικαιοσύνην λαλεῖτε — rather than trying to represent the crux directly. The Talmud (Chullin 89a) found a moral in the ambiguity itself: מַה אֻמָּנוּתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה — יָשִׂים עַצְמוֹ כְּאִלֵּם, "what is a person's craft in this world? To make himself as a mute" — but, lest one fall silent over Torah too, צֶדֶק תְּדַבֵּרוּן, "speak righteousness."
מֵישָׁרִים, "equity," is royal-justice vocabulary (יָדִין עַמִּים בְּמֵישָׁרִים, "He judges peoples with equity," Ps 96:10) — the ideal standard, here held up only to expose its inversion. The verb תְּדַבֵּרוּן carries the archaic, elevated *-ūn* ending that marks the face-to-face confrontation of this opening stretch.
**Verse 3**
אַף־בְּלֵב֮ עוֹלֹ֢ת תִּפְעָ֫ל֥וּן; בָּאָ֡רֶץ חֲמַ֥ס יְ֝דֵיכֶ֗ם תְּפַלֵּסֽוּן׃
The verse maps corruption onto the body: it is conceived בְּלֵב, "in the heart," and executed בְּיָד, "by the hand" — cognition and action both contaminated. Rashi notes a small orthographic teaser in עוֹלֹת, "wrongs": written defectively, it shares its consonants with עוֹלוֹת, "burnt offerings," as though the judges were sacrificing justice on an altar of their own.
The governing irony lives in תְּפַלֵּסוּן, "you weigh out," from פלס, the verb of precise, calibrated balancing. Ibn Ezra catches it exactly: תַּעֲשׂוּ חָמָס כְּאִלּוּ הוּא מְפֻלָּס, "you commit violence as though it were weighed out on a scale" — corruption not careless but expertly measured. As the essay argues, the bite is theological: Proverbs hands that very instrument to God — פֶּלֶס וּמֹאזְנֵי מִשְׁפָּט לַה׳, "a just balance and scales belong to the LORD" (Prov 16:11), and elsewhere כָל־מַעְגְּלֹתָיו מְפַלֵּס, "He weighs all his paths" (Prov 5:21). The judges have not merely been unjust; they have stolen the LORD's scale.
חֲמַס, "violence," is the word for the wrong that filled the earth before the flood (Gen 6:11). Its sound — `ḥa-MAS` — will return, transmuted, as חֲמַת, "venom," in v. 5, binding the indictment to the serpent simile. בָּאָרֶץ, "in the land," opens the frame that the final verse will close.
**Verse 4**
זֹ֣רוּ רְשָׁעִ֣ים מֵרָ֑חֶם; תָּע֥וּ מִ֝בֶּ֗טֶן דֹּבְרֵ֥י כָזָֽב׃
The first verb, זֹרוּ, is itself ambiguous, and the ambiguity matters theologically. From זוּר it means "are estranged, are alien"; from זָרָה, "are scattered." Rashi takes the first decisively: מִמְּעֵי אִמָּם הֵם נַעֲשִׂים זָרִים לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, "from their mother's womb they become strangers to the Holy One," and he reaches for Esau, struggling already in Rebekah's belly. The Septuagint agrees, rendering ἀπηλλοτριώθησαν, "they were alienated" — an ontological claim: the wicked are *other* by origin, not merely by behavior.
Malbim presses a sharp distinction between the verse's two halves. The plain רְשָׁעִים, he says, are זֹרוּ מֵרָחֶם, estranged *after* birth — corrupted later by bad company — for human nature is not born evil. But the דֹּבְרֵי כָזָב, "speakers of lies," the hypocrites, are תָּעוּ מִבֶּטֶן, "go astray from the belly," before they even emerge: their evil is עֲצְמִי וְטִבְעִי, "essential and inborn." The two birth-words, מֵרָחֶם and מִבֶּטֶן, are not idle synonyms but a graded intensification.
This womb-language is the forward pole of an arc the psalm will close in v. 9, where the same wicked are wished into the condition of the נֵפֶל, the stillbirth that never sees the sun. The phrase דֹּבְרֵי כָזָב, "speakers of lies," answers v. 2 directly: they were asked to speak צֶדֶק; from birth they speak כָזָב.
**Verse 5**
חֲמַת־לָ֗מוֹ כִּדְמ֥וּת חֲמַת־נָחָ֑שׁ; כְּמוֹ־פֶ֥תֶן חֵ֝רֵ֗שׁ יַאְטֵ֥ם אׇזְנֽוֹ׃
The word חֲמַת is a deliberate double exposure: it is "venom" (poison of a snake) and "wrath/heat" (חֵמָה) at once — the judges' rage *is* their poison. Its repetition within the line, `ḥa-math-LĀ-mō ... ḥa-math-nā-ḤĀSH`, and its echo of חֲמַס (`ḥa-MAS`) from v. 3 fuse violence and venom by sound. This is not a private quirk of Psalm 58; the same cluster appears almost intact in a sister psalm: שָׁנְנוּ לְשׁוֹנָם כְּמוֹ־נָחָשׁ חֲמַת עַכְשׁוּב תַּחַת שְׂפָתֵימוֹ, "they sharpened their tongue like a serpent; the venom of an adder is under their lips" (Ps 140:4). Venomous speech was a fixed image, and the poet is invoking it knowingly.
The comparison is unusually layered: not simple כְּמוֹ but כִּדְמוּת, "like the likeness of." That form belongs above all to Ezekiel's visionary throne-scenes — כִּדְמוּת ... דְּמוּת אָדָם, "the likeness of ... a human likeness" (Ezek 1:5, 26). The extra layer of distancing slightly heightens the simile.
Then the focus snaps: not the venom but the *deafness* is the point — פֶּתֶן חֵרֵשׁ, "a deaf cobra," that יַאְטֵם אָזְנוֹ, "stops up its ear." The verb אטם is active, deliberate sealing. The snake does not fail to hear; it *refuses*. Isaiah uses the identical idiom for the truly righteous man, אֹטֵם אָזְנוֹ מִשְּׁמֹעַ דָּמִים, "who stops his ear from hearing of bloodshed" (Isa 33:15) — the wicked here perform the same gesture toward correction. Rashi offers the folk-zoology behind the image: the aging snake goes deaf in one ear and presses the other into the dust so as not to hear the charmer's spell. The theology is what counts: this evil is not ignorant but incorrigible.
The charmer's craft is itself the subject of an old literary trope. In Apollonius of Rhodes' *Argonautica* (3rd c. BCE), Medea lulls the sleepless dragon guarding the Golden Fleece with her sweet, hissing voice: ἡ δέ μιν ... γλυκερῇ ἐνοπῇ θέλγε ... ὕπνος γὰρ ἐπὶ σφετέρῃσι μελῳδίαις ... ὄσσε σκοτόεντα, "she charmed him with her sweet voice ... for sleep, cast upon him by her melodies, [overshadowed] his dark eyes." Apollonius' serpent succumbs to the song; that is a familiar ancient expectation of how charmer and snake are supposed to interact. Psalm 58 invokes exactly this script — and then breaks it. The viper that stops its own ear is an evil so absolute it has opted out of the mechanism of control altogether. Where Medea's art proves human mastery over the deadly creature, the psalmist's deaf cobra proves the limit of all art, and prepares the ground for v. 7: if no voice can reach it, only God can act.
**Verse 6**
אֲשֶׁ֣ר לֹֽא־יִ֭שְׁמַע לְק֣וֹל מְלַחֲשִׁ֑ים; חוֹבֵ֖ר חֲבָרִ֣ים מְחֻכָּֽם׃
The verse completes the image with a precise acoustic irony. מְלַחֲשִׁים, "whisperers," is the root לחש, the hiss of the snake-charmer's incantation — the quietest, most intimate form of human speech, the last refined attempt at persuasion. And it is refused.
חוֹבֵר חֲבָרִים, "binder of bindings," is a cognate accusative — a highly emphatic construction, doubling the root for absolute effect: the *consummate* spellbinder. The phrase is lifted straight from the list of forbidden arts in Deut 18:11, וְחֹבֵר חָבֶר. Such acoustic snake-binding is well attested across the ancient Near East: the Ugaritic incantation texts (KTU 1.100 and others) describe priests binding serpents with whispered spells and sacred materials. The poet evokes the entire apparatus of expert magical control — מְחֻכָּם, "expert," literally "made wise" — at the exact moment of its total failure.
That is the structural argument the essay traces: the psalm maximizes human competence precisely where it proves useless. The wisest charmer who ever lived cannot penetrate a self-sealed ear. Radak draws the line plainly: אֵלֶּה הָאֲנָשִׁים לֹא יִשְׁמְעוּ לְקוֹל הַצַּדִּיק בַּמִּשְׁפָּט, "these men will not heed the voice of the righteous one in judgment." When persuasion is exhausted, only demolition remains — which is the next verse.
**Verse 7**
אֱֽלֹקִ֗ים הֲרׇס־שִׁנֵּ֥ימֽוֹ בְּפִ֑ימוֹ; מַלְתְּע֥וֹת כְּ֝פִירִ֗ים נְתֹ֣ץ ׀ ה׳׃
This is the pivot of the psalm, and it is marked twice over. It is the only verse carrying both divine names — אֱלֹקִים opening, ה׳ closing — and they bracket the two destruction-verbs like an envelope. The line is tightly framed: divine name / destruction verb ... destruction verb / divine name, with the predators' jaws caught between the two summonses. The double name is the hinge: God as universal power and the LORD as covenant partner, called together at the precise line where the lawsuit is handed up to the bench, and the address turns from the human "you" to the divine "Thou."
הֲרֹס, "smash," is architectural-demolition vocabulary. Applied to teeth, it treats the mouth as a structure to be razed. The phrase שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ, "their teeth in their mouth," looks redundant — but, as the essay argues, the redundancy is the point: the weapon is destroyed *in situ*, at the very site of its deployment, the lying-and-poisoning mouth of vv. 2–5. נְתֹץ, "shatter," and מַלְתְּעוֹת כְּפִירִים, "the fangs of young lions," escalate the bestiary from venomous snake to predatory cat. The image of God breaking predatory teeth is conventional: שִׁנֵּי רְשָׁעִים שִׁבַּרְתָּ, "You have broken the teeth of the wicked" (Ps 3:8), and Job's וָאֲשַׁבְּרָה מְתַלְּעוֹת עַוָּל וּמִשִּׁנָּיו אַשְׁלִיךְ טָרֶף, "I broke the fangs of the wrongdoer and made him drop his prey" (Job 29:17). The last makes the logic explicit: breaking the teeth is not gratuitous; it liberates the victims. Malbim reads the prayer as a plea that God shatter the jaws even after the prey is seized, so the predator cannot consume it.
For this verse's afterlife — its lone appearance in the Yom Kippur penitential liturgy, and how that placement answers Bonhoeffer's problem of the guilty curser — see the liturgical section above.
**Verse 8**
יִמָּאֲס֣וּ כְמוֹ־מַ֭יִם יִתְהַלְּכוּ־לָ֑מוֹ; יִדְרֹ֥ךְ חִ֝צָּ֗ו כְּמ֣וֹ יִתְמֹלָֽלוּ׃
The sentence now turns from breaking to dissolving. יִמָּאֲסוּ, literally "let them be despised/rejected," is read by most as a variant of מסס, "melt" — the א standing in for a doubled consonant (so Ibn Ezra and Radak: וְיִמַּס לְבַב הָעָם, "the people's heart melted"). The picture is of water that יִתְהַלְּכוּ, "walks itself away," running off and vanishing without a trace. The verb הלך, "walk," will recur in the snail of v. 9 — running water and dissolving snail share a vocabulary of motion-into-nothingness.
The second half is among the psalm's hard knots. יִדְרֹךְ חִצָּו, "let him tread his arrows," omits the expected "bow" — דרך is the verb for treading a bowstring taut, but the object here is the arrow itself. Ibn Ezra supplies the missing archer (God), reading "let *the one who treads* draw his arrows against them." Stranger still is כְּמוֹ יִתְמֹלָלוּ — the comparison particle כְּמוֹ standing before a finite verb, which is grammatically unusual; כְּמוֹ normally introduces a noun. The verb itself, from מלל, means "wither" or "be cut off," the same word used of grass that יָמוֹלֵל וְיָבֵשׁ, "withers and dries" (Ps 90:6). The arrows, as Malbim reads it, are snapped at the tip like a cut-off ear of grain. Rashi takes the whole line as God aiming His arrows "so that they be cut down." Divine arrows are standard judgment-imagery: וַיֹּרֵם אֱלֹקִים חֵץ פִּתְאֹם, "God shoots an arrow at them suddenly" (Ps 64:8).
**Verse 9**
כְּמ֣וֹ שַׁ֭בְּלוּל תֶּ֣מֶס יַהֲלֹ֑ךְ; נֵ֥פֶל אֵ֝֗שֶׁת בַּל־חָ֥זוּ שָֽׁמֶשׁ׃
שַׁבְּלוּל, "snail," is an absolute hapax — it occurs nowhere else in the Bible, which is exactly why the snail's "song" in Perek Shirah had to be this graphic curse rather than a verse of praise (see the liturgical section). The ancient folk-belief is preserved in the image: the snail seems to dissolve into its own slime-trail as it crawls, so that תֶּמֶס יַהֲלֹךְ, "it melts as it goes." The Septuagint did not recognize the creature and substituted κηρός, "wax" — converting an unknown Semitic snail into the familiar Greek picture of wax melting before fire (cf. Ps 68:3). Within the psalm's bestiary the snail completes a steady deflation: the feared cobra of v. 5 and the predatory lion of v. 7 end as a lowly mollusk consuming itself in motion.
The parallel image, נֵפֶל אֵשֶׁת, "a woman's stillbirth," closes the birth-arc opened in v. 4. The wicked, born wicked, are now wished into the state of the never-born. The vehicle is shared with the bitterest passages in Scripture: Job's אוֹ כְנֵפֶל טָמוּן לֹא אֶהְיֶה ... לֹא־רָאוּ אוֹר, "or like a hidden stillbirth, I would not be ... who never saw light" (Job 3:16), and Qohelet's verdict that טוֹב מִמֶּנּוּ הַנָּפֶל, "the stillbirth is better off than he" (Eccl 6:3). The Talmud (Moed Katan 6b) and the Targum took אֵשֶׁת as an underground creature — מַאי אִישׁוּת? ... שֶׁאֵין לָהּ עֵינַיִם, "what is *ishut*? A creature with no eyes," the blind mole that never surfaces — keying directly off בַּל־חָזוּ שָׁמֶשׁ, "never saw the sun."
That verb of seeing, חזה, is the hinge of the whole judgment. It is the prophetic "behold," and the stillbirth's failure to *behold* the sun (v. 9) is answered by the righteous man who *beholds* vengeance (v. 11): non-vision and vision as the two poles of the verdict.
There may be an Assyrian backbone here too. The Vassal Treaty of Esarhaddon curses the disloyal vassal in similar terms: "may they melt away like a snail." The resemblance suggests a stock treaty-curse image, re-aimed here — not at a vassal who broke faith with a king, but at judges who broke faith with justice, and not before capricious gods but before the LORD's own moral order.
**Verse 10**
בְּטֶ֤רֶם ׀ יָבִ֣ינוּ סִּירֹתֵכֶ֣ם אָטָ֑ד; כְּמוֹ־חַ֥י כְּמוֹ־חָ֝ר֗וֹן יִשְׂעָרֶֽנּוּ׃
This is, by wide consent, the most untranslatable verse in the psalm, and the commentators frankly disagree about its every word. The crux is סִּירֹתֵיכֶם אָטָד. סִּיר can mean "thornbush" or "cooking-pot," and אָטָד is a specific bramble. Rashi reads it botanically — "before your tender briars become hardened thorns," i.e., before the wicked's children grow up to full malice. Radak and Malbim read it culinarily, leaning on the cooking image: "before your pots feel the heat of the bramble-fire beneath them," before the meat is even warmed, judgment falls — sudden ruin interrupting an everyday meal. The pot-image deliberately recalls Qohelet's כְּקוֹל הַסִּירִים תַּחַת הַסִּיר, "like the crackling of thorns under a pot" (Eccl 7:6), the proverbial sound of brief, loud, pointless combustion. Hai Gaon, cited by Ibn Ezra, splits the difference: סִּירוֹת are the *buds* of the thornbush, אָטָד the matured thorns — eradication before the evil ripens.
The phrase בְּטֶרֶם יָבִינוּ, "before they discern," lands the psalm's quiet wisdom-irony. The men who "weighed out" violence with precision (v. 3) and were the "expert" charmers (v. 6) are now swept off before they can even register what is happening — their celebrated discernment failing at the only moment it would have mattered.
The verb יִשְׂעָרֶנּוּ, "He will whirl him away," is storm-theophany language: ה׳ ... בְּסוּפָה וּבִשְׂעָרָה דַּרְכּוֹ, "the LORD — His way is in whirlwind and storm" (Nah 1:3). The mid-verse jump from second-person "your pots" to third-person "He will whirl *him*" troubled the commentators; Meiri simply accepts it as prophetic style, וְדֶרֶךְ הַנְּבוּאוֹת לְדַבֵּר פַּעַם לְשׁוֹן יָחִיד פַּעַם לְשׁוֹן רַבִּים, "the manner of prophecy is to speak now in singular, now in plural."
**Verse 11**
יִשְׂמַ֣ח צַ֭דִּיק כִּי־חָזָ֣ה נָקָ֑ם; פְּעָמָ֥יו יִ֝רְחַ֗ץ בְּדַ֣ם הָרָשָֽׁע׃
The choice of חָזָה for "see" is pointed. It is the prophet's verb — the word behind Amos who חָזָה עַל־יִשְׂרָאֵל, "had a vision concerning Israel" (Amos 1:1), and Habakkuk's הַמַּשָּׂא אֲשֶׁר חָזָה, "the oracle that he beheld" (Hab 1:1). The righteous man does not merely *notice* vengeance; he perceives it as a revealed event, long awaited in faith and now witnessed. This sight-as-vindication is the shared theology of Psalm 58's two nearest kin: Psalm 11 (עֵינָיו יֶחֱזוּ ... יָשָׁר יֶחֱזוּ פָנֵימוֹ, "His eyes behold ... the upright will behold His face") and Psalm 17 (עֵינֶיךָ תֶּחֱזֶינָה מֵישָׁרִים, "Your eyes behold equity; אֲנִי בְּצֶדֶק אֶחֱזֶה פָנֶיךָ, I in righteousness will behold Your face"). In all three, the righteous are precisely those *granted to see*.
The shocking image — פְּעָמָיו יִרְחַץ בְּדַם הָרָשָׁע, "he bathes his feet in the blood of the wicked" — is conventional ancient-Near-Eastern victory iconography, not private sadism. It has ancient-Near-Eastern parallels; in Ugaritic myth Anat wades knee-deep in the gore of slaughtered enemies (KTU 1.3 II). But Psalm 58 strips the worshiper of any active role: God executes the vengeance; the righteous merely walk through the aftermath. The Septuagint, perhaps uneasy, read "hands" (χεῖρας) for "feet," aligning it with clean-hands imagery. The aggadah (preserved in Torah Temimah) attached the verse to a grim scene: Esau's head, severed by Hushim son of Dan at Jacob's burial, rolling to rest at Jacob's feet — ישמח צדיק כי חזה נקם, the patriarch's eyes opening in a smile.
The line's disturbing emotional logic — joy at bloodshed — has its great modern challenger in Hayim Nachman Bialik, who wrote "On the Slaughter" (עַל הַשְּׁחִיטָה) in 1903 in the immediate aftermath of the Kishinev pogrom, as Jewish blood ran in the streets of a Russian town. Where the psalmist promises the righteous closure in vengeance, Bialik curses the very wish for it: וְאָרוּר הָאוֹמֵר: נְקֹם! / נְקָמָה כָזֹאת, נִקְמַת דַּם יֶלֶד קָטָן / עוֹד לֹא־בָרָא הַשָּׂטָן — / וְיִקֹּב הַדָּם אֶת־הַתְּהוֹם!, "And cursed be he who says: Avenge! / Such vengeance — vengeance for the blood of a little child — / Satan has not yet devised — / Let the blood pierce the abyss!" Bialik takes the psalm's own imprecatory mode and turns it inside out: the spilled blood will not be answered or washed in, only swallowed unredeemed into the depths. The two poems share the conviction that innocent blood demands a cosmic response; they part absolutely on whether vengeance could ever be that response. Set beside Bialik, the psalmist's confidence that the righteous will one day *rejoice* at the sight measures the distance between a poet who still trusted the verdict and a poet who had watched it fail to come.
**Verse 12**
וְיֹאמַ֣ר אָ֭דָם אַךְ־פְּרִ֣י לַצַּדִּ֑יק; אַ֥ךְ יֵשׁ־אֱ֝לֹקִ֗ים שֹׁפְטִ֥ים בָּאָֽרֶץ׃
The psalm ends not in the poet's own voice but in the mouth of אָדָם, generic humanity — the same בְּנֵי אָדָם, "sons of man," who were the victims of corrupt judgment back in v. 2. The ones judged unjustly become the witnesses who confess that justice was real. This is the speech-arc's resolution: corrupt mouths (v. 2), a willfully deaf ear (vv. 5–6), enforced silence (vv. 7–10), and now ordinary humanity restored to true speech.
The doubled אַךְ, "surely ... surely" (`'AKH ... 'AKH`), gives the confession its emphatic cadence, answering the skeptical הַאֻמְנָם, "is it really?", that opened the poem. The question is closed with a resounding yes. פְּרִי, "fruit," casts reward in agricultural terms — righteousness yields produce — drawing on a settled biblical idiom: לָתֵת לְאִישׁ כִּדְרָכָיו כִּפְרִי מַעֲלָלָיו, "to give each man according to his ways, the fruit of his deeds" (Jer 17:10). It answers, too, what the wicked merely "worked" (תִּפְעָלוּן, v. 3) with what the righteous actually grow.
The closing phrase אֱלֹקִים שֹׁפְטִים, "God judges," uses a grammatically plural participle, and the commentators split. Radak offers two routes: a plural of majesty (כְּמוֹ אֱלֹקִים קְדוֹשִׁים הוּא, "as in 'He is a holy God'"), or a reference to the angelic court — שׁוֹפְטִים בִּשְׁלִיחוּת הָאֵל, "judges acting on God's mission." Ibn Ezra likewise reads the שֹׁפְטִים as the angels who carry out decrees on earth. But the participle does subtler work too: שֹׁפְטִים, "judges," shares the same judging root as תִּשְׁפְּטוּ, "you judge," back in v. 2. The same root, its referent reversed — the broken council of v. 2 supplanted by the true judgment of v. 12. And בָּאָרֶץ, "on earth," snaps the frame shut: the very ground that hosted the violence of v. 3 is named, in the psalm's last breath, as the place where justice is finally done.
That earthly insistence — *here, not in some deferred heaven* — has echoed down to the present. Faiz Ahmed Faiz wrote "Hum Dekhenge" (هم دیکھیں گے, "We Shall See") in 1979, under Zia-ul-Haq's military dictatorship in Pakistan, and it has since become an anthem of protest across South Asia. Its prophetic refrain envisions the physical collapse of tyranny: جب ظلم و ستم کے کوہِ گراں / روئی کی طرح اُڑ جائیں گے, "when the heavy mountains of cruelty and oppression / will blow away like cotton-wool," ہم محکوموں کے پاؤں تلے / جب دھرتی دھڑ دھڑ دھڑکے گی, "and beneath the feet of us, the oppressed, / when the earth will throb and pound." Faiz, like the psalmist, refuses to defer justice to an afterlife; he insists it will arrive visibly, in the trembling of the actual ground, beneath the feet of the wronged. And he too — the title is *we shall see* — makes the moment of vindication a moment of *seeing*, the verb that has organized this psalm from the stillbirth that never sees to the righteous who behold vengeance.---
