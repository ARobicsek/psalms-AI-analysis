# Commentary on Psalm 59
---
## Introduction
A man is trapped in his own house. Outside, the king's assassins have taken up positions; they will wait through the night and kill him in the morning. This is the situation the superscription hands us, and the verb it uses for what the killers are doing is וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ — "and they watched." Seventeen verses later, the same hunted man is singing: אֲזַמֵּרָה, "I will sing." Between the watching and the singing lies the whole psalm — and, as it happens, the distance between the two words is a single consonant. The root of "watch" is שׁמר (`sh-m-r`); the root of "sing," זמר (`z-m-r`). Keep the skeleton mem–resh, swap the opening sound from *sh* to *z*, and surveillance becomes song. The phonetics confirm that the poet heard the kinship: the first confidence-refrain ends אֶשְׁמֹ֑רָה (`'esh-MŌ-rāh`), the last ends אֲזַמֵּ֑רָה (`'a-zam-MĒ-rāh`) — same cohortative shape, same final -rāh, one letter of difference. Psalm 59 is the record of a man who does not escape his watchers so much as *re-vocalize* them.
The architecture is built for this. After the superscription (v. 1), the psalm falls into two matched halves, each ending in a pair of refrains:
- **vv. 2–6** — a fourfold cry for rescue, a protest of innocence, and an appeal to God as cosmic judge of "all the nations."
- **vv. 7–10** — Refrain A (the enemies "growl like a dog," v. 7), the enemies' boast, God's laughter (v. 9), and Refrain B (the confidence-vow, v. 10).
- **vv. 11–14** — God's faithfulness arrives; a strange, restrained imprecation; the cosmic climax.
- **vv. 15–18** — Refrain A again, verbatim (v. 15), the enemies degraded to starving scavengers (v. 16), morning praise, and Refrain B transformed (v. 18).
The two halves are not repetition but reperception. The same dog-refrain returns; the same confidence-vow returns; but everything that happens *between* them — God's mockery, God's faithfulness, God's judgment — changes what the identical words now mean. The poet's wager is that you cannot hear the second occurrence of anything the way you heard the first.
Watch how this works on the psalm's keyword for raw force, עֹז. It first appears as an enemy attribute: יָג֣וּרוּ עָלַ֣י עַזִּ֑ים, "fierce men plot against me" (v. 4). At the first refrain the word is still in enemy hands — and here the medieval commentators solve a problem modern readers have manufactured. The refrain reads עֻ֭זּוֹ אֵלֶ֣יךָ אֶשְׁמֹ֑רָה, literally "his strength — toward You I watch," and scholars have agonized over the mismatch: why "his" (third person) when God is being addressed as "You"? Rashi answers without strain: עֻזּוֹ is "the strength and might of my enemy, who is stronger than I." Radak and the Metzudat David agree — it is Saul's strength. Read this way, the supposed grammatical wreckage is the point: at the first refrain the fierceness still belongs to the enemy, and the psalmist can only watch toward God for help against it. By v. 17 the word has migrated to God directly — אָשִׁ֣יר עֻזֶּךָ֮, "I will sing *Your* strength" — and by the final verse it has come all the way home: עֻ֝זִּי אֵלֶ֣יךָ אֲזַמֵּ֑רָה, "*my* strength, to You I sing." The enemies' defining attribute is dispossessed, passed through God, and handed to the man they hunted. The change of one suffix, עֻזּוֹ → עֻזִּי, is the change of the whole world.
This is why the final verse is the destination of not one journey but three, all landing at once. Strength arrives home (עֻזִּי). Posture converts from watching to singing (אֶשְׁמֹרָה → אֲזַמֵּרָה). And a third word, חֶסֶד — covenant-faithfulness — surfaces late (vv. 11, 17) and becomes the psalm's last two words: אֱלֹקֵ֥י חַסְדִּֽי, "the God of my faithful love." The first refrain (v. 10) lacked this coda entirely; the second adds it. The expansion is not a longer line filled with decoration — it is the third trajectory cashing out alongside the other two, which is precisely why Refrain B had to grow.
The contested axis underneath all of this is whether anyone *perceives*. The enemies' creed is a single sneering question: כִּי־מִ֥י שֹׁמֵֽעַ, "Who hears?" (v. 8). The psalm's answer is not an argument but a laugh: וְאַתָּ֣ה ה׳ תִּשְׂחַק־לָ֑מוֹ, "But You, O LORD, laugh at them" (v. 9). The verb is a near-quotation of the enthronement psalm, where God responds to nations raging against His anointed: יוֹשֵׁ֣ב בַּשָּׁמַ֣יִם יִשְׂחָ֑ק, "He who sits in heaven laughs" (Ps 2:4). By borrowing that laughter, our psalm quietly upgrades Saul's neighborhood death-squad into the rebellion of "all the nations" — and the local question "who hears?" is answered by the God who hears *and* sees and finds the whole thing absurd.
The most theologically peculiar move comes in the imprecation, and it is the mark of Cain rewritten. One expects an imprecatory psalm to ask for blood; this one asks God *not* to kill: אַל־תַּהַרְגֵ֤ם פֶּֽן־יִשְׁכְּח֬וּ עַמִּ֗י, "Do not kill them, lest my people forget" (v. 12). Instead: הֲנִיעֵ֣מוֹ, "make them wander." The verb is נוע — the very root of the sentence passed on the first murderer, נָ֥ע וָנָ֖ד תִּֽהְיֶ֥ה בָאָֽרֶץ, "a wanderer and fugitive you shall be on the earth" (Gen 4:12). All three Cain elements are here: the killer is spared death, condemned to wandering, and kept alive *as a living sign* so that the lesson is not forgotten. The prayer is then answered in its own vocabulary four verses later — הֵ֭מָּה יְנִיע֣וּן לֶאֱכֹ֑ל, "they wander in search of food" (v. 16). Justice that teaches must remain visible; a corpse instructs no one.
So Psalm 59 is not a string of pious moods. It is a single, tightly engineered conversion, and its governing claim is audacious: the enemy's act is not defeated but transposed. The men who *watch* (שׁמר) the house are not overpowered — they are out-sung, their verb reshaped into the psalmist's *song* (זמר) by the exchange of one letter.
That is the thing to carry away: in this psalm the hunted man's deliverance is spelled out, almost literally, in the difference between *sh* and *z*.

#### Full psalm
Psalm 59 has no place in the fixed daily, Shabbat, or festival liturgy of any major rite. Its unrelenting enemies, its dog-imagery, and its plea to "have no mercy on any treacherous villain" (אַל־תָּחֹ֨ן כׇּל־בֹּ֖גְדֵי אָ֣וֶן, v. 6) make it awkward as communal prayer — one does not easily worship in the register of a man besieged in his bedroom. Its life is instead devotional and occasional. In Hasidic practice (Nusach Sefard) it is the fifth of the ten psalms of Rebbe Nachman of Breslov's *Tikkun HaKlali*, the "General Remedy," where Saul's assassins are read as intrusive impurity and David's survival as spiritual resilience.
#### Key verses
Verses 6 and 17 enter the contemplative anthology *Perek Shirah* — the "song" assigned to creation's creatures — which is printed as an appendix and recited voluntarily, often after the weekday Shacharit in Hasidic communities. There the fierce petition הָקִ֗יצָה לִפְקֹ֥ד כׇּֽל־הַגּוֹיִ֑ם, "bestir Yourself to bring all nations to account," and the trusting hymn וַאֲנִ֤י ׀ אָשִׁ֣יר עֻזֶּךָ֮, "But I will sing of Your strength," are voiced not as David's plea but as a timeless note in the cosmic choir — a placement later commentators found hard to justify.
#### Phrases
The psalm's closing refrain is reworked into a Rosh Hashanah piyyut embedded in the Kedushah of the chazzan's Shacharit Amidah repetition (Nusach Ashkenaz). Conscious of his inadequacy before the Day of Judgment, the prayer-leader pleads: עֻזִּי אֵלֶֽיךָ אֶשְׁמְרָה לְסַעֲדִי … כִּי אַתָּה מִשְׂגַּבִּי אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, "My strength — toward You I watch, for my support … for You are my haven, my faithful God." The splice is telling. The poet takes the possessive עֻזִּי from v. 18 but keeps the verb אֶשְׁמְרָה from v. 10 — the *watching* word, not v. 18's *singing* word — because the chazzan's task is precisely to *guard* his reliance as he prepares to speak. Eight centuries before modern analysis, a liturgist had already felt the two refrains as a single convertible pair and chosen, deliberately, the שׁמר pole.

## Verse-by-Verse Commentary
**Verse 1**
לַמְנַצֵּ֣חַ אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת֮ לְדָוִ֢ד מִ֫כְתָּ֥ם; בִּשְׁלֹ֥חַ שָׁא֑וּל וַֽיִּשְׁמְר֥וּ אֶת־הַ֝בַּ֗יִת לַהֲמִיתֽוֹ׃
Four labels and a story crowd this superscription. אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת, "do not destroy," is most likely a tune-name (it heads Pss 57, 58, 75 as well), but Rashi hears it thematically: "He called the psalm by this name because he was close to dying and to being destroyed, and he begged for mercy." There may be more here than a melody. The phrase reappears in Moses' great intercession — אַל־תַּשְׁחֵ֤ת עַמְּךָ֙, "do not destroy Your people" (Deut 9:26) — and the body of our psalm contains its own negated-destruction plea, אַל־תַּהַרְגֵ֤ם, "do not kill them" (v. 12). Perhaps the restraint David asks for himself is the restraint he will extend to his enemies; the conjecture is unprovable but the rhyme is real.
מִכְתָּם is genuinely opaque. BDB confesses the meaning "unknown"; proposals range from כֶּתֶם, "gold" (a golden poem), to a Syriac root for "inscribe." The Septuagint's στηλογραφία, "stele-inscription," supports the engraving sense — a poem cut in stone to last. Whatever it means, the six michtam psalms (16, 56–60) cluster tightly around David in danger.
The hinge of the whole psalm is buried in the narrative clause: וַֽיִּשְׁמְר֥וּ אֶת־הַ֝בַּ֗יִת, "and they watched the house." This is the root שׁמר in its coldest, most lethal mode — surveillance with intent to kill — and the poem will spend itself turning that verb inside out. The final word, לַהֲמִיתֽוֹ, "to put *him* to death," keeps David in the third person; the speaker has not yet found his own voice. He will, in the next breath. The background is 1 Sam 19:11, where Michal lowers David through a window by night, a detail Rashi and Meiri both supply: "she allowed him to flee at night."
**Verse 2**
הַצִּילֵ֖נִי מֵאֹיְבַ֥י ׀ אֱלֹקָ֑י; מִֽמִּתְקוֹמְמַ֥י תְּשַׂגְּבֵֽנִי׃
Two verbs of rescue, two distinct pictures. הַצִּילֵנִי (root נצל) is the snatch — yanking someone clear of immediate peril. תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי (root שׂגב) is the opposite motion: not pulling out but lifting up, setting a person so high that the danger cannot reach. Radak captures it exactly: "as if You would place me in a high and inaccessible spot that he cannot reach me," and Ibn Ezra adds the personal note — "because I was in a low place." This is the petition-form of the word that will become the psalm's great noun of safety, מִשְׂגָּב, in vv. 10, 17, and 18. The verb here grows into a confession there: ask God to set you high, and you will end up calling Him "my height."
The placement of אֱלֹקָ֑י, "my God," is itself craft. It sits wedged between the two requests — rescue me / my God / lift me — so the address is physically embraced by the petitions surrounding it. The poet's two distinct rescue-roots are not redundant; one is horizontal extraction, the other vertical elevation. The danger is everywhere, so the answer must come from more than one direction.
**Verse 3**
הַ֭צִּילֵנִי מִפֹּ֣עֲלֵי אָ֑וֶן; וּֽמֵאַנְשֵׁ֥י דָ֝מִ֗ים הוֹשִׁיעֵֽנִי׃
The opening verb repeats from v. 2 — הַצִּילֵנִי, "rescue me," a true anaphora driving the urgency — but the second half introduces a third rescue-root: הוֹשִׁיעֵנִי (ישע), the verb of granting victory, room, deliverance in battle. Three roots in two verses (נצל, שׂגב, ישע) sketch a full spectrum of saving.
פֹּ֣עֲלֵי אָ֑וֶן, "workers of iniquity," is a Psalter cliché of the besieged innocent; it stands behind David's plea elsewhere with menacing economy: ס֣וּרוּ מִ֭מֶּנִּי כׇּל־פֹּ֣עֲלֵי אָ֑וֶן, "depart from me, all you workers of iniquity" (Ps 6:9). The word אָוֶן carries a useful double charge — both "wickedness" and "emptiness, futility" — so the plotting is at once evil and doomed.
The sting is in אַנְשֵׁ֥י דָ֝מִ֗ים, "men of bloods." The plural דָּמִים signals not one killing but a habit of bloodguilt. And there is a biographical edge the commentators leave implicit: this is precisely the epithet that will later be hurled at David himself. Shimei curses the fleeing king as אִ֥ישׁ הַדָּמִ֖ים, "man of bloods" (2 Sam 16:7–8). The hunted man here disclaims in his pursuers the very charge his accusers will one day fling back at him. His protest of innocence (next two verses) is not abstract; he knows the accusation by name.
**Verse 4**
כִּ֤י הִנֵּ֪ה אָרְב֡וּ לְנַפְשִׁ֗י; יָג֣וּרוּ עָלַ֣י עַזִּ֑ים; לֹֽא־פִשְׁעִ֖י וְלֹא־חַטָּאתִ֣י ה׳׃
אָרְב֡וּ לְנַפְשִׁ֗י, "they lie in ambush for my life," is military vocabulary applied to a personal hunt; the same trap-world haunts other laments — טָ֘מְנ֤וּ פַ֥ח לִ֑י, "they have hidden a snare for me" (Ps 142:4). Note the paradox: these enemies are both covert ambushers here and loud public boasters in v. 8. They plot in silence and brag in the street.
The verb יָג֣וּרוּ is ambiguous. Ibn Ezra and Radak gloss it "they gather, assemble" (יתקבצו); Rashi reads it from a different sense — "they lodge against me," camping outside to keep watch. Either way, the actors are עַזִּ֑ים, "fierce ones," and this adjective seeds the עז-root that the psalm will steadily reclaim from the enemy. Malbim sharpens the injustice: their fierceness, not any guilt of David's, is the whole cause — "fierce men ambushing against all justice."
The protest of innocence deploys two of the three classic sin-terms — פֶּשַׁע (willful rebellion) and חַטָּאת (missing the mark) — under emphatic negation, completing the triad with עָוֺן in the next verse. This exhaustive legal self-defense, covering the full moral vocabulary, is the posture of the innocent sufferer; Job protests his case the same way.
**Verse 5**
בְּֽלִי־עָ֭וֺן יְרֻצ֣וּן וְיִכּוֹנָ֑נוּ; ע֖וּרָה לִקְרָאתִ֣י וּרְאֵֽה׃
The third sin-term lands: בְּלִי־עָ֭וֺן, "with no guilt." Three terms, repeated negation, one comprehensive plea of innocence stretched across vv. 4–5.
Then the verse lurches. The first half watches the enemies in the third person — יְרֻצ֣וּן וְיִכּוֹנָ֑נוּ, "they run and array themselves" — and the second half wheels around to seize God by the second person: ע֖וּרָה לִקְרָאתִ֣י, "rouse Yourself to meet me!" The grammatical whiplash dramatizes panic; the speaker cannot stay in calm description while the killers are running. There is a quiet boldness in the imperative ע֖וּרָה, which implies God is, at the moment, asleep on the job. Ibn Ezra reads the cry as a direct answer to the superscription's verb: "rouse Yourself" responds to "they watched the house" — divine wakefulness against human surveillance.
The verb וְיִכּוֹנָ֑נוּ (root כון) is pointed. כון connotes firm establishment, the stability God grants the righteous — and here the enemies arrogate it to themselves, "establishing" their battle-line. The poet lets them borrow the language of permanence; the rest of the psalm will repossess it.
**Verse 6**
וְאַתָּ֤ה ה׳־אֱלֹקִ֥ים ׀ צְבָק֡וֹת אֱלֹ֘קֵ֤י יִשְׂרָאֵ֗ל; הָקִ֗יצָה לִפְקֹ֥ד כׇּֽל־הַגּוֹיִ֑ם; אַל־תָּחֹ֨ן כׇּל־בֹּ֖גְדֵי אָ֣וֶן סֶֽלָה׃
The first וְאַתָּה pivot, swinging from enemy-complaint to God, comes loaded with the most maximal title in the psalm: four divine designations stacked without normal construct syntax — YHWH, Elohim, Tzevaot, God of Israel. The accumulation is calibrated to the moment, because the scale has just exploded: from one man's ambushers (v. 4) to כׇּֽל־הַגּוֹיִ֑ם, "all the nations." There is an internal irony nobody can miss: the psalmist invokes the God of Israel's *armies* (צְבָקוֹת) against the army of Israel's own king. Malbim builds a whole reading on this — David, until now Saul's commander, formally hands his troops back to God: "You will now be the commander of Israel's host."
The verb לִפְקֹ֥ד (root פקד) is famously two-faced: it can mean a gracious "visiting" (God "visited" Sarah, Gen 21:1) or a punitive "calling to account." The parallel אַל־תָּחֹ֨ן, "have no mercy," fixes the sense here as judicial. בֹּגְדֵי אָוֶן, "treacherous workers of iniquity," uses בגד, the verb of covenant-betrayal — these are not merely opponents but traitors to the bond. The first Selah falls exactly where the lens has zoomed to its widest: cosmic title, cosmic mandate, musical pause.
**Verse 7**
יָשׁ֣וּבוּ לָ֭עֶרֶב יֶהֱמ֥וּ כַכָּ֗לֶב; וִיס֥וֹבְבוּ עִֽיר׃
The refrain *sounds* like its subject. Three verbs end in the same droning vowel — yā-**SHŪ**-vū … ye-he-**MŪ** … wiy-**SŌ**-və-vū — a string of long *u*'s that mimics the rise and fall of a dog's howl, thickened by the recurring *b*-consonants of כַכָּ֗לֶב and וִיסוֹבְבוּ. You hear the pack before you parse the line.
Why dogs, though, and not lions or wolves? The choice is deliberate. Lion-imagery confers terror *with* dignity — פֶּן־יִטְרֹ֣ף כְּאַרְיֵ֣ה נַפְשִׁ֑י, "lest he tear my soul like a lion" (Ps 7:3) — and wolves carry ravenous prowess, even military glory: בִּנְיָמִין֙ זְאֵ֣ב יִטְרָ֔ף, "Benjamin is a ravenous wolf" (Gen 49:27). The ancient dog was neither. It was a scavenger, unclean, nocturnal, shameless — and so it gives the poet exactly what he wants: menace without honor. To call your hunters dogs is to fear them and despise them in the same breath. Ibn Ezra domesticates the picture nicely: like dogs that bark at night to guard the town, "so are these who guard the house."
A textual fork opens here. The Septuagint renders יֶהֱמ֥וּ, "they growl," as λιμώσσω, "they hunger" — importing the starvation theme that the Masoretic text reserves for v. 16. The harmonization comes at a cost. In Hebrew the verb stays identical in v. 7 and v. 15, and that fixity is the whole device: the *same* growl sounds threatening the first time and pathetic the second, because the verses between them have broken the enemy. The Greek, by softening v. 7 toward hunger, flattens the very arc the repetition was built to carry.
Baudelaire, in the prose-poem "Les Bons Chiens" (1869, near the end of his life), took the same despised street-dog and made it a hero: *Je chante le chien crotté, le chien pauvre, le chien sans domicile, le chien flâneur* — "I sing the dirty dog, the poor dog, the homeless dog, the strolling dog." His anaphoric catalogue elevates the scavenger into an emblem of the cunning marginal survivor. The psalmist uses the identical creature for the opposite end: to strip his enemies of stature. Same animal, opposite verdict — and the comparison throws into relief just how loaded the choice of *dog* was for an ancient ear.
**Verse 8**
הִנֵּ֤ה ׀ יַבִּ֘יע֤וּן בְּפִיהֶ֗ם חֲ֭רָבוֹת בְּשִׂפְתֽוֹתֵיהֶ֑ם; כִּי־מִ֥י שֹׁמֵֽעַ׃
The verb יַבִּ֘יע֤וּן (root נבע) ordinarily belongs to water — springs that bubble and gush. Klein notes its primary use of welling fountains. Applied to speech it produces an uncontrolled torrent — and what gushes out is חֲרָבוֹת, "swords." A fountain of blades: the image is deliberately grotesque, fusing the soft overflow of water with edged metal. This psalm treats speech not as a prelude to violence but as the violence itself, which places it inside a Psalter-wide arsenal of weaponized mouths — most closely its michtam-cousin: שִׁנֵּיהֶ֥ם חֲנִ֣ית וְחִצִּ֑ים וּ֝לְשׁוֹנָ֗ם חֶ֣רֶב חַדָּֽה, "their teeth are spears and arrows, their tongue a sharp sword" (Ps 57:5).
The closing taunt is the enemies' creed, three words long: כִּי־מִ֥י שֹׁמֵֽעַ, "for who hears?" This is the standard formula of practical atheism — God is absent, inattentive, blind — and the Psalter records its variants with contempt (Ps 10:11; 94:7). Malbim catalogues why these men are *worse* than the dogs they resemble: "dogs only bark, they do not speak; these gush words. Dogs bite but do not kill with a sword; these have swords on their lips." The whole verse exists to be demolished by the next.
Sylvia Plath's late poem "Words" (written days before her death in 1963, published posthumously in *Ariel*) caught the lie inside "who hears?" exactly. She makes speech all impact and aftermath: *Axes / After whose stroke the wood rings, / And the echoes! / Echoes traveling / Off from the center like horses.* The enemies imagine malicious words evaporate into the night air, unheard and unaccountable. Plath insists on the opposite physics — that an utterance, once released, keeps traveling outward beyond any speaker's control, ringing long after the blow. Her axes are the literalization of the psalm's swords-on-lips, and her ringing echoes are the answer to "who hears?": everyone, eventually, and the sound does not stop.
**Verse 9**
וְאַתָּ֣ה ה׳ תִּשְׂחַק־לָ֑מוֹ; תִּ֝לְעַ֗ג לְכׇל־גּוֹיִֽם׃
The second וְאַתָּה pivot answers "who hears?" not with a lecture but with laughter: תִּשְׂחַק־לָ֑מוֹ, "You laugh at them." The line is a near-citation of the enthronement psalm, where God meets nations conspiring against His anointed with the same response: יוֹשֵׁ֣ב בַּשָּׁמַ֣יִם יִשְׂחָ֑ק אֲדֹנָ֗י יִלְעַג־לָֽמוֹ, "He who sits in heaven laughs; the Lord mocks them" (Ps 2:4). The match is not just the root שׂחק but the archaic suffix -מוֹ in לָמוֹ — the same rare form in the same derisive slot. Meiri makes the connection explicit: "as it says, *the LORD mocks them* (Ps 2)." By quoting the royal psalm, our poet reclassifies Saul's neighborhood thugs as the raging nations of cosmic rebellion — and tells you the stakes are larger than one besieged house.
The two divine verbs escalate. שׂחק is the laugh; תִּ֝לְעַ֗ג (לעג) is the active sneer, the open derision. The figurative force is not amusement but contempt that reduces a threat to absurdity — the same judgment Jeremiah pronounces on Moab: וְהָיָ֥ה מוֹאָ֛ב לִשְׂחֹ֖ק, "Moab shall become a laughingstock" (Jer 48:39).
Milton heard this verse and slowed it to a celestial calm. In *Paradise Lost* (1667), the Father addresses the Son on the eve of the angelic war: *Mighty Father, thou thy foes / Justly hast in derision, and secure / Laugh'st at thir vain designs and tumults vain* (V.735–37). Milton's enjambment delays the laughter across three lines until it arrives as something settled and inevitable, the serenity of a power that was never in doubt. The psalmist's laughter is riskier. He must summon it from inside the trap, with the killers still posted outside the window — divine derision invoked, not observed, by a man whose own survival is the night's open question. Milton laughs from heaven; David asks heaven to laugh on his behalf.
**Verse 10**
עֻ֭זּוֹ אֵלֶ֣יךָ אֶשְׁמֹ֑רָה; כִּֽי־אֱ֝לֹקִ֗ים מִשְׂגַּבִּֽי׃
The first confidence-refrain hides the psalm's most argued-over crux: עֻ֭זּוֹ אֵלֶ֣יךָ אֶשְׁמֹ֑רָה — "*his* strength, toward You I watch." Why "his"? Modern editors have wanted to emend it to עֻזִּי, "my strength," to match v. 18. The classical commentators show why that would wreck the poem. Rashi: "His strength — the strength and might of my enemy, who is stronger than I; toward You I watch and wait for rescue from him." Radak names the enemy: "the strength of Saul, who is mightier than I." On this reading the supposed grammar problem is the engine of the psalm: at the *first* refrain the fierce strength still belongs to the enemy, and all the psalmist can do is keep his eyes fixed on God. The migration to עֻזִּי, "*my* strength," is the work of the next seventeen verses; emend it away and you erase the journey.
And the verb is the turned word. אֶשְׁמֹ֑רָה is the root שׁמר — Saul's men "watched" (וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ) the house to kill; now the hunted man "watches" toward God. The same action, the same root, opposite vectors: lethal surveillance becomes faithful vigil. Direction transforms everything. The closing confession, כִּֽי־אֱלֹקִ֗ים מִשְׂגַּבִּֽי, "for God is my haven," brings home the noun from the v. 2 petition — the verb "lift me up" has matured into "He *is* my height."
The vigil here is confident, grounded in a refuge already proven. Beckett, in *En attendant Godot* (1953), staged the same posture stripped of its ground. Vladimir and Estragon wait for a deliverer who never comes: VLADIMIR: *Nous nous pendrons demain. (Un temps.) À moins que Godot ne vienne.* ESTRAGON: *Et s'il vient?* VLADIMIR: *Nous serons sauvés* — "We'll hang ourselves tomorrow. Unless Godot comes." "And if he comes?" "We'll be saved." Beckett retains the exact architecture of redemptive waiting — the watching, the conditional salvation — and removes the certainty, leaving expectation suspended over despair. The psalmist's אֶשְׁמֹרָה is everything Beckett's wait is not: it watches *because* God is already a fortress, and so it can become, eight verses later, a song.
**Verse 11**
אֱלֹקֵ֣י (חסדו) [חַסְדִּ֣י] יְקַדְּמֵ֑נִי; אֱ֝לֹקִ֗ים יַרְאֵ֥נִי בְשֹׁרְרָֽי׃
A Kethib/Qere wobble sits in the opening phrase: the written text (Kethib) has חַסְדּוֹ, "*his* faithful love"; the read tradition (Qere) has חַסְדִּי, "*my* faithful love." The Septuagint backs the Kethib (ὁ ἔλεος αὐτοῦ). Radak explains both calmly: "the written form refers to the One who does the kindness — God; the read form refers to its recipient — David." Each reading is theologically intact: God acts either from His own intrinsic character or from a covenant the psalmist can claim as his own. The psalm casts its own vote at the very end. The closing phrase, אֱלֹקֵ֥י חַסְדִּֽי (v. 18), is unambiguously in the "my" form — and it forms a bracket around the whole second half, an inclusio that argues for the Qere. There is even a cluster-level clue: the michtam-cousin Ps 57 reads יִשְׁלַ֥ח אֱ֝לֹקִ֗ים חַסְדּ֥וֹ, "God will send *His* faithful love" (57:4) — the same inherited phrase in the "his" form. Psalm 59 ends by personalizing what the neighboring psalm left as God's own attribute.
The verb is the verse's hidden jewel: יְקַדְּמֵ֑נִי, "He will go before me." This is military-vanguard imagery — faithfulness as an advance guard that arrives at the danger *first*. The same picture personifies the covenant-virtues as God's heralds: חֶ֥סֶד וֶ֝אֱמֶ֗ת יְקַדְּמ֥וּ פָנֶֽיךָ, "faithful love and truth go before Your face" (Ps 89:15). Against enemies who *encircle* (vv. 7, 15), the psalm answers with a God who is height above (מִשְׂגָּב), shield between (מָגֵן, v. 12), and now vanguard ahead — protection from more than one direction.
The final word, בְשֹׁרְרָֽי, "over my watchful foes," may derive from שׁוּר, "to spy, watch" — which would add another watching-word to a psalm already saturated with the larger theme of perception (וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ v. 1, שֹׁמֵעַ v. 8, אֶשְׁמֹרָה v. 10). In a poem where being-watched is the crisis, the ambiguity between "enemies" and "watchers" is fitting: here, watching simply *is* the enmity.
**Verse 12**
אַל־תַּהַרְגֵ֤ם ׀ פֶּֽן־יִשְׁכְּח֬וּ עַמִּ֗י; הֲנִיעֵ֣מוֹ בְ֭חֵילְךָ וְהוֹרִידֵ֑מוֹ; מָ֖גִנֵּ֣נוּ אֲדֹנָֽי׃
An imprecatory psalm that asks God *not* to kill is a strange creature. אַל־תַּהַרְגֵ֤ם, "do not kill them" — followed by the reason, פֶּֽן־יִשְׁכְּח֬וּ עַמִּ֗י, "lest my people forget." Rashi states the logic plainly: "this is not a recognizable revenge … all the dead are forgotten. Instead, deprive them of their possessions, so they become poor — a revenge remembered for length of days." Calvin, reading the same line, calls it pedagogical: a slow, visible ruin instructs where summary execution erases the lesson.
The chosen punishment is the mark of Cain rewritten. הֲנִיעֵ֣מוֹ, "make them wander," is the root נוע — the very sentence on the first murderer, נָ֥ע וָנָ֖ד תִּֽהְיֶ֥ה בָאָֽרֶץ, "a wanderer and fugitive you shall be" (Gen 4:12). All three Cain elements line up: the killer is spared death, condemned to wander, and kept alive *as a living sign*, precisely so the lesson is not forgotten. This also dissolves the apparent contradiction with v. 14's "consume them utterly": the sequence is Cain first, final judgment last — wandering witness, then erasure. Ibn Ezra ties the wandering directly to the dogs: they will "wander to seek bread, as he said of the dogs," anticipating v. 16.
Then a single word cracks the psalm open. מָ֖גִנֵּ֣נוּ, "*our* shield" — the only first-person plural in eighteen verses of solitary lament. Suddenly a community is present. Ibn Ezra hears a royal hint: "our shield — that is, our king, truly," reading the plural as the people whose king David is. The lone hunted man has been representative all along; the "we" simply surfaces here. It is followed by אֲדֹנָֽי, a divine name otherwise absent from the psalm.
Townes Van Zandt's "Pancho and Lefty" (1972) offers a folk version of this exact preference for slow diminishment over clean death: *Pancho needs your prayers it's true, / But save a few for Lefty too. / He only did what he had to do, / And now he's growing old.* The betrayer's sentence is not execution but the long burden of survival, aging into the memory of his own deed — which is precisely the "lest my people forget" calculus, that justice does its teaching only when the guilty remain in view.
**Verse 13**
חַטַּאת־פִּ֗ימוֹ דְּֽבַר־שְׂפָ֫תֵ֥ימוֹ; וְיִלָּכְד֥וּ בִגְאוֹנָ֑ם; וּמֵאָלָ֖ה וּמִכַּ֣חַשׁ יְסַפֵּֽרוּ׃
The mouths and lips return, and the reversal is architectural. In v. 8 swords *gushed* from בְּפִיהֶ֗ם … בְּשִׂפְתֽוֹתֵיהֶ֑ם, "their mouths … their lips"; here the identical organs — חַטַּאת־פִּ֗ימוֹ דְּֽבַר־שְׂפָ֫תֵ֥ימוֹ, "the sin of their mouth, the word of their lips" — become the evidence that condemns them. The weapon is now the indictment; the swords have boomeranged.
The trap is self-sprung: וְיִלָּכְד֥וּ בִגְאוֹנָ֑ם, "let them be caught in their pride." Not an external snare but their own arrogance — the swagger of "who hears?" (v. 8) — closing on them. This is a wisdom commonplace: וּבְהַוַּ֖ת בֹּגְדִ֣ים יִלָּכֵֽדוּ, "the treacherous are trapped by their own malice" (Prov 11:6), which even shares our psalm's word for the treacherous, בֹּגְדִים.
Heard alongside the motion-verbs, this verse completes a slow kinetic collapse. The enemies began as confident besiegers — וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר, "they encircle the city" (vv. 7, 15) — were sentenced to aimless wandering (הֲנִיעֵמוֹ, v. 12), and are now arrested entirely (וְיִלָּכְדוּ). Circle, stagger, capture: their movement degrades step by step.
The final clause, וּמֵאָלָ֖ה וּמִכַּ֣חַשׁ יְסַפֵּֽרוּ, "and by the curse and the lies they tell," names their two verbal crimes — the false oath (אָלָה) and the lie (כַּחַשׁ). The verb יְסַפֵּרוּ is deliberately suspended between "they recount" (their crimes catalogued) and the destructive sense the Septuagint reads (διαγγελήσονται, proclaimed *to destruction*). Malbim turns the ambiguity into theater: the enemies will themselves confess — "they will testify with their own lips that they were punished for the sin of their mouth." The instrument of attack becomes the instrument of self-conviction.
**Verse 14**
כַּלֵּ֥ה בְחֵמָה֮ כַּלֵּ֢ה וְֽאֵ֫ינֵ֥מוֹ; וְֽיֵדְע֗וּ כִּֽי־אֱ֭לֹקִים מֹשֵׁ֣ל בְּֽיַעֲקֹ֑ב לְאַפְסֵ֖י הָאָ֣רֶץ סֶֽלָה׃
The doubled imperative — כַּלֵּ֥ה … כַּלֵּ֢ה, "make an end … make an end" — bracketing בְחֵמָה֮, "in fury," is the psalm's hottest moment, an anaphora at the climax matching the desperate הַצִּילֵנִי / הַצִּילֵנִי that opened it (vv. 2–3). And it seems to contradict v. 12's "do not kill." The commentators read the two as sequential, not contradictory. Ibn Ezra: "*Make an end* — little by little, and at last *they are no more*." Radak agrees: "consume them gradually, and in the end none survive." The Cain-stage of visible wandering serves its pedagogical purpose first; total erasure comes after the lesson has been taught.
Then the entire ferocity bends toward a single purpose-clause: וְֽיֵדְע֗וּ כִּֽי־אֱ֭לֹקִים מֹשֵׁ֣ל בְּֽיַעֲקֹ֑ב, "that they may know God rules in Jacob." The preposition is precise — *in* Jacob, not merely *over* him; an immanent rule within the people. The participle מֹשֵׁל marks ongoing, present sovereignty. The psalm's geographic arc reaches its furthest point: from David's house (v. 1) to the city (v. 7) to all nations (v. 6) to לְאַפְסֵ֖י הָאָ֣רֶץ, "the ends of the earth." A bedroom siege has become a demonstration meant for the whole inhabited world; the second Selah marks the expansion, mirroring the first.
Frederick Douglass, in "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" (1852), adopts exactly this register — annihilating force as the only thing that can reveal truth to the ends of a nation: *"For it is not light that is needed, but fire; it is not the gentle shower, but thunder. We need the storm, the whirlwind, and the earthquake."* The psalmist asks for consuming wrath because gradual gentleness cannot make divine rule *known* universally; Douglass rejects gentleness for the same reason, insisting that entrenched injustice yields only to catastrophic moral pressure. Both understand that some truths require a verdict large enough to be seen from a distance.
**Verse 15**
וְיָשֻׁ֣בוּ לָ֭עֶרֶב יֶהֱמ֥וּ כַכָּ֗לֶב; וִיס֥וֹבְבוּ עִֽיר׃
Word for word the dog-refrain returns (only the connective וְ and a vowel shift distinguish it from v. 7) — and the verbatim repetition *is* the argument. Between the two occurrences God has laughed (v. 9), been named sovereign over all nations (v. 14), and pronounced judgment (vv. 12–13). So the identical growl now lands differently: the same dogs return into a universe that has, in the interim, been declared God-ruled. Their circling is unchanged; its threat is already void. Malbim catches the shift before v. 16 even spells it out: "now, after their downfall, they return at evening and howl like a dog again — but not as before, to kill a righteous man's soul; rather, they wander to eat." The refrain's own circularity enacts the dogs' futile circuit: it comes back around just as they do, to no effect.
**Verse 16**
הֵ֭מָּה (ינועון) [יְנִיע֣וּן] לֶאֱכֹ֑ל; אִם־לֹ֥א יִ֝שְׂבְּע֗וּ וַיָּלִֽינוּ׃
The petition of v. 12 is now answered in its own vocabulary: הֵ֭מָּה יְנִיע֣וּן לֶאֱכֹ֑ל, "they wander in search of food" — root נוע, the Cain-wandering David had prayed for. Prayer becomes description; the imprecation has been granted on stage. The confident encirclers of v. 7 are now reduced to scavenging strays.
The degradation is also carried by the contrast of verbs. In v. 7 the dogs יֶהֱמ֥וּ, "growl/roar" — an aggressive, throaty noise. Here they end in וַיָּלִֽינוּ. The verb is itself contested: it may mean "to lodge overnight" (root לין) — they spend the night unsatisfied — or "to grumble, murmur" (root לון). The Septuagint chose γογγύσουσιν, "they will grumble," and the choice generates a delicious irony, because γογγύζω is the exact verb for Israel's complaining in the wilderness — וַיִּלֹּ֤נוּ … עַל־מֹשֶׁה֙, "and they murmured against Moses" (Exod 16:2). Read this way, David's persecutors are reduced to the posture of the faithless desert generation: hungry, dissatisfied, grumbling in the dark. The conditional אִם־לֹ֥א יִ֝שְׂבְּע֗וּ, "if they are not satisfied," concedes that their hunt will fail — a deliberate foil to the next verse, where the once-hunted man sings *un*conditionally. The same insatiability marks the dogs of Isaiah: וְהַכְּלָבִ֣ים עַזֵּי־נֶ֔פֶשׁ לֹ֥א יָדְע֖וּ שָׂבְעָ֑ה, "the dogs are greedy, they never know satiety" (Isa 56:11).
Manuel Bandeira's "O Bicho" (1947) takes the dog-as-degradation image and detonates it. The Brazilian poet watches a creature scavenging in filth: *Vi ontem um bicho / Na imundície do pátio / Catando comida entre os detritos.* Then the gut-punch ending: *O bicho não era um cão, / Não era um gato, / Não era um rato. / O bicho, meu Deus, era um homem* — "The animal was not a dog, not a cat, not a rat. The animal, my God, was a man." Bandeira reverses the psalm's vector exactly. Where the poet weaponizes the hungry scavenger to *degrade* his enemies into dog-like need, Bandeira's scavenger turns out to be human, and the animalizing language becomes an indictment of the society that starved him. Set beside Bandeira, v. 16's cruelty is plain — and so is the gap between an ancient curse upon enemies and a modern lament for the wretched.
**Verse 17**
וַאֲנִ֤י ׀ אָשִׁ֣יר עֻזֶּךָ֮ וַאֲרַנֵּ֥ן לַבֹּ֗קֶר חַ֫סְדֶּ֥ךָ; כִּֽי־הָיִ֣יתָ מִשְׂגָּ֣ב לִ֑י; וּ֝מָנ֗וֹס בְּי֣וֹם צַר־לִֽי׃
The emphatic וַאֲנִ֤י, "but *I*," is syntactically unnecessary — Hebrew verbs already encode the subject — so its presence is pure rhetorical force, the pronoun thrown down against the whining dogs of v. 16. *They* grumble unsatisfied; *I* sing. And the word עֻזֶּךָ֮, "*Your* strength," advances the עז-progression one decisive step: the fierce attribute that was the enemy's (v. 4) and still the enemy's in v. 10 ("his") is now God's strength addressed directly — one rung from full possession.
The temporal word is the theological one: וַאֲרַנֵּ֥ן לַבֹּ֗קֶר, "I will exult at *morning*." The dogs belong to evening (לָעֶרֶב, vv. 7, 15); the psalmist belongs to morning, the hour of covenant renewal and rescue — בָּעֶ֮רֶב יָלִ֥ין בֶּ֑כִי וְלַבֹּ֥קֶר רִנָּֽה, "weeping may lodge for the evening, but at morning, a shout of joy" (Ps 30:6), where the very root רנן recurs.
Most striking is the tense: כִּֽי־הָיִ֣יתָ מִשְׂגָּ֣ב לִ֑י, "for You *have been* my fortress." Past tense — while the dogs still prowl in vv. 15–16. The deliverance is spoken as accomplished before it is fully visible: faith declaring the verdict already rendered. The closing image וּמָנ֗וֹס, "a refuge," literally a place to flee to, Metzudat David glosses with quiet tenderness — "like a man who flees to his friend to be saved."
Solomon Ibn Gabirol's eleventh-century piyyut *Shaḥar Avakeshkha* builds itself directly on this verse's vocabulary. The great Andalusian poet-philosopher opens: שַׁחַר אֲבַקֶּשְׁךָ, צוּרִי וּמִשְׂגַּבִּי, אֶעֱרֹךְ לְפָנֶיךָ שַׁחְרִי וְגַם עַרְבִּי — "At dawn I seek You, my Rock and my haven; I set before You my morning and also my evening." Ibn Gabirol lifts the psalm's מִשְׂגַּבִּי, "my haven," and its morning-praise and turns them into a *repeatable* discipline. The psalm's morning song rises out of one night of mortal danger barely survived; Ibn Gabirol converts that emergency gratitude into a daily liturgical posture, seeking God at dawn not because the assassins are gone but as the standing shape of a devout life. The psalmist sings at morning because he made it through the night; the payyetan sings every morning on principle.
**Verse 18**
עֻ֭זִּי אֵלֶ֣יךָ אֲזַמֵּ֑רָה; כִּֽי־אֱלֹקִ֥ים מִ֝שְׂגַּבִּ֗י אֱלֹקֵ֥י חַסְדִּֽי׃
Everything arrives here. Three separate journeys reach their terminus in this single verse, which is why the refrain has grown longer than its v. 10 twin. First, strength comes fully home: עֻ֭זִּי, "*my* strength" — the last rung of the ladder עַזִּים (enemy, v. 4) → עֻזּוֹ (his, v. 10) → עֻזֶּךָ (Yours, v. 17) → עֻזִּי (mine). The minimal change of suffix, וֹ → ִי, is the psalm's maximal claim: the fierceness that hunted David is now his own, received through God. Second, the posture converts. אֲזַמֵּ֑רָה, "I will sing," replaces v. 10's אֶשְׁמֹ֑רָה, "I will watch" — same cohortative form, same closing -rāh, but the root has shifted from שׁמר to זמר. Keep the mem and resh, swap the *sh* for a *z*, and surveillance has become song; the watcher's verb has been re-vocalized into the singer's. Third, a word absent from the first refrain appears as the psalm's final two: אֱלֹקֵ֥י חַסְדִּֽי, "the God of my faithful love." חֶסֶד, introduced only in vv. 11 and 17, lands last — and lands personalized.
That ending also casts the deciding vote in the v. 11 textual dispute. Where v. 11 hovered between חַסְדּוֹ ("his") and חַסְדִּי ("my"), the final words are unambiguously חַסְדִּי, and they close an inclusio around the whole second half (vv. 11–18). The psalm that began with God's strength still in enemy hands ends with God's faithfulness claimed in the first person — and it ends not on עֹז, raw power, but on חֶסֶד, covenant-love. The deepest security the psalmist names is not the fortress but the relationship.
Rashi, glossing the refrain, reaches for another verse that fuses these very words: "to You, who are *my strength and my stronghold* (Jer 16:19), to You I will sing." Ibn Ezra hears behind עֻזִּי … אֲזַמֵּרָה the ancient victory-song of the Sea — עׇזִּ֤י וְזִמְרָת֙ יָ֔הּ, "my strength and my song is the LORD" (Exod 15:2) — which itself binds the two roots, עֹז and זמר, that this psalm has been driving apart and back together for eighteen verses. The hunted man ends where Israel ended after the Sea: alive, on the far shore, singing.
The one thing to carry away is this: David's whole deliverance is spelled out in the distance between two consonants. The men who *watched* (שׁמר) his house are not overpowered — they are out-sung, their verb reshaped into his *song* (זמר) by the exchange of a single letter.---
