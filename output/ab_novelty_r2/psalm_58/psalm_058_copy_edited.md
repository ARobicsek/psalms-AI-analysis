# Commentary on Psalm 58
---
## Introduction
A psalm that opens with the heading אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת, "do not destroy," then turns to spend four verses begging God to destroy — to smash teeth, dissolve snails, abort the wicked before they see daylight, and whirl them off in a storm of fury — has a problem on its hands, or seems to. One does not easily reconcile a title that forbids ruin with a body that pleads for it in seven escalating images. The temptation is to wave it away: אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת is just a tune-name, borrowed melody-tags shared with Psalms 57, 59, and 75, no more meaningful than "to the tune of Greensleeves."
But the Jewish commentators heard something else. Every one of them — Rashi, Radak, Meiri, Metzudat David — anchors this psalm to a single scene: David in the night-camp, standing over the sleeping Saul with the king's own spear in reach, and refusing. The words he spoke there were אַל־תַּשְׁחִיתֵהוּ, "do not destroy him" (1 Sam 26:9) — the very root, the very negated command, that now heads the poem. Read against that scene, the paradox dissolves into a thesis. David, who could have ended his persecutor by hand, will not; instead he hands the destruction *upward*. The superscription and the body say the same thing from opposite directions: do not destroy by your own hand — pray that God destroy. This is a psalm about who is permitted to wield violence, and the answer is never the petitioner.
That frame matters, because Psalm 58 is not a personal lament. The psalmist is not a victim crying for rescue; he is a prosecutor. The architecture is a courtroom:
- **vv. 2–3** — Indictment. Corrupt judges (אֵלֶם) interrogated to their faces.
- **vv. 4–6** — Evidence. The wicked as defective from birth, and as a deaf, uncharmable viper.
- **vv. 7–10** — Sentence. A sevenfold imprecation of dissolution.
- **vv. 11–12** — Verdict's aftermath. The righteous sees; humanity confesses that God judges בָּאָרֶץ, "on earth."
The earth frames the whole. In v. 3 the wicked "deal out violence בָּאָרֶץ"; in v. 12 "there is a God judging בָּאָרֶץ." The same patch of ground that was the scene of the crime becomes the scene of the sentence. That is the theodicy in miniature.
But the deeper coherence of this poem is anatomical. Psalm 58 prosecutes its case organ by organ, tracking justice through the body's instruments of speech, hearing, and sight. Watch the **mouth** first. The judges *should* speak צֶדֶק, "what is right" (v. 2, תְּדַבֵּרוּן); instead they are דֹּבְרֵי כָזָב, "speakers of lies" (v. 4); and what their mouths actually carry is venom, חֲמַת נָחָשׁ, "the venom of a snake" (v. 5). Then the **ear**. The viper יַאְטֵם אָזְנוֹ, "stops up its ear" (v. 5) — and the verb אטם is active. This snake does not fail to hear; it *refuses* to, "so as not to hear the voice of charmers" (v. 6). And here is the hinge of the whole poem: because the ear is sealed, persuasion is over. No reparative word can reach a creature that has bolted its own hearing shut.
Which is exactly why God's *first* blow, when the prayer turns upward in v. 7, lands not on the hands of v. 3 — though those hands "deal out violence" — but on the **mouth**: הֲרׇס־שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ, "smash their teeth in their mouth." The oddly redundant "in their mouth" (where else would teeth be?) is the point. The organ that produced venomous speech, and that cannot be corrected because the matching ear is deaf, must be destroyed directly. You do not negotiate with a sealed ear; you disarm the mouth beside it.
The verdict is then settled in the **eye**. The rare verb here is חזה — the prophet's word for *seeing*, the seeing of Amos and Micah and Habakkuk, not ordinary רָאָה. In v. 9 the stillborn wicked בַּל־חָזוּ שָׁמֶשׁ, "never see the sun"; in v. 11 the righteous כִּי־חָזָה נָקָם, "when he sees vindication." Same verb, opposite poles: the wicked are denied the light they corrupted; the righteous are granted to witness justice as a kind of revelation. And the poem closes by restoring the organ it opened with — the **mouth**. After the corrupt speakers are silenced, וְיֹאמַר אָדָם, "humanity will say" the truth (v. 12). The mighty ones who were supposed to *speak* justice and could not are gone; ordinary humankind is given back honest speech.
So the sequence of the imprecation is not arbitrary rage. It follows the logic of a closed system. Speech is corrupt, hearing is sealed against repair, therefore sight and speech must be re-tuned by force — and the agent of that re-tuning is never the human petitioner.
One detail seals the case. The verb the corrupt judges use in v. 3 is תְּפַלֵּסוּן — from פֶּלֶס, the balance-scale. They "weigh out" violence with the meticulous instrument of justice itself, calibrating cruelty as if measuring grain. But a proverb tells us precisely whose instrument that is: פֶּלֶס וּמֹאזְנֵי מִשְׁפָּט לַה׳, "a just balance and scales belong to the LORD" (Prov 16:11). The judges have not merely been unfair; they have stolen God's own scale. And the psalm's last word — אֱלֹקִים שֹׁפְטִים בָּאָרֶץ, "God judges on earth" — is the scale returned to its owner.
Here, then, is the one thing to carry away: in Psalm 58, justice is not an abstraction handed down from heaven but the literal *re-tuning of the body's organs* — mouth, ear, eye — back to honest function, which is why a poem so drenched in violence can end, without contradiction, in the quiet sound of human beings finally telling the truth.

#### Full psalm
Psalm 58 is essentially absent from statutory Jewish prayer. It is not part of the weekday, Shabbat, or festival liturgy in any of the major rites — Ashkenaz, the Hasidic Nusach Sefard, or the Edot HaMizrach. Its second-person prosecution of corrupt authorities and its catalogue of imprecations make it ill-suited to communal worship; one does not comfortably daven in the cadence of "do you really decree what is just?"

## Verse-by-Verse Commentary
**Verse 9 — the snail's song in Perek Shirah.** The single most stable liturgical home for any part of this psalm is unexpected. In *Perek Shirah*, the non-obligatory devotional anthology (often printed at the back of siddurim, including in the Hasidic Nusach Sefard, and frequently said privately after Shacharit), each creature "sings" a verse of Scripture. The snail's assigned song is our v. 9: שַׁבְּלוּל אוֹמֵר כְּמוֹ שַׁבְּלוּל תֶּמֶס יַהֲלֹךְ נֵפֶל אֵשֶׁת בַּל חָזוּ שָׁמֶשׁ, "the snail says: 'like a snail that melts as it goes…'" Interpreters have called this a puzzle: why hand the gentle snail a verse of grisly condemnation? The puzzle may answer itself from the dictionary. שַׁבְּלוּל is a hapax legomenon — Psalm 58:9 is the *only* verse in the entire Hebrew Bible that contains the word "snail." A compiler who wanted the creature to sing a verse that names it had no other option in all of Scripture. The "song" may have been forced by lexicography, not chosen for theology.
**Verse 7 — Yom Kippur selichah.** Exactly one verse is mined for the fixed liturgy, and only once: הֲרֹס שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ, "smash their teeth in their mouth" (v. 7), embedded in a selichah within the Shacharit Amidah of the Ashkenazic Yom Kippur machzor. There it is reworked to the poem's rhyme — הֲרֹס שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ וְיֻדְמָמוּ, "smash their teeth in their mouth and let them be silenced" — and punctuated by the refrain חָטָאנוּ, "we have sinned." The placement is theologically shrewd. The imprecation against "those who plotted to seize my soul" is sayable only because it is wrapped inside the community's confession of its own guilt. That instinct — that one may curse the wicked only while admitting one's own sin — is precisely the resolution Dietrich Bonhoeffer reached from the opposite direction, arguing that no sinner can honestly pray this psalm except vicariously, through the only innocent one. The machzor performs what the theologian theorized.

**Verse 1**
לַמְנַצֵּ֥חַ אַל־תַּשְׁחֵ֗ת לְדָוִ֥ד מִכְתָּֽם׃
The essay drew out אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת as an echo of David sparing Saul (1 Sam 26:9, אַל־תַּשְׁחִיתֵהוּ); here the focus is the rest of the heading. לַמְנַצֵּחַ, "for the leader/choirmaster," is the standard musical-administrative tag. אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת, "do not destroy," is shared with Psalms 57, 59, and 75 — a cluster of crisis-psalms, which is what makes the redactional grouping feel deliberate rather than accidental. The root שחת carries heavy freight elsewhere: it is the word for the corruption that filled the earth before the Flood (Gen 6:11–12) and the destruction threatened against Sodom (Gen 18:28). A title borrowed from David's refusal to shed Saul's blood thus sits atop a poem that demands blood — handed up, not taken.
מִכְתָּם is the genuine mystery. The word is confined to six psalms — 16 and the run 56–60 — all Davidic, with 56–60 clustered around persecution, which again suggests an editorial grouping rather than a transparent meaning. The etymology was already lost in antiquity. The Septuagint renders it εἰς στηλογραφίαν, "for a stele-inscription," reading it from כתב, "to write" — an engraving meant to last. Rashi takes it more modestly as שֵׁם נְעִימוּת שִׁיר, "the name of a melody." Others have proposed כתם, "gold" (a golden poem), or the Akkadian katāmu, "to cover/atone." The LXX's "inscription" reading is the most intriguing, because it turns these Davidic poems into a set of monuments — testimonies cut in stone precisely when the psalmist's enemies would prefer them erased.
**Verse 2**
הַאֻמְנָ֗ם אֵ֣לֶם צֶ֭דֶק תְּדַבֵּר֑וּן; מֵישָׁרִ֥ים תִּ֝שְׁפְּט֗וּ בְּנֵ֣י אָדָֽם׃
The opening word is a prosecutor's pounce. הַאֻמְנָם, "is it really true?", is a rare interrogative built from the root for firmness and truth (אמן), and its biblical company is telling: it appears when Balak snaps at Balaam (Num 22:37) and when Solomon marvels הַאֻמְנָם יֵשֵׁב אֱלֹקִים עַל־הָאָרֶץ, "will God really dwell on earth?" (1 Kgs 8:27). It opens challenges, not compliments. Here it expects the answer "no," which v. 3 supplies with its emphatic אַף, "on the contrary."
אֵלֶם is the crux of the psalm. Three readings compete. Read as אֵלִים it means "gods/mighty ones" — the divine council of Psalm 82, addressees indicted for cosmic misrule (compare בְּנֵי אֵלִים, "sons of gods," Ps 29:1). Read as אִלֵּם it means "mute ones," producing a sharp paradox: the silent ones who nonetheless *speak* (תְּדַבֵּרוּן). The Jewish exegetes take a third path. Rashi and Metzudat David, reading the psalm against the night-camp, hear David taunting Saul's silent courtiers: הַאֻמְנָם — were you really *struck dumb* about the justice you should have spoken? Radak and Meiri instead read אֵלֶם as "assembly" (from the sheaf-binding of אֲלֻמִּים, Gen 37:7), addressing the bench of judges. The Septuagint cut the knot by deleting the word entirely and leaving the line second-person: εἰ ἀληθῶς ἄρα δικαιοσύνην λαλεῖτε, betraying that the Hebrew was already opaque to its earliest translators. I take the human-judges reading: the poem's whole force is forensic, and מֵישָׁרִים תִּשְׁפְּטוּ, "do you judge with equity," is courtroom language (cf. Ps 99:4).
The verb תְּדַבֵּרוּן carries the archaic paragogic ending -ūn, a lofty poetic register that, as we will see, clusters precisely in the face-to-face indictment of vv. 2–3. The Talmud, characteristically, repurposes the verse for ethics: Rabbi Yitzḥak reads מַה אוּמָנוּתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה — יָשִׂים עַצְמוֹ כְּאִלֵּם, "what should a person's craft be? to make himself like a mute" — except, the verse adds, for words of Torah (Chullin 89a).
**Verse 3**
אַף־בְּלֵב֮ עוֹלֹ֢ת תִּפְעָ֫ל֥וּן; בָּאָ֡רֶץ חֲמַ֥ס יְ֝דֵיכֶ֗ם תְּפַלֵּסֽוּן׃
The indictment moves through the body: from בְּלֵב, "in the heart," where injustice is devised, to יְדֵיכֶם, "your hands," where it is executed. Rashi catches the anatomy — וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁבִּלְבַבְכֶם אַתֶּם חוֹרְשִׁים רָעָה, "not only that, but in your hearts you plot evil." Cognitive and kinetic corruption together; nothing is accidental.
עוֹלֹת, "wrongdoings," shares its consonants with עוֹלוֹת, "burnt-offerings" — a grim near-pun, as if justice were being sacrificed on the altar of the court. The verb is the gold of the verse. תְּפַלֵּסוּן comes from פלס, "to weigh on a balance, to make level" — the most precise vocabulary the language has for calibrated fairness. It belongs to the wisdom literature's image of God auditing every path: מְפַלֵּס, "He weighs all his ways" (Prov 5:21). Ibn Ezra catches the venom of the choice: תַּעֲשׂוּ חָמָס כְּאִלּוּ הוּא מְפֻלָּס, "you commit violence as though it were weighed out on a scale." The corruption is not careless; it is engineered to the gram.
And the instrument is stolen property. A proverb assigns the balance explicitly to its rightful owner: פֶּלֶס וּמֹאזְנֵי מִשְׁפָּט לַה׳, "a just balance and scales belong to the LORD" (Prov 16:11). When these judges "weigh out" violence, they are handling God's own scale, which is why the poem's final word — that God *judges* on earth (v. 12) — reads as the recovery of a confiscated tool. The phrase בָּאָרֶץ opens the envelope that closes in v. 12: the earth of violence becomes the earth of verdict.
**Verse 4**
זֹ֣רוּ רְשָׁעִ֣ים מֵרָ֑חֶם; תָּע֥וּ מִ֝בֶּ֗טֶן דֹּבְרֵ֥י כָזָֽב׃
The evidence phase argues that wickedness here is congenital, not acquired. The first verb is ambiguous by design. זֹרוּ may come from זוּר, "to be estranged," or from זָרָה, "to scatter." Rashi reads estrangement — מִמְּעֵי אִמָּם הֵם נַעֲשִׂים זָרִים לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, "from their mother's womb they become strangers to the Holy One" — and links it to Esau struggling in Rebekah's womb (Gen 25:22). The Septuagint commits decisively to estrangement: ἀπηλλοτριώθησαν, "they were alienated," making an ontological claim — these are estranged beings, not merely badly-behaved ones. That claim is the engine of what follows: a creature alienated from birth is portrayed as beyond the reach of correction, which is why vv. 5–6 will declare it uncharmable and v. 7 will resort to demolition.
Malbim presses the parallelism into a fine distinction. The open רְשָׁעִים, he says, are זֹרוּ מֵרָחֶם — corrupted *after* birth, by bad company; but the דֹּבְרֵי כָזָב, "speakers of lies," are תָּעוּ מִבֶּטֶן, gone astray *before* birth, in the belly itself: their evil is עַצְמִי וְטִבְעִי, "essential and natural to them." The two synonyms for birth-origin, מֵרָחֶם, "from the womb," and מִבֶּטֶן, "from the belly," do more than fill out a line; they form one pole of the psalm's birth-and-anti-birth frame, answered in v. 9 by the wish that these same wombs had produced only a stillbirth. דֹּבְרֵי כָזָב returns us to the mouth — those who should speak צֶדֶק (v. 2) speak only falsehood, the indictment's first organ already failing.
**Verse 5**
חֲמַת־לָ֗מוֹ כִּדְמ֥וּת חֲמַת־נָחָ֑שׁ; כְּמוֹ־פֶ֥תֶן חֵ֝רֵ֗שׁ יַאְטֵ֥ם אׇזְנֽוֹ׃
חֲמַת is a deliberate double meaning. It is "venom," but its root is also that of חֵמָה, "wrath, burning rage" — the judges' fury *is* their poison. And the word sounds, across the section break, against חֲמַס, "violence," of v. 3: the ḥ-m cluster acoustically welds judicial violence to serpent venom. This is no isolated pun. The sister-psalm 140 fuses the very same elements — שָׁנְנוּ לְשׁוֹנָם כְּמוֹ־נָחָשׁ חֲמַת עַכְשׁוּב תַּחַת שְׂפָתֵימוֹ, "they sharpen their tongues like a serpent; the venom of the asp is under their lips" (Ps 140:4) — and Psalm 140 also carries חָמָס, confirming that "violent speech = serpent venom" was a conventional cluster, not a chance collision of sounds.
The comparison is curiously elaborate: not the simple כְּמוֹ but כִּדְמוּת, "like the *likeness* of." The doubled form adds a layer of mediation; it is phrasing familiar from visionary description (compare Ezek 1:5, 26). The extra layer makes the serpent slightly less barnyard and more uncanny.
But the focus then swerves from the venom to the *ear*. The real horror is not that the viper bites but that it יַאְטֵם אָזְנוֹ, "stops up its ear." The verb אטם is active and deliberate: this is a creature that shuts its own hearing. Rashi preserves an old folk-zoology: the aging snake goes deaf in one ear and plugs the other with dust so as not to hear the charmer's spell. The theological payoff is the hinge of the whole psalm: this evil is not merely dangerous, it is *uncharmable* — sealed against every word of correction. And the singular אָזְנוֹ, "its ear," for a creature with two, is enough: one stopped ear symbolizes total closure.
**Verse 6**
אֲשֶׁ֣ר לֹֽא־יִ֭שְׁמַע לְק֣וֹל מְלַחֲשִׁ֑ים; חוֹבֵ֖ר חֲבָרִ֣ים מְחֻכָּֽם׃
The verse completes the deafness: the viper "will not hear the voice of charmers. מְלַחֲשִׁים, whisperers," are snake-charmers whose root לחש is itself a hiss — the technique is acoustic, the quietest, most intimate form of address. The irony is exquisite: the charmer deploys the gentlest possible sound against an ear that has bolted itself shut. The Bible can treat such arts suspiciously; Deut 18:11, for example, bans חֹבֵר חָבֶר, the spell-binder named in the next colon.
חוֹבֵר חֲבָרִים, "binder of bindings," is a cognate accusative — Hebrew's most emphatic construction, doubling the root to mean "the consummate spell-binder." It too appears verbatim in the prohibition list (חֹבֵר חָבֶר, Deut 18:11), and מְחֻכָּם means "expert": this is mastered, professional skill. The Ugaritic incantation texts (KTU 1.100, RS 92.2014) show what such expertise meant in the wider world — priests binding serpents with sacred wood, stones, and whispered formulae. Psalm 58 conjures the full apparatus of Near Eastern snake-magic and then declares it useless. And that is the verse's structural work: the poet builds the charmer's competence to its absolute peak — *expert* binder, mastered wisdom — at the exact instant of total failure. Human persuasion, even at its most skilled, cannot penetrate a willed deafness. Only God can act, which is why v. 7 turns upward. Metzudat David draws the moral plainly: לֹא יוֹעִיל בָּם הַמּוּסָר כְּאִלּוּ הָיוּ חֵרְשִׁים, "rebuke does them no good, as though they were deaf."
**Verse 7**
אֱֽלֹקִ֗ים הֲרׇס־שִׁנֵּ֥ימֽוֹ בְּפִ֑ימוֹ; מַלְתְּע֥וֹת כְּ֝פִירִ֗ים נְתֹ֣ץ ׀ ה׳׃
This is the pivot, and it is marked twice over. It is the only verse carrying both divine names — אֱלֹקִים opening, ה׳ closing — and Nils Lund identified its symmetrical bracketing: Elohim / smash (הֲרֹס) / teeth and fangs / shatter (נְתֹץ) / YHWH. The two names bracket the two demolition-verbs like an envelope wrapped around the violence, summoning God as universal power *and* covenant partner at the exact line where the lawsuit is handed up to the bench. Every prior verse addressed human beings; from here the petition is vertical.
Both verbs are demolition vocabulary. הֲרֹס, "tear down," is architectural language; נְתֹץ, "shatter," likewise. Teeth treated as a structure to be pulled down. The apparently redundant בְּפִימוֹ, "in their mouth," is the verse's signature: destroy the weapon precisely at its site of deployment — the mouth that produced the venomous speech of vv. 2–5 is the mouth that gets unmade. מַלְתְּעוֹת כְּפִירִים, "the fangs of young lions," shifts the predator-image from covert serpent to overt lion; the כְּפִיר, the young lion, is the hungrier and fiercer beast. The figure is conventional for instrument-targeting judgment: וְשִׁנֵּי כְפִירִים נִתָּעוּ, "the teeth of the young lions are broken" (Job 4:10); Job 29:17 makes the ethics explicit — וָאֲשַׁבְּרָה מְתַלְּעוֹת עַוָּל וּמִשִּׁנָּיו אַשְׁלִיךְ טָרֶף, "I broke the fangs of the wrongdoer and snatched the prey from his teeth." The prayer is not for slaughter but for *disarmament* at the mouth.
The liturgical afterlife of this one verse is striking and was discussed above: only הֲרֹס שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ enters the fixed prayer-book, and only on Yom Kippur morning, reworked into a selichah under the refrain חָטָאנוּ, "we have sinned." The community may pray for its enemies' teeth to be smashed only while confessing that its own guilt helped empower those enemies — a liturgical answer to Bonhoeffer's worry that no sinner can honestly utter this curse at all.
**Verse 8**
יִמָּאֲס֣וּ כְמוֹ־מַ֭יִם יִתְהַלְּכוּ־לָ֑מוֹ; יִדְרֹ֥ךְ חִ֝צָּ֗ו כְּמ֣וֹ יִתְמֹלָֽלוּ׃
The dissolution cascade begins, and the syntax begins to buckle — appropriately, for verses about things losing their form. יִמָּאֲסוּ literally means "let them be rejected/despised," but the medieval grammarians read the aleph as standing in for a doubled letter, so the sense is יִמַּסּוּ, "let them melt" — water that "walks away" (יִתְהַלְּכוּ) downstream and leaves no trace. Radak: שֶׁיִּהְיוּ בְּטֵלִים, "let them be nullified." The image inverts the usual force of water in Scripture: elsewhere water is overwhelming power (a flood "sweeps through," Dan 11:10); here it is power draining off into the ground.
The second colon is one of the psalm's genuine hard cases. יִדְרֹךְ חִצָּו, "he treads/draws his arrows," elides the expected object — one normally "treads a *bow*" (דרך + קֶשֶׁת), not arrows — so an object has dropped out. Ibn Ezra supplies the missing archer: as though it read יִדְרֹךְ הַדּוֹרֵךְ חִצָּיו אֲלֵיהֶם, "let the one who draws aim his arrows at them," with God as the archer (so too Metzudat David: הַמָּקוֹם יִדְרֹךְ עֲלֵיהֶם חִצָּיו). Then comes the grammatical oddity: כְּמוֹ before a finite verb, יִתְמֹלָלוּ, where כְּמוֹ normally precedes a noun. The verb מלל means to wither or be cut off, as grass "withers" (Ps 90:6). Malbim reads it vividly: like a stalk whose head is nipped off, so the arrowheads are snapped. Whether one hears two images here (melting water, snapped arrows) or one strained simile, the difficulty is not the commentator's failure but the poet's medium — the lines about things coming apart are themselves coming apart at the seams.
**Verse 9**
כְּמ֣וֹ שַׁ֭בְּלוּל תֶּ֣מֶס יַהֲלֹ֑ךְ; נֵ֥פֶל אֵ֝֗שֶׁת בַּל־חָ֥זוּ שָֽׁמֶשׁ׃
The cascade reaches its strangest pair: a snail and a stillbirth. שַׁבְּלוּל is an absolute hapax, found nowhere else in the Bible — which, as noted above, may explain why *Perek Shirah* assigns this verse to the snail; no other verse names the creature. The ancient folk-belief was that a snail dissolves into its own slime-trail as it crawls, consuming itself in the act of moving forward: תֶּמֶס יַהֲלֹךְ, "it melts as it goes." Biologically false, poetically devastating. The Septuagint, faced with the unknown word, substituted κηρός, "wax" — a recognizable melting-metaphor (compare כְּהִמֵּס דּוֹנַג, "as wax melts," Ps 68:3), surrendering the obscure creature for a familiar image. The snail also completes a degradation-chain: the feared serpent of v. 5, the powerful young lion of v. 7, and now a slug oozing itself away — what the wicked claim to be, reduced to what God makes of them.
The French poet Francis Ponge, in his 1942 prose-poem "Escargots," looked at the same trail and saw something quieter: *Un sillage argenté les suit. Et peut-être les signale au bec des volatiles. Voilà le hic, la rançon* — "A silvery wake follows them, and perhaps betrays them to the beaks of the birds. There's the rub, the ransom." Ponge's snail is slowly exposed by the very trace it leaves; the psalmist takes the identical observation and accelerates it into a curse — not slow betrayal but immediate self-dissolution.
נֵפֶל אֵשֶׁת, "a woman's stillbirth," supplies the human pole. נֵפֶל is the untimely birth that, as Job says, is "better" than a wretched long life — אוֹ כְנֵפֶל טָמוּן לֹא אֶהְיֶה כְּעֹלְלִים לֹא־רָאוּ אוֹר, "or like a hidden stillbirth I would not be, like infants who never saw light" (Job 3:16). That "never saw light" matches our בַּל־חָזוּ שָׁמֶשׁ, "never saw the sun" — and the verb is חזה, the prophetic verb of *seeing* that returns in v. 11. The Talmud preserves the folk-reading that אֵשֶׁת here, or אִישׁוּת, denotes a creature with no eyes at all, a blind mole-like thing born underground (Moed Katan 6b) — non-sight made flesh. This is the back wall of the birth-frame: those born wicked from the womb (v. 4) are wished into the condition of the womb's failure.
**Verse 10**
בְּטֶ֤רֶם ׀ יָבִ֣ינוּ סִּירֹתֵכֶ֣ם אָטָ֑ד; כְּמוֹ־חַ֥י כְּמוֹ־חָ֝ר֗וֹן יִשְׂעָרֶֽנּוּ׃
Widely conceded the hardest verse in the Psalter, and the difficulty is real: the address shifts mid-verse from second-person plural (סִּירֹתֵיכֶם, "your pots/thorns") to a third-person singular object (יִשְׂעָרֶנּוּ, "he sweeps *him* away"), and סִּיר means both "thornbush" and "cooking-pot." Two readings result. On the botanical reading (Hai Gaon, via Ibn Ezra): סִּירֹתֵכֶם are tender thorn-buds, אָטָד the mature hardened bramble, and the sense is "before your thorns harden into a bramble" — the wicked uprooted before they reach full destructive growth (so Metzudat David: עַד לֹא יַבִינוּ בְּנֵי הָרְשָׁעִים לְהַרְשִׁיעַ כַּאֲבִיהֶם, "before the children of the wicked learn to do evil like their fathers"). On the culinary reading (Radak, Malbim): the thorns are kindling under a cooking-pot, and judgment strikes כְּמוֹ־חַי, "while the meat is still raw" — before the pot even feels the heat. This second reading quietly invokes Ecclesiastes' image of the fool's brief, crackling laughter, כְּקוֹל הַסִּירִים תַּחַת הַסִּיר, "like the sound of thorns under the pot" (Eccl 7:6): loud, fast, and gone.
Either way, the gold of the verse is בְּטֶרֶם יָבִינוּ, "before they understand." Recall that this psalm has already dwelt on calculated expertise — the judges "weigh out" with precision (תְּפַלֵּסוּן, v. 3), and the charmer of v. 6 represents human skill at its most professional. Now understanding itself fails at the one moment it would matter: the wicked are swept off *before they can even discern* what is happening. The poem builds human competence to a peak in vv. 3 and 6 precisely so it can collapse here — all that skill, caught uncomprehending. The closing verb יִשְׂעָרֶנּוּ, from סָעַר, "to storm/whirl," is theophany-vocabulary: ה׳ בְּסוּפָה וּבִשְׂעָרָה דַּרְכּוֹ, "the LORD's way is in whirlwind and storm" (Nah 1:3). On the person-shift, Meiri shrugs with hard-won wisdom: וְדֶרֶךְ הַנְּבוּאוֹת לְדַבֵּר פַּעַם לְשׁוֹן יָחִיד פַּעַם לְשׁוֹן רַבִּים, "prophecy speaks now in singular, now in plural" — the grammar of a poem in a hurry.
**Verse 11**
יִשְׂמַ֣ח צַ֭דִּיק כִּי־חָזָ֣ה נָקָ֑ם; פְּעָמָ֥יו יִ֝רְחַ֗ץ בְּדַ֣ם הָרָשָֽׁע׃
The verdict's aftermath turns on the eye. The verb is חָזָה, not ordinary רָאָה — the *prophet's* word for seeing, the seeing of Amos, Micah, Habakkuk (חָזָה עַל־יִשְׂרָאֵל, "what he envisioned concerning Israel," Amos 1:1). The righteous does not merely witness vengeance; he *beholds* it as a revelation, the thing faith had anticipated now made visible. And it answers v. 9 exactly: the stillborn wicked בַּל־חָזוּ, "never saw," the sun; the righteous צַדִּיק חָזָה, "the righteous has seen," vindication. Sight is the axis of who participates in the divine order — and Psalm 58 is not alone here. Its two nearest neighbors in the family of judgment-psalms share the same signature: in Psalm 11 "the upright shall behold His face" (יָשָׁר יֶחֱזוּ פָנֵימוֹ, 11:7), and in Psalm 17 "in righteousness I shall behold Your face" (אֲנִי בְּצֶדֶק אֶחֱזֶה פָנֶיךָ, 17:15). Across all three, the righteous are defined as those granted to *see*.
נָקָם is not personal spite but covenant-justice — God's prerogative (Ez 24:8 uses it for divine retribution). The image פְּעָמָיו יִרְחַץ בְּדַם, "he will bathe his feet in the blood," is conventional Near-Eastern victory iconography, the wading-through-gore of total defeat (cf. Isa 63:1–3). Its closest mythological cousin is the Ugaritic Anat, who wades knee-deep, neck-deep, in the blood of her foes (KTU 1.3 II) — but with a pointed difference: Anat does the slaughtering herself, while here the righteous merely *observes* and bathes in the aftermath. The execution belongs to God; the human is a spectator.
No reader of this verse should leave it without Bialik's devastating answer. Writing in 1903 after the Kishinev pogrom, the Hebrew national poet stood the imprecatory tradition on its head in עַל הַשְּׁחִיטָה, "On the Slaughter":
> וְאָרוּר הָאוֹמֵר: נְקֹם!
> נְקָמָה כָזֹאת, נִקְמַת דַּם יֶלֶד קָטָן
> עוֹד לֹא־בָרָא הַשָּׂטָן
"And cursed be he who says: Avenge! / Such vengeance — vengeance for the blood of a small child — / Satan himself has not yet created." Where the ancient psalmist promises the righteous the *joy* of seeing vengeance, the modern one curses the very word נְקָם, declaring that no revenge could be adequate and so none should be invoked — only the unredeemable cry of the blood remains. Bialik knows the psalm cold; that is why his refusal lands. He has taken the psalm's own forensic confidence — *there will be vengeance, and you will see it* — and confronted it with an atrocity for which the promise feels obscene. The two poems together mark the full arc of Jewish wrestling with this verse: the psalm's certainty that justice will be *seen*, and the modern poet's fear that some horrors outrun the very category of recompense.
The Talmud, meanwhile, found the verse fulfilled at Jacob's burial, when Jacob's grandson Ḥushim struck the obstructing Esau and Esau's eyes fell onto Jacob's lap, so that the dead patriarch smiled: בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה נִתְקַיְּמָה נְבוּאָתָהּ שֶׁל רִבְקָה, "at that moment Rebekah's prophecy was fulfilled" (Sotah 13a).
**Verse 12**
וְיֹאמַ֣ר אָ֭דָם אַךְ־פְּרִ֣י לַצַּדִּ֑יק; אַ֥ךְ יֵשׁ־אֱ֝לֹקִ֗ים שֹׁפְטִ֥ים בָּאָֽרֶץ׃
The poem ends not in the psalmist's voice but in the restored voice of humanity. וְיֹאמַר אָדָם, "and humankind will say" — and the choice of speaker is the psalm's final argument. The אָדָם here is the same generic humanity that was the *object* of corrupt judgment in v. 2 (תִּשְׁפְּטוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם, "you judge mankind"). Those judged unjustly become the confessing witnesses to justice. The mouth that opened the psalm in corruption (the judges who would not speak צֶדֶק) is, at the close, returned to honest function in the mouth of ordinary people. The whole speech-arc resolves: corrupt speech (v. 2) → willed deafness (vv. 5–6) → enforced silence (vv. 7–10) → true speech (v. 12).
The doubled אַךְ, "surely… surely," is an emphatic cadence that answers the skeptical הַאֻמְנָם, "is it really true?", of v. 2 — the opening doubt resolved into closing certainty. אַךְ־פְּרִי לַצַּדִּיק, "there is indeed fruit for the righteous," recasts the entire judgment as harvest: the wicked dissolved like water and snail-slime (vv. 8–9), but the righteous *bear fruit*. The agricultural metaphor for just recompense is standard — כִּפְרִי מַעֲלָלָיו, "according to the fruit of his deeds" (Jer 17:10) — but it lands here as a deliberate contrast to dissolution: against things that come to nothing, something that grows.
The final phrase is grammatically charged: אֱלֹקִים שֹׁפְטִים, with a plural participle, "God who judges." Radak offers the safe reading — לְשׁוֹן רַבִּים דֶּרֶךְ כָּבוֹד, "a plural of majesty." But the plural also completes a reversal that the whole psalm has been arranging. The verb שׁפט bracketed the poem: the corrupt judges *judged* mankind (תִּשְׁפְּטוּ, v. 2); now God *judges* (שֹׁפְטִים, v. 12) — same root, agency transferred. Ibn Ezra hears the divine council itself — שֹׁפְטִים, הַמַּלְאָכִים, "the judges are the angels," administering decrees by God's word — so that the corrupt council indicted in v. 2 is answered by the true one. And בָּאָרֶץ shuts the envelope opened in v. 3: the earth that was the site of "violence" is now, in the poem's last word, the site of judgment — the very ground of the crime made the ground of the verdict.---
