# Commentary on Psalm 59
---
## Introduction
The header to Psalm 59 reports a night of surveillance. Saul "sent men, and they watched the house to kill him" — וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת־הַבַּיִת לַהֲמִיתוֹ. The poem that grows out of that night ends, eighteen verses later, with a man who has stopped being watched and started to sing: אֲזַמֵּרָה, "I will make music to You." Between the watching and the singing lies the whole psalm — and, it turns out, a single consonant. The verb "to watch," שׁמר, and the verb "to sing," זמר, share the same two-letter spine, mem-resh. Trade the opening sibilant for a *zayin* and the watchman's vigil becomes worship. The psalmist appears to have heard this. He builds his final refrain out of the enemy's own verb and then converts it by the smallest change the language allows.
That is the argument here: Psalm 59 does not so much defeat the surveillance that opens it as transmute it, and the transmutation is the work of a refrain that shifts one word at a time. To see this we need the floor plan first. The psalm runs in two matched halves on a double refrain. **Verses 2–6** open with a fourfold cry for rescue, a triple protest of innocence, and an appeal that suddenly widens from Saul's thugs to "all the nations." **Verses 7–10** then give the first cycle: a dog-simile (v. 7), the enemies' boast (v. 8), God's derisive laughter (v. 9), and a refrain of confidence (v. 10). **Verses 11–14** bring God's covenant-love onto the field and issue a strange, restrained imprecation. **Verses 15–18** run the cycle again — the same dog-simile (v. 15), the enemies degraded (v. 16), morning praise (v. 17), and the confidence-refrain returned (v. 18). Two refrains alternate: Refrain A, the dogs (vv. 7, 15, almost word for word), and Refrain B, the confession (vv. 10, 18, deliberately altered). The dogs come back almost unchanged because dogs circling a city is what relentlessness *sounds* like. The confession comes back changed because the psalmist has changed.
Everything turns on those changes. Set the two halves of Refrain B side by side. Verse 10: עֻזּוֹ אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמֹרָה כִּי אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי, and verse 18: עֻזִּי אֵלֶיךָ אֲזַמֵּרָה כִּי אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי. Three things move, and they are not three ornaments — they are three separate journeys that the poet has arranged to land in the same line. First, the possessive: עֻזּוֹ, "*his* strength," becomes עֻזִּי, "*my* strength." Second, the verb: אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "I watch," becomes אֲזַמֵּרָה, "I sing" — the *sh*-to-*z* swap, with the cohortative shell and the closing syllable held in place (the phonetics are consistent with a near-rhyme: `'esh-MŌ-rāh` against `'a-zam-MĒ-rāh`, both ending *-rāh*). Third, an addition: the psalm acquires three new final words, אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, "God of my faithful love."
Take the possessive first, because the medieval commentators quietly solve a problem modern readers tend to call unsolvable. In verse 10, "*his* strength" (third person) is addressed to "*You*" (second person) — a grammatical mismatch that has tempted editors to "fix" עֻזּוֹ into עֻזִּי. But Rashi, Radak, and the Metzudat all read עֻזּוֹ as the *enemy's* strength: "the strength and might of my enemy, who is stronger than I" (Rashi), "Saul's strength, who is mightier than I" (Radak). On their reading nothing is broken; the wrong person is the point. And it slots into a four-stage handover that runs the length of the psalm. In verse 4 the attackers are עַזִּים, "fierce ones"; in verse 10 the strength still belongs to them, עֻזּוֹ; in verse 17 it has passed to God, עֻזֶּךָ, "*Your* strength"; in verse 18 it arrives in the psalmist's own hand, עֻזִּי, "*my* strength." The enemies' defining attribute — fierce strength — is confiscated and routed, through God, to the man they were hunting. The single-letter change from suffix *-o* to suffix *-i* is the last rung of a ladder four verses tall.
The verb-change and the third element work the same way. אֶשְׁמֹרָה in verse 10 is still the language of the besieged: watching, vigilant, defensive, the same root the superscription used of Saul's men watching David's house. By verse 18 watching has become singing — not because the danger is gone (the dogs are still circling in v. 15) but because the posture has been converted. And the new closing words, אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, are the third trajectory reaching ground. חֶסֶד, covenant-loyalty, has been climbing toward the surface — named as חַסְדֶּךָ in verse 17, and quietly seeded back in verse 11. The refrain is longer the second time not because the line needed padding but because a third progression had to cash out alongside the other two. Strength, posture, and covenant-love all finish in the final verse, which is why the final verse is the only one that says all three.
This is also a psalm about a recognizable human terror: being hunted inside your own home, watched by men who assume no one is watching them. Their creed is verse 8 — כִּי מִי שֹׁמֵעַ, "Who hears?" — the practical atheism of every persecutor who believes the night is private. The psalm answers not with thunder but with laughter: וְאַתָּה ה׳ תִּשְׂחַק לָמוֹ, "But You, O LORD, laugh at them" (v. 9), in the very words Psalm 2 uses for God's contempt of raging nations (יִשְׂחָק). The God who is asked "Who hears?" both hears and finds the question funny. Milton caught the same register in *Paradise Lost*, where the Father "secure / Laugh'st at thir vain designs and tumults vain" — though Milton's laughter rolls down from untouched heaven, while the psalmist has to summon it from inside a surrounded house.
And that gap — between Milton's serenity and the psalmist's danger — is where the consonant earns its keep. The enemy's act is *watching*. The psalmist does not erase it; in verse 10 he takes it up himself and redirects it toward God. Then, in verse 18, holding the mouth-shape of the word almost intact, he lets the vigil tip over into song.
The one thing to carry away: in this psalm surveillance is never defeated, only converted — שׁמר becomes זמר, and the distance from being watched to giving praise turns out to be exactly one letter wide.

#### Full psalm
Psalm 59 is not part of the fixed daily, Shabbat, or festival liturgy of any major rite. Its most structured communal life is in Hasidic practice through Rebbe Nachman of Breslov's **Tikkun HaKlali**, the "General Remedy" of ten psalms (16, 32, 41, 42, 59, 77, 90, 105, 137, 150) recited for spiritual rectification — Psalm 59 standing fifth. The mapping is allegorical: Saul's assassins become intrusive impurity, David's survival becomes spiritual resilience, and the psalm's contrast between brazen enemy *azut* and the holy boldness of trust (*azut d'kedushah*) is read as the inner drama of self-mastery. One does not easily build daily prayer out of "consume them, consume them" (v. 14), and the psalm's afterlife reflects that: it lives at the edges of the siddur, in voluntary and penitential settings rather than at the center.
#### Key verses and phrases
**Psalm 59:6 and 17 in Perek Shirah.** In the printed Siddur Sefard (the Hasidic rite), these verses enter worship through *Perek Shirah*, the anthology of "songs" assigned to creation, recited as a voluntary devotion after the weekday morning service. The fierce petition הָקִיצָה לִפְקֹד כָּל־הַגּוֹיִם … אַל־תָּחֹן כָּל־בֹּגְדֵי אָוֶן, "rouse Yourself to call all the nations to account … have no mercy on any treacherous villain," is placed in the mouth of a non-human creature alongside the trusting וַאֲנִי אָשִׁיר עֻזֶּךָ, "But I will sing of Your strength." Why an imprecation against "all the nations" should serve as one creature's hymn in a collection otherwise full of praise is not self-evident, and later commentators offer various explanations.
**Psalm 59:18 in the Rosh Hashanah Kedushah.** The richest deployment is a piyyut embedded in the *chazzan's* repetition of the Shacharit Amidah in the Ashkenazi machzor, where the prayer-leader pleads for strength to speak before the Day of Judgment: עֻזִּי אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמְרָה לְסַעֲדִי … כִּי אַתָּה מִשְׂגַּבִּי אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, "My strength — toward You I keep watch, for my support … for You are my stronghold, God of my faithful love." This is worth noticing closely. The poet has spliced the two halves of the psalm's refrain: he borrows the *possessive* from verse 18 (עֻזִּי, "*my* strength," and the closing אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי) but keeps the *verb* from verse 10 (אֶשְׁמְרָה, "I keep watch," from שׁמר — not verse 18's זמר). A liturgist working centuries ago felt the interchange between the two refrains and exploited it, choosing the "watching" pole deliberately: the *shaliach tzibbur* guards his reliance on divine strength (לְסַעֲדִי) precisely as he prepares to proclaim God's kingship. It is concrete external evidence that the psalm's refrain-pair was heard as convertible — the very move the poem itself performs.

## Verse-by-Verse Commentary
**Verse 1**
לַמְנַצֵּחַ אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת לְדָוִד מִכְתָּם; בִּשְׁלֹחַ שָׁאוּל, וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת־הַבַּיִת לַהֲמִיתוֹ׃
Three labels and a story, packed into one line. The performance note אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת, "do not destroy," is shared with Psalms 57, 58, and 75 — most likely the name of a tune, possibly the incipit of a vintage song (compare Isaiah 65:8, אַל־תַּשְׁחִיתֵהוּ כִּי בְרָכָה בּוֹ, "do not destroy it, for a blessing is in it"). Rashi, uninterested in musicology, reads it theologically: David "was close to dying and to being destroyed, and he begged for mercy." There is a quiet possibility worth flagging as no more than that: a similar negated-destruction imperative reappears inside the psalm at verse 12, אַל־תַּהַרְגֵם, "do not kill them," and the only other famous אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת in the Bible is Moses interceding — אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת עַמְּךָ, "do not destroy Your people" (Deut 9:26). The restraint David asks for himself may be the restraint he is made to extend to his enemies; this is suggestive, not demonstrable.
מִכְתָּם is opaque — it clusters only with David's crisis psalms (16, 56–60). The Greek renders it στηλογραφία, "an inscription on a stele," as if it were a poem to be engraved. The opacity is itself the datum: a genre-term we can no longer hear.
The verb that matters is וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ, "and they watched" — the seed of the whole psalm's arc (see the essay). One detail the essay passed over: the superscription speaks of David in the third person and ends לַהֲמִיתוֹ, "to put *him* to death," a small narrative distance opened just before the first-person voice takes over in verse 2. We watch the watchers before we hear the watched.
Lu Xun opens his 1918 *Diary of a Madman* (狂人日记) with the same conversion of a quiet street into a surveilled trap. His narrator's lucidity curdles mid-sentence into dread: 才知道以前的三十多年，全是发昏…… 那赵家的狗，何以看我两眼呢？ — "Only now do I realize the past thirty-odd years were all a daze… why did Zhao's dog look at me twice?" The psalmist, by contrast, suffers no such uncertainty: he knows exactly who is outside and who sent them. The clarity is the difference between political persecution and paranoia — but the felt architecture, a house turned inside-out by eyes, is shared.
**Verse 2**
הַצִּילֵנִי מֵאֹיְבַי אֱלֹקָי; מִמִּתְקוֹמְמַי תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי׃
Two requests with two different verbs of rescue, and the divine name held between them like a hinge: הַצִּילֵנִי … אֱלֹקָי … תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי. הַצִּילֵנִי (root נצל) is to be snatched out of reach; תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי (root שׂגב) is to be set up high, lifted beyond the attacker's grasp. Ibn Ezra and Radak both catch the spatial logic: Radak glosses, "as though You set me in a high and inaccessible place, that he cannot reach me." This is not a synonym but a second mode of safety — extraction versus elevation.
The verb תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי is also a seed. It returns as the noun מִשְׂגַּבִּי, "my high refuge," in the refrains of verses 10 and 18, and as past testimony in verse 17 (הָיִיתָ מִשְׂגָּב לִי, "You have *been* a refuge to me"). The petition becomes a confession becomes a memory: the word climbs through the psalm exactly as it asks God to climb the speaker out of danger.
מִמִּתְקוֹמְמַי, "from those who rise up against me," is built on a form of קום that suggests self-raising — they *raise themselves*. Self-elevation is baked into the grammar, and it connects forward to גְּאוֹנָם, "their pride" (v. 13), the very thing that will spring their trap. The doubled opening *mim-* (`MIM`-mith-qō-mə-may) gives the word a stammering emphasis, as if the threat were piling up faster than it can be named.
**Verse 3**
הַצִּילֵנִי מִפֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן; וּמֵאַנְשֵׁי דָמִים הוֹשִׁיעֵנִי׃
A third rescue verb, הוֹשִׁיעֵנִי (root ישׁע), adds battlefield victory to the spectrum of verse 2 — three distinct deliverance-words in two verses, a comprehensive request. פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן, "workers of iniquity," is a Psalter formula; compare Psalm 6:9, סוּרוּ מִמֶּנִּי כָּל־פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן כִּי שָׁמַע ה׳ קוֹל בִּכְיִי, "Depart from me, all workers of iniquity, for the LORD has heard the sound of my weeping" — note that there too the formula sits beside a claim that God *hears*, which is precisely what verse 8 will deny and verse 9 confirm.
The second epithet carries a biographical sting. אַנְשֵׁי דָמִים, "men of bloods" (plural — accumulated bloodshed), is the very charge thrown at David himself by Shimei: צֵא צֵא אִישׁ הַדָּמִים, "Out, out, you man of bloods!" (2 Sam 16:7). The hunted innocent disclaims in his pursuers the exact label tradition records being flung back at him. Some modern scholars (following Mowinckel) hear in פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן an allusion to sorcerers — practitioners of malevolent ritual — which would make the "swords on their lips" of verse 8 literal curses; the reading is speculative but it fits the psalm's obsession with weaponized speech.
**Verses 4–5**
כִּי הִנֵּה אָרְבוּ לְנַפְשִׁי, יָגוּרוּ עָלַי עַזִּים; לֹא־פִשְׁעִי וְלֹא־חַטָּאתִי ה׳׃ בְּלִי־עָוֺן יְרֻצוּן וְיִכּוֹנָנוּ; עוּרָה לִקְרָאתִי וּרְאֵה׃
The innocence-protest is exhaustive by design. Across these two verses the speaker denies three different categories of guilt with three different terms: פֶּשַׁע (willful rebellion), חַטָּאת (missing the mark), and עָוֺן (accumulated guilt) — the full legal-covenantal spectrum, each with its own negation. This is the Job posture: not "I am roughly innocent" but "search every drawer." The Septuagint flattens the distinctions into ἀνομία and ἁμαρτία; the Hebrew insists on the trichotomy.
עַזִּים, "fierce ones," is the first appearance of the עז root that the psalm will redistribute (see essay). Malbim sharpens the legal point: the ambush is "not for my offense nor my sin — only fierce men ambushing against all law." יָגוּרוּ the medievals read as "they gather, assemble" (Ibn Ezra, Radak), an ambush massing.
Then the verse pivots without warning from describing the enemies (יְרֻצוּן וְיִכּוֹנָנוּ, "they run, they array themselves") to seizing God by the sleeve: עוּרָה לִקְרָאתִי וּרְאֵה, "Rouse Yourself toward me, and see!" Ibn Ezra hears the polemic precisely: עוּרָה, "wake up," is said כנגד וישמרו את הבית — *against* the watching of verse 1. The men kept their lethal vigil; let God keep His.
Ingeborg Bachmann's 1952 "Alle Tage," written into the ruins of postwar Europe, makes the opposite assumption from the psalm's. Where the psalmist treats unprovoked assault on the innocent as a cosmic scandal that must wake God, Bachmann treats it as the new normal: *Der Krieg wird nicht mehr erklärt, / sondern fortgesetzt. Das Unerhörte / ist alltäglich geworden* — "War is no longer declared, / but continued. The unheard-of / has become an everyday thing." Her enjambments let atrocity bleed into routine; the psalmist refuses the bleed, and the refusal is the prayer.
**Verse 6**
וְאַתָּה ה׳־אֱלֹקִים צְבָקוֹת אֱלֹקֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, הָקִיצָה לִפְקֹד כָּל־הַגּוֹיִם; אַל־תָּחֹן כָּל־בֹּגְדֵי אָוֶן סֶלָה׃
The most maximal divine title in the psalm — four designations stacked in rapid sequence: ה׳ אֱלֹקִים צְבָקוֹת אֱלֹקֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. The accumulation is deployed at exactly the moment the camera pulls back from one house to "all the nations," as though cosmic scope demands a comprehensive name. There is a dry irony in the choice of צְבָקוֹת, "of armies": the God of armies is being summoned against forces sent by Israel's own king. Malbim turns this into the psalm's hinge — David, until now Israel's own field commander against the Philistines, hands his command to God: "You will now be Israel's commander and fight her wars."
The verb הָקִיצָה, "awake," is a *second* wake-up call, from a different root (קיץ) than verse 5's עוּרָה (עור). The pairing is not idle: Psalm 44:24 uses both in one breath, עוּרָה לָמָּה תִישַׁן … הָקִיצָה, "Awake — why do You sleep? … Rouse Yourself." Whether the poet means a fine distinction (stir from dormancy / wake from sleep) or simply varies for music, the doubled imperative makes the demand twice. לִפְקֹד is the famously two-faced verb פקד — it can mean to visit in blessing (God "visited" Sarah, Gen 21:1) or to call to account; the parallel אַל־תָּחֹן, "show no favor," fixes it as judicial. The first Selah lands here, a musical breath after the cosmic mandate.
**Verse 7**
יָשׁוּבוּ לָעֶרֶב, יֶהֱמוּ כַכָּלֶב; וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר׃
The first dog-refrain, and a deliberate choice of animal. Biblical poets had nobler predators on the shelf — the lion that confers terrifying majesty (Ps 7:3, פֶּן־יִטְרֹף כְּאַרְיֵה נַפְשִׁי, "lest he tear my soul like a lion"), the ravenous wolf, the "evening wolves" of Habakkuk and Zephaniah. The poet wants none of that dignity. The dog gives him menace *and* contempt at once: a creature both dangerous and low. To be called a כֶּלֶב was an insult — Goliath sneers at David, הֲכֶלֶב אָנֹכִי, "Am I a dog?" (1 Sam 17:43) — and the comparison strips the assassins of honor while keeping their threat.
The sound carries the snarl. Three consecutive long *u*-endings — `yā-SHŪ-vū … ye-he-MŪ … wiy-SŌ-və-vū` — with sibilants and *b*'s churning underneath, so the line growls as it describes growling. יֶהֱמוּ (root המה) is a thick, visceral word: it does the sea (Ps 46:4), it does churning bowels (Jer 31:20), it does a mob — here it does dogs. The Septuagint reads instead λιμώσσω, "they hunger," importing the starvation that the MT reserves for verse 16. That harmonizing instinct destroys the very mechanism the Hebrew is built on: the same verb and line return almost word-for-word in verse 15, threatening the first time and pathetic the second, the change living entirely in the context. The Greek translator, faced with a verb held constant across a degradation, normalized it — and flattened the trick.
Baudelaire, in his 1869 prose-poem "Les Bons Chiens," took the same urban scavenger and made it lyrical, singing *le chien crotté, le chien pauvre, le chien sans domicile … dont l'instinct … est merveilleusement aiguillonné par la nécessité* — "the dirty dog, the poor dog, the homeless dog … whose instinct is marvelously sharpened by necessity." Baudelaire redeems the stray as the cunning survivor of the modern street; the psalmist uses the identical figure to degrade. Same animal, opposite verdict.
**Verse 8**
הִנֵּה יַבִּיעוּן בְּפִיהֶם, חֲרָבוֹת בְּשִׂפְתוֹתֵיהֶם; כִּי־מִי שֹׁמֵעַ׃
A grotesque image, engineered. יַבִּיעוּן (root נבע) is what *springs* do — Proverbs has wisdom as a gushing brook (Prov 18:4) — and what gushes here from the mouth is edged metal: חֲרָבוֹת, "swords." Water-source and bladed weapon fused into one picture of speech that pours and cuts. The same michtam-cluster runs the figure in Psalm 57:4, וּלְשׁוֹנָם חֶרֶב חַדָּה, "and their tongue a sharp sword," and Psalm 64:4 sharpens it to a bowshot, דָּרְכוּ חִצָּם דָּבָר מָר, "they aimed their arrow — a bitter word." The choice of *sword* over *arrow* is a choice of range: not distant sniping but close encirclement, knives at the throat.
The boast is the psalm's theological core in three words: כִּי־מִי שֹׁמֵעַ, "for who hears?" This is the standard creed of the practical atheist — God has forgotten (Ps 10:11), God does not see (Ps 94:7). The enemies do not deny that God exists; they deny that He is paying attention. Rashi puts it inside their heads: וְאוֹמְרִים בְּלִבָּם מִי שׁוֹמֵעַ, "and they say in their hearts, 'Who hears?'" The whole rest of the psalm is the answer.
Sylvia Plath's late poem "Words" (1965) refuses the assumption baked into "Who hears?" Her speech does not vanish into private air: *Axes / After whose stroke the wood rings, / And the echoes! / Echoes traveling / Off from the center like horses.* The enemies imagine malicious words evaporating unheard; Plath insists the opposite — once struck, words ring outward beyond recall, traveling like the horses they can no longer rein in. The psalmist's God is precisely the One in whose hearing those echoes never die.
**Verse 9**
וְאַתָּה ה׳ תִּשְׂחַק־לָמוֹ; תִּלְעַג לְכָל־גּוֹיִם׃
The pivot, and the punchline to verse 8. The enemies ask "Who hears?" — and God not only hears but laughs: וְאַתָּה ה׳ תִּשְׂחַק־לָמוֹ, "But You, O LORD, laugh at them." The verb is the same one Psalm 2 turns on the rebel kings: יוֹשֵׁב בַּשָּׁמַיִם יִשְׂחָק … יִלְעַג־לָמוֹ, "He who sits in heaven laughs … mocks them" (Ps 2:4) — even down to the archaic suffix לָמוֹ. By echoing the enthronement psalm, this poet recasts Saul's local death-squad as bit-players in the cosmic mutiny of "the nations," and David as the anointed they cannot touch. The two verbs escalate: תִּשְׂחַק is to laugh, תִּלְעַג is to jeer outright. Divine contempt, not divine outrage, is the answer to swaggering atheism.
That laughter is a category of judgment, not comic relief. Jeremiah threatens that Moab וְהָיָה לִשְׂחֹק, "shall become a laughingstock" (Jer 48:39); Job hears that the righteous man "shall laugh at destruction and famine" (Job 5:22) out of fearless superiority. To be laughed at by God is to be reduced to absurdity before being removed.
Milton gave the line its grandest English staging in *Paradise Lost* (Book V), as the Father regards the rebel angels: *thou thy foes / Justly hast in derision, and secure / Laugh'st at thir vain designs and tumults vain.* Milton's syntax delays the laughter to make it feel inevitable and calm — derision from a throne nothing can reach. The psalmist's situation is harder and the achievement larger: he has to call that serene laughter down into a house that is, at this very moment, surrounded.
**Verse 10**
עֻזּוֹ אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמֹרָה; כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי׃
The first half of Refrain B, and the verse modern editors most want to "correct." עֻזּוֹ is "*his* strength" (third person), yet the line addresses "*You*" (second person), אֵלֶיךָ — so some emend עֻזּוֹ to עֻזִּי, "my strength," to match verse 18. The pshat tradition shows the emendation is unnecessary and costly. Rashi: "*his strength* — the strength and might of my enemy, who is stronger than I; toward You I keep watch and wait for You to rescue me from him." Radak and the Metzudat agree: it is Saul's strength. Read this way the mismatch is the meaning — the strength still belongs to the enemy at the *first* refrain, and only migrates into the psalmist's hand at the second (עֻזִּי, v. 18). The "wrong" pronoun is the missing first rung of the dispossession ladder.
אֶשְׁמֹרָה carries the essay's central reversal: the enemy's verb שׁמר, now turned Godward. Ibn Ezra hears an even finer echo — אֶשְׁמֹרָה is like לֵיל שִׁמֻּרִים, the "night of watching," set deliberately כנגד וישמרו את הבית, against their watching of the house. They held a death-watch; he holds a hope-watch, on the same root, through the same night.
The closing מִשְׂגַּבִּי, "my high refuge," is the noun grown from verse 2's petition. The Septuagint renders it ἀντιλήμπτωρ, "supporter, helper," losing the height entirely — a reminder of how much of the Hebrew's spatial imagination (God as inaccessible elevation) drops out in translation.
Beckett's *Waiting for Godot* (1953) stands as the modern photographic negative of this verse. Vladimir's hope has the *structure* of אֶשְׁמֹרָה drained of its content: *Nous nous pendrons demain… À moins que Godot ne vienne. — Et s'il vient? — Nous serons sauvés.* "We'll hang ourselves tomorrow… Unless Godot comes. — And if he comes? — We'll be saved." The psalmist watches *because* God has already proven a refuge (כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי, "*for* God is my refuge"); Beckett keeps the posture of expectant waiting and removes the warrant for it, leaving redemptive vigil suspended over a void. The *kî*, the "for," is everything.
**Verse 11**
אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי יְקַדְּמֵנִי; אֱלֹקִים יַרְאֵנִי בְשֹׁרְרָי׃
A textual crux and a striking image. The Kethib reads חַסְדּוֹ, "*his* faithful love"; the Qere reads חַסְדִּי, "*my* faithful love." Radak lays it out: "written *his* and read *mine*, and the sense is one — the written form points to the One who *does* the kindness, God; the read form to the one who *receives* it, David." The Septuagint backs the harder Kethib (τὸ ἔλεος αὐτοῦ, "his mercy"). The case for the Qere is that it makes a clean inclusio with the psalm's final words, אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי (v. 18). And there is a cluster-wide angle: the michtam-cousin Psalm 57:4 reads יִשְׁלַח אֱלֹקִים חַסְדּוֹ, "God will send *his* faithful love" — the *Kethib* form. So Psalm 59 may be ending by personalizing what the cluster inherited as God's own attribute: *his* chesed at the start of verse 11, *my* chesed by the close. The reading is an inference, but it lets the psalm cast its own vote in the dispute.
The image is unusual and vivid. יְקַדְּמֵנִי (root קדם) means "will come *before* me, meet me in advance" — covenant-love as a vanguard rushing ahead of the speaker into danger. The same personification opens Psalm 89:14, חֶסֶד וֶאֱמֶת יְקַדְּמוּ פָנֶיךָ, "faithful love and truth go before Your face," where the abstractions are God's heralds; here one of them runs point for a single hunted man. The Septuagint preserves the temporal precedence exactly with προφθάνω, "to arrive before."
בְשֹׁרְרָי is itself contested — from שׁרר, "my enemies" (so most), or from שׁוּר, "my watchers, those who spy on me." In a psalm this saturated with watching, the ambiguity is almost too apt: here, to watch *is* to be the enemy. Malbim reads the verse autobiographically — God "showed me" the surveillance through Michal, Saul's own daughter, who warned David in time.
**Verse 12**
אַל־תַּהַרְגֵם פֶּן־יִשְׁכְּחוּ עַמִּי, הֲנִיעֵמוֹ בְחֵילְךָ וְהוֹרִידֵמוֹ; מָגִנֵּנוּ אֲדֹנָי׃
The strangest petition in an imprecatory psalm: *don't* kill them. אַל־תַּהַרְגֵם פֶּן־יִשְׁכְּחוּ עַמִּי, "do not slay them, lest my people forget." Death is too quick, too soon forgotten; what teaches is a living, visible, ongoing punishment. Rashi spells out the mechanics: "because the dead are forgotten — instead, *make them wander from their possessions, that they be poor*; that is the revenge long remembered." This is judgment as public, durable pedagogy.
And it is the sentence of Cain, transposed. When God spared the first murderer from death, He made him נָע וָנָד תִּהְיֶה בָאָרֶץ, "a wanderer and fugitive you shall be on the earth" (Gen 4:12) — preserved precisely so that he would remain a walking sign. Psalm 59 reproduces that Cain pattern: non-execution, enforced wandering on the same root נוע (הֲנִיעֵמוֹ, "make them wander"), and punishment preserved as a visible sign. The prayer is even answered in its own vocabulary — the enemies do wander, יְנִיעוּן, in verse 16. That the poet consciously cites Genesis 4 is an interpretive step, but the convergence of these features is hard to attribute to accident.
The line ends with the psalm's only first-person *plural*: מָגִנֵּנוּ אֲדֹנָי, "*our* shield, my Lord." After a chapter of "me" and "I," a "we" breaks the surface — the עַמִּי, "my people," of the same verse, the community that must not forget. Ibn Ezra hears a barb in מָגֵן, "shield": it gestures, he says, at *Saul the king* — for the king was Israel's shield, and now the true Shield must act against him.
Townes Van Zandt's "Pancho and Lefty" (1972) knows this verse's logic from the inside. The traitor's punishment is not the dramatic death but the long humiliation of outliving the deed: *He only did what he had to do, / And now he's growing old.* That is exactly verse 12's preference — not erasure but a wandering, diminishing survival that keeps the moral legible.
**Verse 13**
חַטַּאת־פִּימוֹ דְּבַר־שְׂפָתֵימוֹ, וְיִלָּכְדוּ בִגְאוֹנָם; וּמֵאָלָה וּמִכַּחַשׁ יְסַפֵּרוּ׃
Poetic justice built into the architecture. The same body parts that gushed swords in verse 8 — בְּפִיהֶם, בְּשִׂפְתוֹתֵיהֶם, "their mouth, their lips" — return here as חַטַּאת־פִּימוֹ דְּבַר־שְׂפָתֵימוֹ, "the sin of their mouth, the word of their lips." What was an attack-instrument becomes the indictment. Malbim presses the irony to its limit: the enemies will *themselves* "confess the sin of their mouth," their own lips testifying to the slander they spoke. The weapon turns state's evidence.
The snare is internal. וְיִלָּכְדוּ בִגְאוֹנָם, "let them be caught in their pride" — not trapped from outside but by their own arrogance, the same self-elevation flagged in verse 2 (מִתְקוֹמְמַי) and voiced in verse 8 ("Who hears?"). Proverbs states the principle flatly: וּבְהַוַּת בֹּגְדִים יִלָּכֵדוּ, "and by their own malice the treacherous are trapped" (Prov 11:6). The final clause, וּמֵאָלָה וּמִכַּחַשׁ יְסַפֵּרוּ — "the curses and lies they tell" — keeps the focus on speech to the end, so the doom that overtakes them is still traced back to their own words.
Al-Mutanabbi, the tenth-century Arab poet whose self-assertion is proverbial, embodies the danger this verse prays for. His most famous boast — الخَيْلُ وَاللّيْلُ وَالبَيْداءُ تَعرِفُني / وَالسّيفُ وَالرّمحُ والقرْطاسُ وَالقَلَمُ, "The steeds, the night, and the desert know me, / as do the sword, the spear, the paper, and the pen" — became, in literary tradition, the very emblem of pride that traps its speaker: his legend reportedly compelled him to fight to his death rather than flee. The psalmist asks God for nothing more than to let arrogant speech do what Al-Mutanabbi's did on its own — close like a snare on the one who utters it.
**Verse 14**
כַּלֵּה בְחֵמָה כַּלֵּה וְאֵינֵמוֹ, וְיֵדְעוּ כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מֹשֵׁל בְּיַעֲקֹב; לְאַפְסֵי הָאָרֶץ סֶלָה׃
The most intense moment, and a real tension with verse 12. There David prayed *don't* kill; here he doubles the imperative of annihilation, כַּלֵּה … כַּלֵּה, "consume… consume," bracketing בְחֵמָה, "in fury," with the verb repeated until וְאֵינֵמוֹ, "and they are no more." How do the two stand together? Ibn Ezra and Radak read them as sequence, not contradiction: כַּלֵּה — "little by little, and at the end, gone." First the long pedagogical wandering of verse 12 (the Cain phase), then final erasure. The mark-of-Cain preserves the witness; the consuming closes the file. The doubled כַּלֵּה also answers the doubled הַצִּילֵנִי that opened the psalm (vv. 2–3) — the poem begins in repeated petition and climaxes in repeated imprecation.
Then the imprecation is immediately subordinated to a teaching purpose: וְיֵדְעוּ כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מֹשֵׁל בְּיַעֲקֹב, "that they may know God rules in Jacob." The participle מֹשֵׁל is ongoing — present, habitual sovereignty — and the preposition is "*in* Jacob," locating that rule within Jacob's sphere, not merely over it. The geographic arc reaches its furthest point, לְאַפְסֵי הָאָרֶץ, "to the ends of the earth" (אֶפֶס here marking the extremity), echoing the universal scope of Psalm 22:28, כָּל־אַפְסֵי־אָרֶץ … וְיִשְׁתַּחֲווּ, "all the ends of the earth … shall bow." David's one besieged house has become a demonstration for the whole world.
Frederick Douglass, in "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" (1852), adopts this verse's conviction that only catastrophic judgment can publish moral truth: "it is not light that is needed, but fire; it is not the gentle shower, but thunder. We need the storm, the whirlwind, and the earthquake." Douglass, like the psalmist, refuses gentleness not from cruelty but from a belief that entrenched injustice yields only to overwhelming pressure — and that the yielding must be *seen*, "to the ends of the earth."
**Verses 15–16**
וְיָשֻׁבוּ לָעֶרֶב, יֶהֱמוּ כַכָּלֶב; וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר׃ הֵמָּה יְנִיעוּן לֶאֱכֹל; אִם־לֹא יִשְׂבְּעוּ וַיָּלִינוּ׃
The dog-refrain returns almost verbatim — but it now arrives into a world God has been declared to rule (v. 14), so the same circling that menaced in verse 7 is already a dead loop. Then verse 16 does what the first cycle never did: it *extends* the image into deflation. The growl becomes mere hunger.
Watch the motion-verbs trace a single downhill plot. The enemies first encircle the city with purpose, וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר (vv. 7, 15) — predatory, military, a siege-circuit. The imprecation re-labels that motion: הֲנִיעֵמוֹ, "make them *wander*" (v. 12). And here the re-labeling comes true: הֵמָּה יְנִיעוּן לֶאֱכֹל, "*they* wander about for food." Purposeful circling has decayed into aimless scavenging; the same dogs, no longer hunting a man but begging at doors. The scene collapses to match — from יֶהֱמוּ, the threatening growl, to וַיָּלִינוּ, the mere fact of spending the night hungry. Isaiah's portrait of insatiable curs supplies the type: וְהַכְּלָבִים עַזֵּי־נֶפֶשׁ לֹא יָדְעוּ שָׂבְעָה, "the dogs are greedy, they never know fullness" (Isa 56:11). The condition אִם־לֹא יִשְׂבְּעוּ, "if they are not satisfied," pictures them as unsated: a hunger with no floor.
The Septuagint reads וַיָּלִינוּ as γογγύζω, "they grumble" — the very verb it uses for Israel's wilderness murmuring (Exod 16). If that reading is right, the enemies are reduced to the posture of complaining Israel in the desert, a quietly devastating demotion.
Manuel Bandeira's "O Bicho" (1947) runs this verse's vector in reverse, and the reversal is the shock. Bandeira watches a creature scavenging in filth — *Catando comida entre os detritos* — and catalogs what it is not: *O bicho não era um cão, / Não era um gato, / Não era um rato. / O bicho, meu Deus, era um homem.* "The creature was not a dog, / not a cat, / not a rat. / The creature, my God, was a man." The psalm weaponizes the scavenging dog to *strip* its enemies of humanity; Bandeira discovers a human being *under* the animal image, turning the same scene from a curse on opponents into an indictment of the society that starved a man down to a beast. The figure is identical; the moral aim is opposite.
**Verse 17**
וַאֲנִי אָשִׁיר עֻזֶּךָ, וַאֲרַנֵּן לַבֹּקֶר חַסְדֶּךָ; כִּי־הָיִיתָ מִשְׂגָּב לִי, וּמָנוֹס בְּיוֹם צַר־לִי׃
The pronoun is the argument. וַאֲנִי, "But *I*" — emphatic, syntactically unnecessary, set hard against the הֵמָּה, "*they*," of verse 16. They wander and whine; I sing. The same contrastive *I* that turns the tide in other laments (Ps 13:6, Ps 52:10) here pivots the whole second half from enemy-deflation to praise. עֻזֶּךָ, "*Your* strength," is the third rung of the עז-ladder — the fierce attribute now firmly God's, one step short of becoming the speaker's own in verse 18.
The temporal turn is theological. The dogs belong to לָעֶרֶב, "the evening" (vv. 7, 15); the psalmist's song belongs to לַבֹּקֶר, "the morning." This is the Psalter's settled grammar of deliverance: בָּעֶרֶב יָלִין בֶּכִי וְלַבֹּקֶר רִנָּה, "weeping may lodge for the evening, but joy comes in the morning" (Ps 30:6) — note that Psalm 30 uses the same root לין, "lodge," that the dogs do in verse 16, so the enemies "lodge" in unsatisfied night while the speaker's joy "comes in the morning." Morning is when חֶסֶד becomes visible.
Most striking is the tense. כִּי־הָיִיתָ מִשְׂגָּב לִי, "for You *have been* a refuge to me" — perfect, completed, while the dogs have only just been circling. The form lets the speaker ground the present crisis in help already proven, and it can also sound like faith spoken in the past tense before the rescue is fully visible. מָנוֹס, "a place to flee," the Metzudat glosses as the image of one running to a friend's house to escape — the very house-imagery of the superscription, redeemed: the house that could not protect him (v. 1) replaced by a God who is himself the refuge.
**Verse 18**
עֻזִּי אֵלֶיךָ אֲזַמֵּרָה; כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי׃
The landing. Three trajectories that have run the length of the psalm all touch down in this one verse, which is why it is the only verse that holds all three. The strength is finally the speaker's: עֻזִּי, "*my* strength" — the four-stage handover (עַזִּים v. 4 → עֻזּוֹ v. 10 → עֻזֶּךָ v. 17 → עֻזִּי v. 18) complete, the enemies' fierceness now owned by the man they hunted, the whole journey accomplished by the move from suffix *-o* to suffix *-i*. The posture is converted: אֲזַמֵּרָה, "I will sing," replaces verse 10's אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "I will watch" — same cohortative shell, the *sh* of surveillance swapped for the *z* of song, the watchman become a singer. And the third trajectory cashes out in three new words absent from verse 10: אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, "God of my faithful love." חֶסֶד, surfacing in verse 11 and named in verse 17, here becomes the psalm's last word — and the certain חַסְדִּי supports the Qere at verse 11 by closing an inclusio around the whole second half. The deepest security the psalm can name turns out not to be עֹז, strength, but חֶסֶד, covenant-loyalty — and the possessive makes it personal, not theological abstraction.
Radak hears the entire modulation as a single thought: "Now I say *His* strength, for the strength belonged to Saul, and therefore I watch and wait for You; and *then* I say *my* strength in place of *His*, and *I sing* in place of *I watch* — for in the time of trouble I watch and hope, and in the time of deliverance I sing."
Solomon Ibn Gabirol's eleventh-century piyyut *Shaḥar Avakeshkha* takes the psalm's morning-and-refuge vocabulary and builds an entire spiritual discipline from it: שַׁחַר אֲבַקֶּשְׁךָ, צוּרִי וּמִשְׂגַּבִּי, / אֶעֱרֹךְ לְפָנֶיךָ שַׁחְרִי וְגַם עַרְבִּי — "At dawn I seek You, my Rock and my *misgab* (haven); / I set before You my morning and my evening too." Ibn Gabirol lifts מִשְׂגַּבִּי, "my haven," straight from the refrain and weaves in the dawn of verse 17 — but he turns the psalm's *singular* emergency gratitude, the morning song of one night survived, into a *repeatable* daily liturgy, morning and evening, forever. What David sang once after escaping a watched house, Ibn Gabirol resolves to sing at every sunrise. The psalm's morning becomes a discipline — which is, in the end, only the consonant-swap made permanent: a life lived in the key of *zamar* rather than *shamar*.---
