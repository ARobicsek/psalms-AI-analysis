# Commentary on Psalm 60
---
## Introduction
Psalm 60 opens with a victory parade and then spends thirteen verses in mourning. Its heading is one of the longest historical notes in the Psalter, and it records a triumph: Joab returning from the north to cut down twelve thousand Edomites in the Valley of Salt. Turn to verse 3 and the celebration curdles instantly — אֱלֹקִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ פְרַצְתָּנוּ, "O God, You have rejected us, You have made a breach in us." A reader expecting a war-song gets a war-*wound*. Something has gone wrong between the title and the text, and the psalm seems to know it.
The key to that friction is a single word in the superscription that appears nowhere else as a psalm-heading: לְלַמֵּד, "to teach." This is not a tune-name or a cultic term; it is a stated purpose. And there is exactly one other Davidic composition in the entire Bible carrying that purpose. In 2 Samuel 1:18, David's lament over Saul and Jonathan — dead on Mount Gilboa, the worst defeat of his career — is ordered taught: וַיֹּאמֶר לְלַמֵּד בְּנֵי־יְהוּדָה קָשֶׁת, "and he said to teach the sons of Judah *the Bow*." Both of David's "teaching" pieces are laments after catastrophe. And both can be linked to the consonants קשת in or beside the heading — because Psalm 60:6 turns on one of the most discussed textual cruxes in the book: the Masoretic Text (the received Hebrew, "MT") reads מִפְּנֵי קֹשֶׁט, "because of truth," while the Septuagint (the ancient Greek, "LXX") reads קֶשֶׁת, "bow" (τόξον) — a difference of a single consonant, ט versus ת. The ancient "bow" reading suddenly looks far less arbitrary: it can be heard sitting squarely inside a Davidic tradition of *taught songs of the Bow*. Whatever the poet meant, the psalm wears the dress of David's defeat-laments.
That is the argument of this essay: Psalm 60 is a *taught* lament — a defeat deliberately kept on the curriculum — and everything in its craft is built to make a broken faith both honest and bearable.
**The floor plan.** The psalm moves in five movements. The superscription (vv. 1–2) sets the historical anchor and the didactic rubric. A complaint (vv. 3–5) makes God the grammatical subject of every disaster. A transition (vv. 6–7) speaks of a banner and asks for the rescue of "those You love." Then the pivot: a quoted divine oracle (vv. 8–10) in which God claims the whole land and reduces the enemy nations to household objects. A renewed lament (vv. 11–12) collapses back into "You have rejected us," and a closing resolution (vv. 13–14) sets worthless human help against confidence in God. The oracle of dominion is thus sandwiched between two identical cries of rejection — and that sandwich is the poem's whole theology in miniature.
**Destruction and the measuring-line.** Watch what the verbs do to the ground. In the complaint, God dismantles the earth: הִרְעַשְׁתָּה אֶרֶץ פְּצַמְתָּהּ, "You have made the land quake, You have torn it open," followed by the plea רְפָה שְׁבָרֶיהָ כִי־מָטָה, "mend its fractures, for it is collapsing." פְּצַמְתָּהּ is a true hapax legomenon — a word occurring only here in the Bible — and the poet reached for it where common verbs for splitting (בָּקַע, פָּצַח) lay ready to hand. The rarity itself marks the rupture as unique, off the standard list. The land has become a wounded body, and the verb asked of God is not "rebuild" but רְפָה, "heal."
Now jump to the oracle. The same earth is reassembled, and the vocabulary is the surveyor's: אֲחַלְּקָה שְׁכֶם וְעֵמֶק סֻכּוֹת אֲמַדֵּד, "I will divide up Shechem and measure the Valley of Sukkoth," followed by the drumbeat of possession — לִי גִלְעָד וְלִי מְנַשֶּׁה, "Gilead is mine, and Manasseh is mine." The God who fissured the ground takes up the measuring-line. The two panels answer each other gesture for gesture: tearing open is met by dividing up, tottering by measuring. This is why the plea רְפָה שְׁבָרֶיהָ and the oracle's cartography are the same act seen from opposite ends — the oracle *is* the requested healing, delivered as a land-grant.
**Edom, three times.** If the whole psalm's victory-versus-defeat tension feels abstract, the poet pins it to one name. Edom is *defeated* in the title (v. 2), *owned* in the oracle — עַל־אֱדוֹם אַשְׁלִיךְ נַעֲלִי, "on Edom I will cast my shoe," the ancient legal gesture of seizing property (cf. Ruth 4:7) — and then, two verses later, *unreachable*: מִי נָחַנִי עַד־אֱדוֹם, "Who will lead me to Edom?" The same name is conquered, possessed, and inaccessible within nine verses. The argument of the entire psalm can be tracked through that one orbiting word.
**The grammar of an unfinished faith.** Here is the craft that makes the psalm honest. Sort God's verbs by their tense and a timeline appears. Every act of destruction in vv. 3–5 is a perfect — completed, done: זְנַחְתָּנוּ, פְרַצְתָּנוּ, הִרְעַשְׁתָּה, הִשְׁקִיתָנוּ. The oracle of dominion in v. 8 is cohortative — willed, intended, *I-resolve-to*: אֶעְלֹזָה אֲחַלְּקָה אֲמַדֵּד. And the closing triumph is imperfect — not yet: וְהוּא יָבוּס צָרֵינוּ, "He *will* trample our foes." Already-broken, then promised, then not-yet-won. The conjugation system itself carries the plot. This is precisely why the psalm can end "confident" while Edom stands unreached (v. 11) and the cry of rejection is still ringing (v. 12): the grammar never once claims an accomplished victory. The open-endedness is not a failure of nerve; it is wired into the verbs.
You can hear the same restraint in the imagery the poet *declines* to complete. Israel is made to drink יַיִן תַּרְעֵלָה, "wine of reeling" (v. 5) — the cup that the prophets will eventually force on the nations (Isa 51:17, Jer 25:15, Lam 4:21). The expected reversal would be to hand that cup to Moab and Edom. The poet never does. The enemies are humiliated as washbasin and footstool, but the cup is never passed. The unreversed cup matches the unfinished triumph: both are left grammatically open.
The reader who finishes Psalm 60 should never again hear its last line as a shout of victory. It is a verb in the imperfect — a triumph still bending toward the future, taught to a community that has learned to sing its confidence and its defeat in the very same breath. That is the lesson of לְלַמֵּד: faith is not the denial of the breach, but the decision to keep singing across it.

#### Full psalm
Psalm 60 is not recited in its entirety in the fixed daily, Shabbat, or festival liturgy of any of the major rites. Its accusatory second-person verbs and its embedded conquest-oracle make it an awkward congregational prayer — one does not easily lead a minyan in "O God, You have rejected us." Its afterlife is therefore a matter of *fragments*: individual verses prized loose and re-set in new frames.
#### Key verses
The most widely used line is verse 7, לְמַעַן יֵחָלְצוּן יְדִידֶיךָ הוֹשִׁיעָה יְמִינְךָ וַעֲנֵנִי, "That those whom You love might be rescued, deliver with Your right hand and answer me." In Nusach Sefard (the Hasidic rite) and among Edot HaMizrach (Mizrachi/Sephardic communities), it is appended to the post-Amidah supplications, immediately after the chain עֲשֵׂה לְמַעַן שְׁמָךְ... עֲשֵׂה לְמַעַן יְמִינָךְ, "Act for the sake of Your name... act for the sake of Your right hand." The placement is no accident of piety: the preceding plea climaxes on יְמִינָךְ, and verse 7 supplies the matching הוֹשִׁיעָה יְמִינְךָ — the line is chained in by the catchword "right hand." A second motive operates alongside it: the singular Qere וַעֲנֵנִי, "answer *me*" (against the Ketiv "answer us," and preserved identically in Ps 108:7), lets a national battlefield cry become a single worshipper's private petition. Many Sephardic siddurim formalize this, offering verse 7 as the model "name-verse" (סגולה) for those whose names begin with ל and end with י.
Verse 13, הָבָה לָּנוּ עֶזְרָת מִצָּר, "Grant us aid against the foe," surfaces in the Edot HaMizrach Yom Kippur Neilah liturgy, woven into a penitential piyyut at the "closing of the gates" — where "help against the foe" is heard no longer as military rescue but as a cry against sin and exile.
The banner phrase נֵס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס (v. 6) is reread in two penitential settings: in the Ashkenazi Rosh Hashanah Musaf it becomes a cosmic standard gathering "upper and lower realms" for judgment; in the Nusach Sefard selichot for the fast of the twentieth of Sivan it is applied to martyrs — נֵס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס מִפְּנֵי קֹשֶׁט, the slain themselves raised as a "banner because of truth." That last reading quietly settles the v. 6 crux in favor of the Masoretic קֹשֶׁט, "truth."

## Verse-by-Verse Commentary
**Verse 1**
לַמְנַצֵּחַ עַל־שׁוּשַׁן עֵדוּת; מִכְתָּם לְדָוִד לְלַמֵּד׃
Three opaque technical terms are crammed into one heading, and they were already obscure to the ancient translators. שׁוּשַׁן עֵדוּת — literally "lily of testimony" — is most likely a melody-name; Ibn Ezra and Meiri take שׁוּשַׁן as the name of a musical instrument and עֵדוּת in the sense of עֲדִי, "ornament." The LXX, reading a שׁנה-root, renders the whole thing ἀλλοιόω, "to alter/change" — a completely different word, proof the phrase was already a riddle by the second century BCE.
מִכְתָּם appears only six times, clustered in Psalms 16 and 56–60, which suggests it tagged a discrete collection. The root is disputed: either כֶּתֶם, "gold" (so Rashi: a דָּבָר חָמוּד, "a precious thing," like כֶּתֶם פָּז, "finest gold," in Song 5:11), or כתב, "to write." The LXX's στηλογραφία, "stele-inscription," is the more startling option — it imagines the psalm as a public monument, the genre of an ANE victory-stele, which fits a composition anchored to a military campaign. The Talmud (Sotah 10b), preserved by Rashi, hears instead an acronym: David was מָךְ וְתָם, "humble and perfect," even as king.
The decisive word is לְלַמֵּד, "to teach," unique among psalm-headings. As argued in the essay, its only meaningful parallel is David's Gilboa lament in 2 Samuel 1:18, also a defeat taught "to the sons of Judah." Metzudat David captures the intent plainly: עָשׂוּי לְלַמֵּד דַּעַת אֶת הָעָם אֵיךְ הַכֹּל בָּא בְהַשְׁגָּחָה, "made to teach the people that everything comes by [divine] providence." The heading does not merely record a prayer; it assigns homework.
**Verse 2**
בְּהַצּוֹתוֹ אֶת־אֲרַם נַהֲרַיִם וְאֶת־אֲרַם צוֹבָה; וַיָּשָׁב יוֹאָב וַיַּךְ אֶת־אֱדוֹם בְּגֵיא־מֶלַח שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר אָלֶף׃
The historical note is a small text-critical knot. בְּהַצּוֹתוֹ Rashi derives from נצה, "to strive," comparing בְּהַצּוֹתָם עַל ה', "when they quarreled with the LORD" (Num 26:9) — hence "when he fought." The LXX reads ἐμπυρίζω, "to set on fire," implying a fire-root (יצת/צות) and a different scene: burning Aram, not battling it.
Three canonical sources assign the Valley of Salt victory to different commanders, and two different casualty totals appear: 2 Samuel 8:13 says David and eighteen thousand, 1 Chronicles 18:12 says Abishai and eighteen thousand, and here Joab and twelve thousand. Traditional harmonizations distribute the victory among all three rather than choosing among them, but the very fact that the tradition floats among several names may suggest the psalm had detached from one fixed event, exactly what a recyclable teaching-text would do. בְּגֵיא־מֶלַח, "the Valley of Salt," is a recurring Edomite battlefield (2 Kgs 14:7; 2 Chr 25:11, where Amaziah fights there again), and salt carries the curse-register of desolation (Deut 29:22). A victory site, but one shadowed by sterility.
The deeper point is the friction itself: the heading celebrates a *win* the poem then *mourns*. Meiri preserves the midrash that turns the gap into a legal drama: the Arameans confronted Joab with the ancient Laban–Jacob treaty (Gen 31:52, "this heap is a witness" — note עֵדוּת again), and only a Sanhedrin ruling nullifying the pact let the campaign proceed. Whatever its historicity, the tradition felt the same seam we feel and tried to suture it.
**Verse 3**
אֱלֹקִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ פְרַצְתָּנוּ; אָנַפְתָּ תְּשׁוֹבֵב לָנוּ׃
Three staccato accusations, then one abrupt imperative — the emotional logic of lament compressed into a single line. The verbs make God the agent: זְנַחְתָּנוּ is no temporary absence but deliberate repudiation, the same verb that appears in Korahite and Asaphite laments (לָמָה אֱלֹקִים זָנַחְתָּ לָנֶצַח, "Why, O God, have You rejected us forever?" Ps 74:1).
פְרַצְתָּנוּ, "You have breached us," is the more shocking word, because breach-language normally describes God *defending* Israel. At Baal-Perazim, פָּרַץ ה׳ אֶת־אֹיְבַי לְפָנַי כְּפֶרֶץ מָיִם, "the LORD has burst through my enemies before me like a bursting of waters" (2 Sam 5:20). Here the same verb is turned around: God breaks through *us*, like a besieger breaching the wall. Metzudat David draws the picture exactly — כְּעִיר שֶׁנִּפְרַץ חוֹמָתָהּ, "like a city whose wall has been breached," through which attackers pour from every side.
Note the sound. זְנַחְתָּ֣נוּ and פְרַצְתָּ֑נוּ both close on the suffix **-tānû**, and הִשְׁקִיתָ֗נוּ in v. 5 follows suit — a grammatical rhyme that makes every accusation toll on the same note, "(you did this) to-us." It is the acoustic signature of the complaint, and when זְנַחְתָּנוּ returns in v. 12 the ear, not just the eye, recognizes the bracket closing.
The closing imperative reverses everything: תְּשׁוֹבֵב, "restore us," is a rare intensive form of שוב, asking not for a simple return but a thorough reconstitution. The LXX softens it to οἰκτείρω, "have pity." Malbim hears the whole verse as the logic of consoled wrath: כָּלִיתָ חֲרוֹן אַפְּךָ עָלֵינוּ, "You have spent Your anger upon us" — and a spent anger can now turn to healing.
**Verse 4**
הִרְעַשְׁתָּה אֶרֶץ פְּצַמְתָּהּ; רְפָה שְׁבָרֶיהָ כִי־מָטָה׃
The verse pivots mid-line from earthquake to surgery. הִרְעַשְׁתָּה, "You made it quake," is followed by פְּצַמְתָּהּ, a hapax legomenon whose meaning ("split, cleave open") is reconstructed from Arabic and Aramaic cognates; Rashi confesses he relies on Dunash and Targumic evidence (וּפְצִים for "windows cut open," Jer 22:14). The poet had בָּקַע and פָּצַח available — both common, both meaning "split open," the latter ringing through Isaiah's joy-songs (פִּצְחוּ רִנָּה, "break out in song," Isa 52:9). Choosing the obscure word marks this rupture as categorically unlike any other.
Then the register shifts to medicine. רְפָה שְׁבָרֶיהָ, "heal its fractures," treats the land as an injured body — and רפה is a medical verb, where one would expect a builder's חַזֵּק or בָּנָה. The image belongs to a wider biblical habit of medicalizing national disaster: מִכַּף־רֶגֶל וְעַד־רֹאשׁ אֵין־בּוֹ מְתֹם, "from sole to head there is no soundness in it, only wound and bruise" (Isa 1:6). And the same God both wounds and heals: כִּי אַעֲלֶה אֲרֻכָה לָךְ וּמִמַּכּוֹתַיִךְ אֶרְפָּאֵךְ, "for I will bring you healing and heal you of your wounds" (Jer 30:17). That is the verse's rhetorical engine — You broke it, so You must mend it.
The final clause כִי־מָטָה, "for it is collapsing," supplies the reason for the plea: the land is in the process of falling, not yet fallen. The urgency is real. Du Fu's great poem of the An Lushan rebellion, *Spring Prospect* (757 CE), holds the opposite vision: 國破山河在, "the nation is shattered, but mountains and rivers remain." Du Fu can console himself that the *land* outlasts the *state*. The psalmist is denied even that comfort — for him the dirt itself gives way, מָטָה, and the political and cosmic collapse are one.
**Verse 5**
הִרְאִיתָ עַמְּךָ קָשָׁה; הִשְׁקִיתָנוּ יַיִן תַּרְעֵלָה׃
הִרְאִיתָ, "You made [Your people] *see*," adds a psychological turn of the knife: Israel did not merely suffer, it was forced to *watch* itself suffer. קָשָׁה, "hard things," may carry an Egyptian undertone — the same root describes the עֲבֹדָה קָשָׁה, the "hard bondage" of Exodus 1:14 — so defeat is framed faintly as a re-enslavement.
The image that follows, יַיִן תַּרְעֵלָה, "wine of reeling," is the psalm's signature metaphor and belongs to the prophetic cup-of-wrath tradition: כּוֹס הַתַּרְעֵלָה, "the cup of reeling," that Jerusalem must drain (Isa 51:17), or the cup Jeremiah is told to make the nations drink, וְהִשְׁקִיתָה אֹתוֹ אֶת־כָּל־הַגּוֹיִם, "and you shall make all the nations drink it" (Jer 25:15). The sting of Psalm 60 is that Israel drinks *first*. Rashi catches the specifically *numbing* effect — תַּרְעֵלָה, he says, is wine הָאוֹטֵם אֶת הַלֵּב, "that stops up the heart" — and the LXX agrees, rendering κατάνυξις, "stupor," not mere staggering. This is not drunken merriment but anesthesia: the incapacitation of a community that can no longer stand upright.
Here is a crucial restraint to notice across the whole psalm: the prophets eventually *transfer* this cup — גַּם־עָלַיִךְ תַּעֲבָר־כּוֹס, "to you also the cup shall pass," to Edom (Lam 4:21). Psalm 60 humiliates its enemies in vv. 8–10 with washbasin and sandal, but it never hands them the cup. The reversal is left undone — of a piece with the not-yet triumph the poem will end on.
Wilfred Owen's *Dulce et Decorum Est* (published 1920, written from the trenches of the Great War) secularizes precisely this image. His gas-blinded soldiers are "Drunk with fatigue; deaf even to the hoots / Of tired, outstripped Five-Nines that dropped behind." Owen, like the psalmist, reaches for intoxication to name the *loss of bodily autonomy* under overwhelming force — but where Owen's men stagger from sheer human abandonment, the psalmist's reeling is poured out by an active divine hand. The causative verb הִשְׁקִיתָנוּ keeps God as bartender, and that is the harder theology: the cup is not an accident of war but a draft administered.
**Verse 6**
נָתַתָּה לִּירֵאֶיךָ נֵּס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס; מִפְּנֵי קֹשֶׁט סֶלָה׃
The phrase נֵּס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס pairs a noun with a cognate verb — "a banner, to be banner-raised." The banner is the holy-war signal of divine presence: at the Amalek battle Moses names his altar ה׳ נִסִּי, "the LORD is my banner" (Exod 17:15), and Isaiah promises the root of Jesse עֹמֵד לְנֵס עַמִּים, "standing as a banner for the peoples" (Isa 11:10). The poet's choice of a *banner* rather than a *shield* is pointed: a banner does not protect, it gathers. After the disorientation of the wine, the community needs first to be re-assembled before it can be led.
The next phrase splits the verse's whole temper: MT gives a theological banner raised "for truth"; LXX gives a signal for flight from enemy archers, φεύγειν ἀπὸ προσώπου τόξου, "to flee from before the bow" — taking קֹשֶׁט as קֶשֶׁת, "bow," a one-consonant difference (ט/ת). Symmachus and the Syriac back the MT; Rashi reads קֹשֶׁט as "to vindicate Your standards." The Selah falls precisely on this fork, marking it as a structural threshold. As the essay argued, the "bow" reading is not careless: it would place the verse inside the Davidic tradition of a *taught song of the Bow* (2 Sam 1:18).
**Verse 7**
לְמַעַן יֵחָלְצוּן יְדִידֶיךָ; הוֹשִׁיעָה יְמִינְךָ וַעֲנֵנִי׃
Across three verses the addressed community is renamed with rising intimacy: עַמְּךָ, "Your people" (v. 5), then יְרֵאֶיךָ, "those who fear You" (v. 6), now יְדִידֶיךָ, "Your beloved." ידיד is love-poetry vocabulary: Solomon is named יְדִידְיָה, "beloved of the LORD" (2 Sam 12:25), and Benjamin is יְדִיד ה׳, "the LORD's beloved" (Deut 33:12). The petition narrows from nation to intimates exactly as it turns from complaint to request — and it is this most intimate verse, not the earlier two, that the liturgy later lifts out for personal prayer.
The verse also carries a textual seam preserved across both Psalm 60 and its twin Psalm 108: the Ketiv (written) form וְעַנֵּנוּ, "answer *us*," against the Qere (read) וַעֲנֵנִי, "answer *me*." The surrounding grammar is plural and communal (יֵחָלְצוּן, "may they be rescued; יְדִידֶיךָ, Your beloved ones"), so the singular "me" punctures the corporate voice — a royal "I" breaking through, or a community's adaptation of an older royal petition. The seam is not a flaw to be smoothed; the later tradition exploited it. Precisely because וַעֲנֵנִי is singular, the verse could be detached as an *individual's* plea at the close of the Amidah, where "answer me" becomes one worshipper's own word.
**Verse 8**
אֱלֹקִים דִּבֶּר בְּקׇדְשׁוֹ; אֶעְלֹזָה אֲחַלְּקָה שְׁכֶם וְעֵמֶק סֻכּוֹת אֲמַדֵּד׃
The pivot. God speaks, and the verbs change tense and mood: from the perfects of disaster to the cohortatives of resolve — אֶעְלֹזָה, "let me exult, אֲחַלְּקָה, let me divide, אֲמַדֵּד, let me measure." Who speaks? The ambiguity is productive and probably deliberate: it may be God exulting over the land-grant, or the king seamlessly voicing God's claim as his own — a royal-divine fusion in which the speaker's "I" and God's "I" cannot be cleanly pried apart.
בְּקׇדְשׁוֹ is itself two-faced: "in His sanctuary" (the oracle delivered in the temple) or "by His holiness" (the oath sworn on God's own being). The second is the more binding — compare אַחַת נִשְׁבַּעְתִּי בְקָדְשִׁי, "Once I have sworn by My holiness" (Ps 89:36). If the speech is grounded in God's holiness rather than merely His house, it is irrevocable, and the whole confidence-structure of the psalm rests on it.
The content is the surveyor's work. אֲחַלְּקָה, "I will allot," is the vocabulary of Joshua's land-distribution; אֲמַדֵּד, "I will measure," makes God the divine cartographer. And this is the answering panel to the destruction of v. 4. There the land was torn open (פְּצַמְתָּהּ) and tottering (מָטָה); here it is divided and measured. The God who fissured the ground takes up the measuring-line — so the oracle is not a change of subject from the complaint but its resolution, the requested healing arriving as a deed of property. שְׁכֶם (west of the Jordan) and סֻכּוֹת (east of it) thus mark both sides of the river, so the claim reaches across the land rather than naming a single point.
**Verse 9**
לִי גִלְעָד וְלִי מְנַשֶּׁה; וְאֶפְרַיִם מָעוֹז רֹאשִׁי יְהוּדָה מְחֹקְקִי׃
The drumbeat of possession — לִי... וְלִי, "mine... and mine" — claims the contested northern and Transjordanian tribes first, the very territories most exposed to the Aramean campaigns. Then two tribes are mapped onto the body of a warrior-king: אֶפְרַיִם מָעוֹז רֹאשִׁי, "Ephraim is the fortress of my head," i.e. a helmet, and יְהוּדָה מְחֹקְקִי, "Judah my scepter." Ephraim, the dominant northern fighting tribe, supplies the armor; Judah supplies the rule. North gives the muscle, south gives the dynasty — a compact theology of the united monarchy.
מְחֹקְקִי is the loaded word. The root חקק means "to engrave, decree," and the word carries the Jacobite blessing directly: לֹא־יָסוּר שֵׁבֶט מִיהוּדָה וּמְחֹקֵק מִבֵּין רַגְלָיו, "the scepter shall not depart from Judah, nor the ruler's-staff from between his feet" (Gen 49:10). The LXX amplifies to βασιλεύς μου, "my king," reading full royal authority.
**Verse 10**
מוֹאָב סִיר רַחְצִי; עַל־אֱדוֹם אַשְׁלִיךְ נַעֲלִי; עָלַי פְּלֶשֶׁת הִתְרוֹעָעִי׃
Three enemies, three escalating postures of contempt. Moab is סִיר רַחְצִי, "my washbasin" — Rashi and Radak specify the basin into which one pours dirty foot-water, the lowliest household vessel, fit for a menial servant. A nation once famous for its pride (cf. Jer 48) is reduced to a wash-pot. The LXX preserves a wildly divergent reading here, λέβης τῆς ἐλπίδος μου, "cauldron of my hope" — inexplicable from the MT and probably reflecting a different Hebrew text; the humiliation reading is the one the context demands.
עַל־אֱדוֹם אַשְׁלִיךְ נַעֲלִי, "on Edom I cast my sandal," is a legal gesture, not just an insult. The transfer of a sandal sealed property conveyance in Israel — שָׁלַף אִישׁ נַעֲלוֹ וְנָתַן לְרֵעֵהוּ, "a man drew off his sandal and gave it to his neighbor" (Ruth 4:7) — and Nuzi contracts and Egyptian royal boasts ("I have set my sandal on Nubia") use the same imagery for seizing land. To throw the shoe *onto* Edom is to file a deed.
הִתְרוֹעָעִי addressed to Philistia is genuinely ambiguous: "shout *for* me" (a vassal's acclamation) or "shout *against* me" (an enemy war-cry, received with contempt). Either way Philistia is demoted — and the LXX forecloses the ambiguity toward submission, ὑπετάγησαν, "they have been subjected."
This verse has had a startling cultural afterlife. Spurgeon preached a whole sermon, "Moab Is My Washpot," allegorizing the basin as the unregenerate world; and Stephen Fry borrowed the phrase as the title of his memoir *Moab Is My Washpot*, applying the image of being a repository for "defiled water" to the humiliations of adolescence — testimony to the strange durability of an ancient domestic-military metaphor.
**Verse 11**
מִי יֹבִלֵנִי עִיר מָצוֹר; מִי נָחַנִי עַד־אֱדוֹם׃
The oracle's swagger collapses into a human question. One verse after God declares "on Edom I cast my shoe," the speaker asks מִי נָחַנִי עַד־אֱדוֹם, "Who will lead me to Edom?" — the very territory just claimed is humanly inaccessible. עִיר מָצוֹר, "the fortified city" (from צור, "to besiege"), Radak and most moderns identify as Edom's rock-hewn capital, Sela/Petra (cf. 2 Kgs 14:7). The irony is the psalm's whole crisis in miniature: the oracle says owned, experience says unreachable.
The two verbs are not perfectly aligned. יֹבִלֵנִי looks forward, "who will bring me," while נָחַנִי can be heard as "who has led me" or, under the pull of the parallelism, "who will guide me." Either way, the double מִי, "who?", is a rhetorical question whose only possible answer is God — which is exactly what sets up the accusation in the next verse.
Edom has now appeared in all three of the psalm's voices: defeated in the title, owned in the oracle, unreachable here. The friction the whole poem dramatizes is concentrated in this one circling name.
**Verse 12**
הֲלֹא־אַתָּה אֱלֹקִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ; וְלֹא־תֵצֵא אֱלֹקִים בְּצִבְקוֹתֵינוּ׃
The verbatim return of זְנַחְתָּנוּ from v. 3 snaps the bracket shut. The oracle of dominion (vv. 8–10) is now sealed inside two identical statements of rejection — the promise nested within unresolved complaint, so that no cheap triumphalism can escape. The interrogative הֲלֹא, "Is it not [the case that]," expects a yes: this is rhetorical affirmation, not real doubt. The ear, too, registers the closure — זְנַחְתָּנוּ tolls again on the same **-tānû** note that rang three times in vv. 3–5.
The second colon delivers a buried irony through its lone military noun. The poem's only occurrence of army-language is human: בְּצִבְקוֹתֵינוּ, "with our armies" — the very company God will not march with. וְלֹא־תֵצֵא, "You do not go out," negates the holy-war premise that the divine warrior accompanies Israel's armies (Deut 20; Josh 5–6). The point lands bitterly: the armies are present, but God is not with them.
**Verse 13**
הָבָה־לָּנוּ עֶזְרָת מִצָּר; וְשָׁוְא תְּשׁוּעַת אָדָם׃
הָבָה, "Grant!", is an archaic, urgent imperative of יהב, the form used in old narrative ("come, let us..." Gen 11:3); it is weightier than the ordinary תֵּן, a primal demand laid before God. The petition's logic turns on its second colon. וְשָׁוְא תְּשׁוּעַת אָדָם, "and worthless is the deliverance of man," fronts שָׁוְא for emphasis — *worthless* is the first thing said. And שָׁוְא is a loaded word: it is the term of the Third Commandment, לֹא תִשָּׂא אֶת־שֵׁם־ה׳ אֱלֹקֶיךָ לַשָּׁוְא, "you shall not take the name of the LORD your God in vain" (Exod 20:7). Human deliverance is filed in the same drawer as false oaths and empty idols — the strongest possible devaluation, and the grounds (not the cause of despair) for the petition.
There is a quiet sound-binding at the psalm's close. The unreachable stronghold of v. 11, עִיר מָצוֹר (mā-**TSŌR**), and the distress of this verse, עֶזְרָת מִצָּר (mits-**TSĀR**), share the consonants מ-צ-ר and the field of constriction (though from distinct roots, צור and צרר). The besieging fortress the speaker cannot enter and the besieging distress he cannot escape rhyme — tightening the move from "who will bring me to the bastion?" to "give us aid against the constraint."
The verse's theology echoes Psalm 127:1: אִם־ה׳ לֹא־יִבְנֶה בַיִת שָׁוְא עָמְלוּ בוֹנָיו בּוֹ, "Unless the LORD builds the house, its builders labor in vain (שָׁוְא)." Same word, same logic — human effort without God is empty.
**Verse 14**
בֵּאלֹקִים נַעֲשֶׂה־חָיִל; וְהוּא יָבוּס צָרֵינוּ׃
The communal "we" reasserts itself — נַעֲשֶׂה, "we shall do" — answering the isolated "me" of v. 11. בֵּאלֹקִים, "with/by God," uses the ב of accompaniment: human valor and divine action distributed, not human passivity. עָשָׂה חַיִל is an old idiom of triumph, ringing through the blessing on Ruth, וַעֲשֵׂה־חַיִל בְּאֶפְרָתָה, "do valiantly in Ephrathah" (Ruth 4:11), and through the victory-shout of Psalm 118:15, יְמִין ה׳ עֹשָׂה חָיִל, "the right hand of the LORD does valiantly."
The decisive word is the last verb's tense. יָבוּס, "He will trample," is imperfect — future, not accomplished. The LXX flattens its violence to ἐξουδενώσει, "He will bring to nothing," losing the physical tread; the Hebrew בוס is bodily, the trampling of an enemy in the mud (cf. כְּטִיט חוּצוֹת, "like mud of the streets," Mic 7:10). But it has not happened yet.
This is where the whole psalm's grammar comes to rest. Sort God's verbs across the poem and a timeline emerges: the destruction is in the perfect (done — זְנַחְתָּנוּ, פְרַצְתָּנוּ), the oracle in the cohortative (willed — אֲחַלְּקָה, אֲמַדֵּד), and the triumph here in the imperfect (not-yet — יָבוּס). The psalm ends confident, but it never claims the victory as finished. Edom is still unreached (v. 11), the rejection is still on record (v. 12), and the foe is trampled only in a future tense. Ibn Ezra reads the line as prophecy — שֶׁהוּא יְנַצֵּחַ בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה, "that He will be victorious in the end." That is the lesson of a *taught* lament: faith said in the imperfect, a triumph still bending toward the future, sung by a people who have learned to carry their confidence and their defeat in a single breath.---
