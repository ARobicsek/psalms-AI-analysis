# Verse-by-Verse Commentary: Psalm 126

---

**Verse 1**

שִׁ֗יר הַֽמַּ֫עֲל֥וֹת בְּשׁ֣וּב יְ֭הוָה אֶת־שִׁיבַ֣ת צִיּ֑וֹן; הָ֝יִ֗ינוּ כְּחֹלְמִֽים׃

The opening colon introduces us to one of the most debated textual cruxes in the Psalter. The phrase בְּשׁוּב יְהוָה אֶת־שִׁיבַת צִיּוֹן (*b'shuv Adonai et-shivat Tzion*) contains a deliberate temporal ambiguity that has generated two millennia of competing interpretations. The form בְּשׁוּב can function as an infinitive construct ("when...returned"), suggesting either completed past action or anticipated future event. Rashi, reading messianically, rendered it as prophetic future perfect: "When God *will* return us... we *will have been* like dreamers"—making the current exile seem ephemeral compared to the eschatological restoration. Ibn Ezra, committed to grammatical *peshat*, insisted on the historical past: the verse describes the actual post-538 BCE return from Babylon.

The noun שִׁיבַת itself presents a kethiv/qere variation that shapes the entire trajectory of interpretation. The Masoretic vocalization reads שִׁיבַת (*shivat*), a construct form from the root ש-ו-ב (to return), yielding "the return of Zion." Yet many manuscripts preserve שְׁבוּת (*sh'vut*), from ש-ב-ה (to capture), meaning "captivity." The idiomatic phrase שׁוּב שְׁבוּת (*shuv sh'vut*), attested across biblical literature (Deuteronomy 30:3, Jeremiah 29:14, Ezekiel 29:14), functions as a technical term for "restore the fortunes" or "reverse the fate"—a broader theological concept than mere geographical relocation. Modern Zionism's appropriation favored the *shivat* reading to emphasize physical *aliyah*, but the original semantic range encompasses both repatriation and cosmic restoration of Israel's destiny.

The second colon introduces the notorious simile הָיִינוּ כְּחֹלְמִים (*hayinu k'cholmim*), standardly translated "we were like dreamers." The lexical debate here is particularly consequential. The consensus derivation from חָלַם I (to dream) evokes the surreality of sudden deliverance—the Joseph and Daniel narratives where dreams mediate divine revelation. The restoration was so miraculous it defied waking consciousness. But N.H. Tur-Sinai proposed חָלַם II, a cognate with Syriac and Aramaic roots meaning "to be strong, healthy, recovered," as in Job 39:4 (בְּנֵיהֶם יַחְלְמוּ, "their young ones are in good liking"). This yields "we were like those restored to health"—the nation in exile was *diseased*, and return to Zion constituted national *healing*. This pathological reading creates elegant parallelism with verse 4's hydrological imagery: as the desert wadis are revitalized by sudden floods, the people are revitalized by homecoming. The "dream" interpretation dominated liturgical use (note the psalm's recitation before *Birkat HaMazon* on Shabbat, where it contrasts with weekday Psalm 137's mourning), but the "healing" reading deserves scholarly reconsideration for its somatic-political theology.

**Verse 2**

אָ֤ז יִמָּלֵ֪א שְׂח֡וֹק פִּינוּ֮ וּלְשׁוֹנֵ֢נוּ רִ֫נָּ֥ה; אָ֭ז יֹאמְר֣וּ בַגּוֹיִ֑ם הִגְדִּ֥יל יְ֝הוָ֗ה לַעֲשׂ֥וֹת עִם־אֵֽלֶּה׃

The double אָז (*az*, "then") creates powerful anaphoric parallelism, establishing consequentiality: "THEN... THEN..." The rhetoric insists these results are inevitable, not contingent. The first colon employs striking somatic imagery—שְׂחוֹק (*s'choq*, laughter) and רִנָּה (*rinah*, jubilant song) do not merely describe emotions but physically *fill* (יִמָּלֵא, intensive Niphal) the mouth and tongue. This is not polite joy but overwhelming, involuntary bodily expression. Targum Jonathan amplified this: "Then our mouth will be filled with praise." The Meiri noted the progression from internal affect (laughter's visceral origin) to external articulation (tongue's song), suggesting redemption transforms private relief into public testimony.

The second colon introduces the nations' (בַגּוֹיִם) astonished declaration: הִגְדִּיל יְהוָה לַעֲשׂוֹת עִם־אֵלֶּה (*higdil Adonai la'asot im-eleh*, "The LORD has done great things for them"). This Hiphil perfect of גָּדַל (to magnify) with the infinitive construct לַעֲשׂוֹת creates an idiomatic intensification: "greatly did great things." The preposition עִם governs אֵלֶּה ("these/them"), maintaining third-person distance. The gentiles are external observers bearing witness to Israel's transformation. This international recognition motif appears prominently in Deutero-Isaiah (Isaiah 52:10: "All the ends of the earth shall see the salvation of our God"; compare the nations' acknowledgment in Ezekiel 36:23). The tradition embedded this verse in the liturgy *Nishmat Kol Chai*, the climactic prayer of *Pesukei D'Zimra* on Shabbat: וְאִלּוּ פִינוּ מָלֵא שִׁירָה כַּיָּם, וּלְשׁוֹנֵנוּ רִנָּה כַּהֲמוֹן גַּלָּיו ("Were our mouths full of song as the sea, and our tongues with joyous song as the multitude of its waves..."). The liturgical redeployment transforms the psalm's national restoration into a universal declaration of humanity's inability to adequately praise God—a profound theological repurposing from particular to cosmic scope.

Concordance analysis reveals הִגְדִּיל יְהוָה לַעֲשׂוֹת appears only here and in Joel 2:21 (לַאֲדָמָה... כִּי־הִגְדִּיל יְהוָה לַעֲשׂוֹת, "Fear not, O soil... for the LORD has wrought great deeds"), linking redemptive reversal to agricultural fecundity—a connection the psalm will exploit in verses 5-6.

**Verse 3**

הִגְדִּ֣יל יְ֭הוָה לַעֲשׂ֥וֹת עִמָּ֗נוּ; הָיִ֥ינוּ שְׂמֵחִֽים׃

This verse executes a rhetorically stunning maneuver through verbatim repetition with pronominal transformation. The entire phrase הִגְדִּיל יְהוָה לַעֲשׂוֹת is repeated exactly, but the prepositional object shifts from עִם־אֵלֶּה (*im-eleh*, "with them," verse 2) to עִמָּנוּ (*imanu*, "with us"). Israel appropriates the nations' testimony, converting external validation into internal affirmation. This is not mere echo but theological claiming: what the nations observe, Israel *owns*. The grammatical shift from third-person to first-person plural marks the pivot from spectacle to possession, from witnessed event to lived reality.

The second colon—terse, almost laconic—states הָיִינוּ שְׂמֵחִים (*hayinu s'meḥim*, "we were joyful"). The perfect tense verb creates temporal alignment with verse 1's הָיִינוּ כְּחֹלְמִים ("we were like dreamers"), forming an inclusio around verse 2's gentile witness. Yet this joy is already past-tensed, retrospective. The psalm narrates completed deliverance but—crucially—verse 4 will shatter this celebratory closure with an urgent imperative petition, revealing the restoration as incomplete. Radak captured this brilliantly: "The historical return happened, but we are *still* in some form of captivity—hence verse 4's cry 'Restore our fortunes!'" The joy of verses 1-3 functions as proleptic memory: Israel rehearses the *future* restoration as *past* fact, following prophetic convention (the "prophetic perfect"). This grammatical sleight-of-hand allows the psalm to exist simultaneously in multiple temporal registers.

Malbim, with his signature grammatical maximalism, argued that the repetition of הִגְדִּיל יְהוָה signals two distinct theological moments: the nations' recognition (v. 2) acknowledges the *extrinsic* wonder of political reversal, while Israel's appropriation (v. 3) affirms the *intrinsic* covenantal fidelity. The gentiles marvel at the geopolitical improbability; Israel rejoices in the theological inevitability. The same words carry different epistemic weight depending on the speaker's covenantal position. This reading transforms apparent redundancy into dialogical depth.

**Verse 4**

שׁוּבָ֣ה יְ֭הוָה אֶת־שְׁבִיתֵ֑נוּ; כַּאֲפִיקִ֥ים בַּנֶּֽגֶב׃

The psalm executes a jarring generic pivot. After three verses of perfective narration (past-tense testimony), the urgent imperative שׁוּבָה (*shuvah*, "Restore!") in the Hiphil shatters the celebratory mood. This is not request but demand, grammatically marked by the lengthened suffix -ah indicating intensified entreaty. The sudden shift from indicative to imperative, from praise to petition, reveals verses 1-3 as either incomplete past or envisioned future—perhaps both simultaneously. The restoration celebrated was real but insufficient; the return occurred but the redemption remains partial. This explains the psalm's liturgical power: it speaks to every generation experiencing the "already but not yet" tension of redemptive history.

The phrase אֶת־שְׁבִיתֵנוּ (*et-sh'vitenu*) repeats the shivat/sh'vut crux from verse 1, now with first-person plural suffix ("our captivity/restoration"). The kethiv preserves שְׁבוּתֵנוּ (*sh'vutenu*), from the root ש-ב-ה (captive); the qere reads שְׁבִיתֵנוּ (*sh'vitenu*), creating an inclusio with the opening. Both readings converge semantically on the idiom "restore fortunes," but the phonetic variance preserves interpretive plurality. The Masoretic tradition's tolerance for this textual doubleness (kethiv/qere) suggests recognition that the psalm operates in overlapping semantic fields: physical return, political liberation, economic restoration, and cosmic *tikkun* are inseparable dimensions of one divine act.

The second colon introduces the psalm's most vivid geographical-meteorological simile: כַּאֲפִיקִים בַּנֶּגֶב (*ka'afikim baNegev*, "like watercourses in the Negev"). The noun אֲפִיקִים (*afikim*) denotes the seasonal riverbeds (wadis) that cut through the southern desert. The Negev experiences extreme aridity for most of the year; its wadis are bone-dry channels. But winter rains in the Judean highlands trigger sudden, violent flash floods that rush through these empty beds, instantly transforming the landscape. The metaphor is not gradual improvement but *sudden, overwhelming, life-giving inundation*. The psalmist is not praying for incremental progress but invoking the image of miraculous deluge. Radak explained: "The Negev is perpetually dry (נָגוּב), thirsting for water. When streams (אֲפִיקִים) suddenly flow through it, it is a great wonder and goodness—thus the return from exile." The hydrological realia encode a theology of abrupt divine intervention.

This imagery resonates with Ugaritic mythology, where the storm god Baal's return from death causes the wadis to flow with fertility (KTU 1.6 iii: "The heavens rain down oil, the wadis run with honey"). The psalm has demythologized this motif: the "flowing" is not the resurrection of a dying-rising deity but YHWH's sovereign restoration of Israel. The *afikim* became so central to the psalm's reception that the Talmud (Ta'anit 8b) declared: "Great is the day of rain gathering, like the day of the ingathering of exiles, as it says, 'Restore our fortunes, O LORD, like watercourses in the Negev.'" The rabbis collapsed the simile into identity: rain *is* redemption.

**Verse 5**

הַזֹּרְעִ֥ים בְּדִמְעָ֗ה; בְּרִנָּ֥ה יִקְצֹֽרוּ׃

The psalm modulates from petition to proverbial wisdom. The genre shift is marked by the universalizing plural participle הַזֹּרְעִים (*hazor'im*, "those who sow"), moving from Israel's specific historical plea to a generalized agricultural principle. This is synthetic parallelism at its most elegant: the first colon states the premise (sowing in tears), the second colon completes with the consequence (reaping in joy). The syntax creates inevitability—the imperfect יִקְצֹרוּ (*yiktzoru*, "they will reap") functions as logical necessity, not mere temporal future.

The phrase בְּדִמְעָה (*b'dim'ah*, "in/with tears") has been consistently sentimentalized in modern reception, but the economic context of post-exilic Judah (reflected in Haggai 1:6: "You have sown much, and harvested little") reveals existential anxiety. Seed corn was the most precious commodity in a subsistence economy—the grain a family could eat today to survive but must instead bury for a hoped-for harvest. In the drought-prone highlands, every planting was a life-risking gamble. If the rains failed (compare verse 4's *afikim* imagery), the seed died and the family starved. The "tears" are not poetic melancholy but the dread of the farmer carrying his seed bag (מֶשֶׁךְ־הַזָּרַע, verse 6) to the field, knowing he buries his children's sustenance in uncertain soil. Radak captured this: "The seed is precious in arid land, and the poor man carrying it goes weeping from fear that it might be delayed and not sprout due to the land's dryness."

The antithetical pairing דִמְעָה / רִנָּה (*dim'ah* / *rinah*, "tears / jubilant song") structures the wisdom saying. The noun רִנָּה appears four times in the psalm (vv. 2, 5, 6 twice), creating sonic cohesion. Its semantic range spans from the cry of supplication (Psalm 17:1, 61:2) to the shout of victory (Psalm 47:2)—here unambiguously the latter. The Septuagint rendered it ἐν ἀγαλλιάσει (*en agalliasei*, "in exultation"), and Christian liturgy absorbed the verse into funeral rites, reinterpreting the "tears" as earthly suffering and "reaping" as eschatological reward (compare 1 Corinthians 15:42: "It is sown in dishonor; it is raised in glory").

The Ugaritic Baal Cycle provides striking (if demythologized) parallel. After Mot (Death) devours Baal, the goddess Anat "seizes him, cleaves him with a sword, winnows him in a sieve, grinds him with millstones, and sows him in the fields" (KTU 1.6 ii). Following this violent "sowing," El dreams: "In a dream (b'chalam), the heavens rain down oil, the wadis run with honey" (KTU 1.6 iii). The structural parallels—dream, sowing, flowing wadis—suggest the psalm has appropriated and monotheized Canaanite agricultural mythology. The "sowing in tears" is no longer ritualized mourning for a dying vegetation deity but the historical experience of the covenant people trusting YHWH with their survival.

**Verse 6**

הָ֘ל֤וֹךְ יֵלֵ֨ךְ ׀ וּבָכֹה֮ נֹשֵׂ֢א מֶשֶׁךְ־הַ֫זָּ֥רַע; בֹּא־יָבֹ֥א בְרִנָּ֑ה; נֹ֝שֵׂ֗א אֲלֻמֹּתָֽיו׃

The psalm culminates with intensified verbal forms that convert the proverbial generalization (v. 5) into vivid narrative particularity. The opening construction הָלוֹךְ יֵלֵךְ (*halokh yelekh*, "going he goes") employs the infinitive absolute + finite verb pattern, indicating continuous, durative action—the long, weary trudge to the field. This is not a single act but a repetitive, grinding labor. The addition of וּבָכֹה (*uvakho*, "and weeping") completes the participial picture: the sower is *simultaneously* walking and weeping, the tears accompanying every step. The shift from plural (v. 5) to singular individualizes the experience, making it intimate and immediate. This is not "those who sow" (generalized wisdom) but "*he* who sows" (embodied person).

The phrase נֹשֵׂא מֶשֶׁךְ־הַזָּרַע (*nosei meshekh-hazara*, "bearing the measure of seed") contains the lexical knot מֶשֶׁךְ (*meshekh*). BDB offers two possibilities: (1) "a drawing, drawing up, a trail" (from the root מ-ש-כ, to draw/pull), as in Job 28:18 where it denotes the arduous "drawing up" of precious wisdom; or (2) the specific container/bag holding seed. If "trail," the image is the careful line of seed being dropped behind the sower—precious, limited, not abundant. If "seed-bag," the emphasis is on the *weight* being carried. Either reading stresses scarcity and preciousness. Ibn Ezra glossed: מֶשֶׁךְ הַזֶּרַע—"the vessel in which the sower carries the seed." Malbim interpreted it as the *exact measure* of seed needed, suggesting there was no surplus—every grain counted. The Syriac cognate (related to "draw/pull") reinforces the sense of laborious conveyance.

The antithetical second half employs the same intensive construction: בֹּא־יָבֹא (*bo-yavo*, "coming he comes") with the same durative force, but now בְרִנָּה (*b'rinah*, "with jubilant song") replaces וּבָכֹה (*uvakho*, "and weeping"). The sonic near-rhyme (בָכֹה / יָבֹא, *vakho* / *yavo*) reinforces the structural parallelism. The transformation is complete: the one who departed *weeping* returns *singing*, the one who carried the *measure of seed* (מֶשֶׁךְ־הַזָּרַע) now carries *his sheaves* (אֲלֻמֹּתָיו, *alumotav*).

The noun אֲלֻמָּה (*alumah*, "sheaf") appears 8 times in Scripture, most famously in Joseph's dream (Genesis 37:7: וְהִנֵּה קָמָה אֲלֻמָּתִי, "my sheaf stood upright"), where it symbolizes Joseph's future authority over his brothers. Here it signifies the multiplication of the seed: a single seed-bag becomes multiple sheaves, a quantitative contrast embodying divine blessing. The Targum expanded: "bearing the bundles of his harvest" (נָסֵב אַלְמוּתָהּ דִקְצֵירֵהּ). The Meiri connected this to Hosea 10:12 (זִרְעוּ לָכֶם לִצְדָקָה קִצְרוּ לְפִי־חֶסֶד, "Sow for yourselves righteousness, reap according to kindness"), moralizing the agriculture: the "seed" is the performance of *mitzvot* in hardship, the "sheaves" are the eschatological reward. The liturgical tradition embedded this verse in *Tikkun Leah* (the midnight service lamenting the Temple's destruction in Edot HaMizrach rite), framing midnight tears as the "sowing" that will yield messianic "sheaves."

The chiastic structure of verse 6 is exquisite:  
A — הָלוֹךְ יֵלֵךְ (going forth)  
   B — וּבָכֹה (weeping)  
      C — נֹשֵׂא מֶשֶׁךְ־הַזָּרַע (bearing seed)  
   B' — בְרִנָּה (singing)  
      C' — נֹשֵׂא אֲלֻמֹּתָיו (bearing sheaves)  
A' — בֹּא־יָבֹא (coming back)

The center is the *act of carrying* (נֹשֵׂא, *nosei*), which remains constant, but the *content* transforms: from scarcity to abundance, from investment to return, from risk to reward. The journey motif (going/coming) frames the entire experience, reinforcing that restoration is not static arrival but dynamic process—a pilgrimage, befitting the *Shir HaMa'alot* collection.

The Christian reception history transformed this agricultural metaphor into missionary theology. The American gospel hymn "Bringing in the Sheaves" (Knowles Shaw, 1874) reinterpreted the "seed" as the Gospel Word and the "sheaves" as converted souls: "Sowing in the morning, sowing seeds of kindness, / Sowing in the noontide and the dewy eve; / Waiting for the harvest, and the time of reaping, / We shall come rejoicing, bringing in the sheaves." This drastically shifted the metaphor from Israel's national restoration to individual evangelical labor, universalizing the psalm into 19th-century Protestant industriousness. Johannes Brahms' *Ein deutsches Requiem* (1868) set verses 5-6 to music in Movement I, using the metaphor to comfort the bereaved—the tears of grief (sowing) will yield eschatological consolation (reaping). The orchestration shifts from dark, trudging strings (the sower's weeping) to bright, flowing arpeggios (the reaper's song), physically enacting the transformation in sound.

The psalm thus concludes not with petition answered but with *assurance of process*: the sowing has occurred (whether in Babylonian exile, post-exilic poverty, or ongoing diaspora), the tears have been shed, and the divine economy *guarantees* the harvest. The final word אֲלֻמֹּתָיו (*alumotav*, "his sheaves") dangles grammatically, an object without a verb of arrival—the reaping is proleptically present, grammatically assumed, theologically certain but temporally open. The psalm refuses closure, leaving the reader/worshiper perpetually positioned between sowing and reaping, tears and songs, exile and restoration—the liturgical genius that has sustained its recitation for twenty-six centuries.
