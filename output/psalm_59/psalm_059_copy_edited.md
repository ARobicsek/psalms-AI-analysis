# Commentary on Psalm 59
---
## Introduction
Twice in this psalm almost the same sentence appears: יָשׁוּבוּ לָעֶרֶב יֶהֱמוּ כַכָּלֶב וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר, "They come back each evening, growling like dogs, circling the city" (vv. 7, 15). Only a prefixed waw and a slight vowel shift distinguish the second occurrence. Yet the first time these dogs are assassins prowling a house, and the second time they are strays whimpering for scraps. Between the two refrains, nothing has happened on earth — Saul's men are still outside, the door is still watched. What has happened is that God has laughed. And the repeated words, returning almost unaltered, now carry the opposite weight.
That is the puzzle this psalm is built to produce, and the key to it is grammar. Psalm 59 sorts its three actors — the enemies, the speaker, and God — by the kind of verb each is allowed to use, and the sorting *is* the verdict. The enemies are conjugated almost entirely in the imperfect, the open, never-finished aspect of Hebrew: they ambush, run, array, return, growl, circle, gush, wander — and arrive at last at אִם־לֹא יִשְׂבְּעוּ, "if they are not satisfied" (v. 16), an unfinished verb for an unfinished hunger. They act endlessly and conclude nothing. The speaker, by contrast, resolves into willed first-person forms — אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "I will keep watch" (v. 10), and finally אֲזַמֵּרָה, "I will sing" (v. 18). He wills one thing and reaches it. Their grammar can never close; his does.
**The architecture.** The psalm is a double cycle bolted to an opening cry. Verses 2–6 are a fourfold petition that swells, by its end, from "my enemies" to "all the nations." Then the machine runs twice. Cycle one (vv. 7–10): the dog refrain, the enemies' arrogant speech, God's laughter, and a confidence refrain — עֻזּוֹ אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "his strength — toward You I keep watch." Cycle two (vv. 15–18): the same dog refrain, the enemies' degradation into scavengers, the speaker's morning song, and the confidence refrain transformed — עֻזִּי אֵלֶיךָ אֲזַמֵּרָה, "my strength, to You I sing." Between the cycles sits an imprecatory core (vv. 11–14), bracketed by the two סֶלָה markers that fall, both times, precisely on the line where the threat goes maximally public: "to bring all nations to account" (v. 6) and "to the ends of the earth" (v. 14).
**One word fought over by three parties.** The superscription plants the psalm's governing pun before the prayer begins. Saul's men וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת־הַבַּיִת, "watched the house" (v. 1) — hostile surveillance, lethal intent. The very same root שמר returns in the speaker's mouth at v. 10: אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "toward You I keep watch." Same verb, reversed vector. And in vv. 5, 10–11 three watching-words run in three directions at once: the speaker begs God וּרְאֵה, "and see!" (v. 5), then thanks Him that יַרְאֵנִי בְשֹׁרְרָי, "He lets me look on my watchers" (v. 11) — שׁוֹרְרַי, the "eyers," the same predatory gaze of the superscription now thrown back exposed. The man who was watched to be killed ends by watching, with God's permission, the watchers themselves.
The same dispossession happens to power. The enemies' defining trait is עַזִּים, "fierce ones" (v. 4) — from the root עזז, strength. The psalm then confiscates that root and re-gifts it: עֻזּוֹ, "his strength" (v. 10), עֻזֶּךָ, "Your strength" (v. 17), and finally עֻזִּי, "my strength" (v. 18). Fierceness, the enemies' signature, is revealed as a thing on loan; true strength belongs to God and is handed to the man they hunted. The four occurrences trace an argument: the bully's "fierce" ends as the singer's "mine."
**The hinge is laughter.** The enemies' creed is a single causal clause: כִּי־מִי שֹׁמֵעַ, "for who hears?" (v. 8). This is the Psalter's stock voice of practical atheism — God is real but blind, so do as you please (compare Ps. 10:11; 94:7). The psalm answers it with brutal economy. Not only does God hear; God laughs — וְאַתָּה ה׳ תִּשְׂחַק־לָמוֹ, "but You, O LORD, laugh at them" (v. 9). The verb is lifted straight from the enthronement psalm: יוֹשֵׁב בַּשָּׁמַיִם יִשְׂחָק, "He who sits in heaven laughs" (Ps. 2:4), where the nations and their kings conspire against the LORD's anointed. By borrowing that laughter, Psalm 59 quietly files Saul's surveillance squad under the same heading as a global rebellion: petty thugs become a specimen of cosmic insolence, and the cosmic answer fits the petty crime. And the enemies' one denying "for" (כִּי, v. 8) is rebutted by three affirming ones — כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי (v. 10), כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מֹשֵׁל בְּיַעֲקֹב (v. 14), כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי again (v. 18): "for God is my haven… for God rules Jacob… for God is my haven." One "for" denies that anyone is listening; three "for"s seal the refrain.
This is why the choice of *dog* matters so much. The poet could have reached for the lion of Psalm 22 — אַרְיֵה טֹרֵף וְשֹׁאֵג, "a tearing, roaring lion" (Ps. 22:14) — or the ravening wolf of Jacob's blessing. He wants neither. A lion would dignify the enemies; a wolf would naturalize them into noble predators of the wild. The dog of the ancient city is something else: a shameless, noisy, low-status scavenger, impure and contemptible. The simile grants the enemies real menace and strips them of all honor in the same breath, and it sets up the reversal: the threat that growls in verse 7 is, by verse 16, a beast that יְנִיעוּן לֶאֱכֹל, "wanders for food," and whines when unfed.
There is a recognizable human experience under all this craft — the particular vertigo of being hunted by people who feel entirely safe, who do their work in the open because they are sure no one with power is watching. The psalm does not deny the danger; the dogs are still at the door at the end. What it changes is the grammar of the danger. The enemies are frozen in an aspect that cannot complete; the speaker is granted the verbs that can.
And so the single thing to carry away: the most repeated lines in this psalm are spoken about creatures who can never finish a sentence, and the last word of the whole poem is חַסְדִּי, "my faithfulness." The enemies return forever and arrive nowhere; the man they hunted wills one thing, watching turned to song, and reaches it.

#### Full psalm
Psalm 59 is recited in full as the fifth of the ten psalms of the *Tikkun HaKlali* ("General Remedy") instituted by Rabbi Naḥman of Breslov — a sequence (Pss. 16, 32, 41, 42, 59, 77, 90, 105, 137, 150) used in the Breslov (Hasidic) world as a regimen of spiritual repair. The reading allegorizes the psalm's grammar of siege: Saul's lawless force becomes the assault of intrusive, impure thought, and David's survival becomes the holy boldness (עַזּוּת דִּקְדֻשָּׁה) that overcomes the brazenness (עַזּוּת פָּנִים) of the enemy — a reading that, with some justice, hears the psalm's own contest over the root עז.
#### Key verses
Verses 6 and 17 enter the prayer book through *Perek Shirah*, the "Chapter of Song," a devotional anthology printed in many siddurim of the Hasidic (Nusach Sefard) rite and recited privately, most often after the morning service. There the lines are voiced not as one man's lament but as a cosmic chorus: וְאַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹקִים צְבָקוֹת אֱלֹקֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל הָקִיצָה לִפְקֹד כָּל־הַגּוֹיִם, "You, O LORD God of hosts, God of Israel, bestir Yourself to bring all nations to account," and וַאֲנִי אָשִׁיר עֻזֶּךָ, "But I will sing of Your strength." The placement turns a private escape into the natural speech of creation acknowledging God's rule — exactly the move the psalm itself makes when it widens from a watched house to the ends of the earth.
#### Phrases
The psalm's closing refrain surfaces in the Ashkenazic machzor for Rosh Hashanah, woven into a piyyut in the ḥazzan's repetition of the Shaḥarit Amidah, within the expanded Kedushah of the third blessing. The cantor, pleading for worthy speech before the Judge, prays: עֻזִּי אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמְרָה לְסַעֲדִי… כִּי אַתָּה מִשְׂגַּבִּי אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, "my strength, to You I keep watch for my support… for You are my haven, my faithful God." The poet has fused the psalm's *opening* refrain-verb אֶשְׁמֹרָה with its *closing* phrase מִשְׂגַּבִּי אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, recasting one fugitive's trust as the representative voice of a congregation "pouring out the heart like water" on the Day of Judgment. The natural (pshat) sense — a hunted individual's confidence — is preserved but enlarged: the personal "my haven" becomes the shield of a community standing in judgment.

## Verse-by-Verse Commentary
**Verse 1**
לַמְנַצֵּחַ אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת לְדָוִד מִכְתָּם; בִּשְׁלֹחַ שָׁאוּל; וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת־הַבַּיִת לַהֲמִיתוֹ׃
The title does in miniature what the psalm does at length. אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת, "Do not destroy," is most likely a tune-name (the same cue heads Pss. 57, 58, 75), but Rashi hears it as a prayer: David "called the psalm by this name because he was close to dying and being destroyed, and he begged mercy in the matter." Either way the irony is structural — the heading says "do not destroy" while the men outside intend לַהֲמִיתוֹ, "to kill him." The third-person suffix on לַהֲמִיתוֹ ("to kill *him*," not "me") tells us this is narrated framing, not yet the prayer-voice: the psalm quotes a moment in which David, in a third-person-described crisis, will speak in the first person.
מִכְתָּם is an unsolved word, clustering in six Davidic psalms (16, 56–60). BDB simply concedes "meaning unknown"; Klein offers "epigram." The LXX guessed στηλογραφία, "inscription on a stele" — which, if right, lends a quiet irony: an ephemeral night of persecution recorded as if cut in stone.
וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת־הַבַּיִת, "they watched the house," plants the root שמר that the whole psalm will pull inside out. Here it is lethal surveillance; at v. 10 it becomes the speaker's faithful vigil toward God. Rashi and Meiri tie the scene to 1 Samuel 19:11–12, where Michal lowers David through the window — וַתֹּרֶד מִיכַל אֶת־דָּוִד בְּעַד הַחַלּוֹן, "Michal let David down through the window" — so that the watched house is the very house he escapes.
The terror of being surveilled in one's own home found an uncanny modern echo in Lu Xun's *Diary of a Madman* (狂人日记, 1918), written as China's old order collapsed into paranoia. His narrator notes: 不然，那趙家的狗，何以看我兩眼呢, "Otherwise, why did Zhao's dog look at me twice?" The psalmist and the madman share the architecture of the watched man — but where Lu Xun's speaker has lost the frame and cannot say who is hunting him, the psalmist names his assassins and their sender exactly. His clarity is itself a kind of defense; the dread is real, but it is not formless.
**Verse 2**
הַצִּילֵנִי מֵאֹיְבַי אֱלֹקָי; מִמִּתְקוֹמְמַי תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי׃
Two petitions, the first an emergency snatch, the second a quiet planting of the psalm's keyword. הַצִּילֵנִי, from נצל, means to tear away from a grasp — extraction, not yet ongoing safety. מִמִּתְקוֹמְמַי, "from those who rise against me," is a rare and intense form: where simple קָמַי would mean "my attackers," this reflexive-intensive shape (only a handful of times in the Psalter) implies organized, self-driven, sustained insurrection — which fits a state operation rather than a brawl.
תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי, "set me high / make me inaccessible," from שגב, is the structurally crucial word, slipped in unobtrusively as an ordinary plea. It will harden into the noun מִשְׂגַּבִּי, "my haven," that seals both refrains (vv. 10, 18). Radak catches its spatial logic precisely: כְּאִלּוּ תְּשִׂימֵנִי בְּמָקוֹם רָם וְנִשְׂגָּב שֶׁלֹּא יוּכַל אֵלַי, "as if You set me in a high, lofty place he cannot reach." The verb answers the threat's geometry: the enemies move horizontally — encircling, closing in — and the prayer asks for the vertical, to be lifted out of reach. The LXX's λυτρόω, "redeem," loses the height entirely; the Hebrew is architectural, not commercial.
**Verse 3**
הַצִּילֵנִי מִפֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן; וּמֵאַנְשֵׁי דָמִים הוֹשִׁיעֵנִי׃
The repeated הַצִּילֵנִי heading both this verse and the last creates an insistent hammering before the threat is even described. The enemy-labels escalate from the relational ("my enemies," v. 2) to the characterological: פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן, "doers of wickedness," and אַנְשֵׁי דָמִים, "men of bloods." The plural דָמִים — bloods, not blood — is technical: in Hebrew it signals violent, guilty bloodshed, the standing of a habitual killer (compare Ps. 5:7, אִישׁ־דָּמִים וּמִרְמָה יְתָעֵב ה׳, "the LORD abhors the man of bloods and deceit"). Malbim assigns the two phrases to two enemy types — slanderers "in secret" against פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן, would-be murderers against אַנְשֵׁי דָמִים. The word אָוֶן is the first of a thread (vv. 3, 5, 6) that binds the enemies' guilt to the speaker's innocence: they *do* אָוֶן; he is *without* the near-sounding עָוֺן.
הוֹשִׁיעֵנִי, the fourth petition verb, closes the opening barrage. It is broader than הַצִּילֵנִי — not emergency extraction but full deliverance-with-wellbeing, the root that generates יְשׁוּעָה, "salvation." The LXX's σῶσον preserves that wider range.
**Verse 4**
כִּי הִנֵּה אָרְבוּ לְנַפְשִׁי; יָגוּרוּ עָלַי עַזִּים; לֹא־פִשְׁעִי וְלֹא־חַטָּאתִי ה׳׃
Here the governing grammar of the enemies shows its hand. Of their two verbs, one is a completed perfect — אָרְבוּ, "they have set an ambush" — and one is an open imperfect, יָגוּרוּ, "they gather / assail." The trap is *laid* (perfect) but the kill never *lands* (imperfect): the ambush is permanently cocked and never fired. The root of יָגוּרוּ is itself ambiguous — גור can mean "sojourn," "assail," or "dread"; Rashi reads לָנִים בְּבֵיתִי לְשָׁמְרֵנִי, "they lodge against me to guard me," hearing the assault as a kind of grim houseguest, while the LXX's ἐπιτίθημι, "attack," takes the hostile sense.
עַזִּים, "fierce ones," is the first sounding of the עז root that the psalm will confiscate from the enemies and redistribute through God to the speaker (vv. 10, 17, 18). Their identifying quality will turn out to be borrowed property.
The last two phrases open the formal plea of innocence: לֹא־פִשְׁעִי וְלֹא־חַטָּאתִי, "no rebellion of mine, no sin of mine." פֶּשַׁע (covenant-breach) and חַטָּאָה (missing the mark) are two of the three great Hebrew guilt-terms; the third arrives in v. 5. Note חַטָּאתִי especially — the very noun the speaker disowns here will be re-pinned to the enemies in v. 13 as חַטַּאת־פִּימוֹ, "the sin of *their* mouth." What he sheds, they wear.
**Verse 5**
בְּלִי־עָוֺן יְרֻצוּן וְיִכּוֹנָנוּ; עוּרָה לִקְרָאתִי וּרְאֵה׃
עָוֺן, "guilt / iniquity," the third term, completes the legal triad. Across vv. 4–5 the speaker negates all three categories of Hebrew moral failure — פֶּשַׁע, חַטָּאָה, עָוֺן — a courtroom declaration of total blamelessness, rare to find compressed so tightly without overlap. And here the ear hears something the eye misses: עָוֺן (with ʿayin, "guilt") is a near-homophone of אָוֶן (with aleph, "wickedness," vv. 3, 6). The enemies are twice "men of אָוֶן"; the speaker is once "without עָוֺן" — two different words whose sounds collide, so that the contrast between the wicked and the guiltless is carried by the acoustics, invisible to anyone hearing the verse aloud without seeing the consonants.
Their two verbs are again imperfect and unresolved: יְרֻצוּן, "they run" (with an emphatic energic nun, urgency built into the form), and וְיִכּוֹנָנוּ, "they array themselves" — frantic motion plus deliberate military positioning, neither arriving anywhere.
Then the audacity: עוּרָה לִקְרָאתִי וּרְאֵה, "rouse Yourself to meet me, and see!" Ibn Ezra catches the design — וְטַעַם עוּרָה כְּנֶגֶד וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת־הַבַּיִת, "the sense of 'rouse Yourself' answers 'they watched the house'": human watchfulness is countered by a demand that God wake and watch. Malbim is bolder still, placing God at the table where Saul hurled the spear: עֲמֹד עַל צַד הַמַּגְבִּיל לִקְרָאתִי, "stand on the side opposite me." The verb וּרְאֵה, "and see," joins the gaze-war: the speaker who was watched now asks God to look.
**Verse 6**
וְאַתָּה ה׳־אֱלֹקִים צְבָקוֹת אֱלֹקֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל; הָקִיצָה לִפְקֹד כָּל־הַגּוֹיִם; אַל־תָּחֹן כָּל־בֹּגְדֵי אָוֶן סֶלָה׃
The first of two וְאַתָּה, "but You," pivots — and the psalm's most elaborate address. Four divine titles stack where elsewhere one or two suffice: YHWH, Elohim, Tsevaot, Elohei-Israel. The maximal invocation matches the sudden maximal scope, for here the prayer leaps from Saul's squad to כָּל־הַגּוֹיִם, "all the nations." Ibn Ezra reads the double authority into the names: God of hosts above (angels) and below (Israel). The verb הָקִיצָה, "wake up," is a *second* rousing-imperative after v. 5's עוּרָה — two different roots (קיץ here, עור there) for waking God in consecutive verses. The pairing is attested elsewhere only a handful of times: הָעִירָה וְהָקִיצָה לְמִשְׁפָּטִי, "rouse and wake to my cause" (Ps. 35:23). The escalation is the point: "come meet me" (v. 5) becomes "judge all nations" (v. 6).
לִפְקֹד is juridical — "bring to account," from a verb (פקד) that spans visiting, mustering, and punishing. בֹּגְדֵי אָוֶן, "traitors of wickedness," adds covenant-betrayal (בגד) to the charge: these are not merely wicked but faithless to bonds of trust. The סֶלָה falls exactly on the line of cosmic judgment — and will fall again, in v. 14, on the matching line of cosmic recognition, the two pauses framing the imprecatory core.
The covenantal logic — God of Israel, awaken against the nations — was turned devastatingly inside out by Kadya Molodowsky, writing in Yiddish in 1945 in the immediate shadow of the Holocaust. Her *El Khanun* opens: קֵל חַנּוּן, קלײַב אויס אַן אַנדער פֿאָלק, "O God of Mercy, choose another people. We are tired of dying and of being dead. We have no more prayers." The psalmist invokes the martial God of Israel to wake and punish the nations on his people's behalf; Molodowsky, three thousand years downstream, asks that God revoke the chosenness itself, the protective bond having become an unbearable wound. The same covenant address, the same God of hosts — and a petition that has learned to fear being heard.
**Verse 7**
יָשׁוּבוּ לָעֶרֶב; יֶהֱמוּ כַכָּלֶב; וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר׃
The refrain that anchors the whole poem, and a small marvel of sound. The transcription shows three consecutive u-vowel endings — yā-**SHŪ**-vū… ye-he-**MŪ**… wiy-**SŌ**-və-vū — the verse itself half-howling. Every verb is imperfect and cyclical: יָשׁוּבוּ, "they keep returning; יֶהֱמוּ, they growl; וִיסוֹבְבוּ, they circle." The enemies are caught in a loop the grammar will not let them exit.
יֶהֱמוּ is a surprising choice. The root המה usually describes rushing water — הָמוּ מֵימָיו, "its waters roar" (Ps. 46:4) — or a tumultuous crowd, not a dog. Applied here it lends an overwhelming, irrational, chaotic noise; Malbim notes the enemies are *worse* than dogs, since "dogs only bark, they do not speak." The LXX read a different word entirely — λιμώσσω, "they are famished" — importing the hunger that the MT withholds until v. 16. If the LXX is right, the dogs are starving from the start; if the MT is right (and the near-verbatim refrain argues it is), the transformation from howling prowler to whining scavenger is the psalm's own work, not a fact stated twice.
The choice of *dog* over lion or wolf is deliberate degradation. Abishai's contempt for Shimei — לָמָּה יְקַלֵּל הַכֶּלֶב הַמֵּת הַזֶּה, "why should this dead dog curse my lord the king?" (2 Sam. 16:9) — shows the register: the dog is the insult animal, impure and shameless.
Baudelaire, in his 1869 prose poem "Les Bons Chiens" (*Le Spleen de Paris*), set out deliberately to redeem exactly this creature: Je chante le chien crotté, le chien pauvre, le chien sans domicile, le chien flâneur, "I sing the dirty dog, the poor dog, the homeless dog, the strolling dog… whose instinct, like that of the poor man, is marvelously sharpened by necessity." The psalmist and the poet seize the same figure — the masterless urban scavenger circling the streets at dusk — and pull it in opposite directions. The psalm makes the stray a thing of menace and disgust; Baudelaire makes it the patron emblem of every cunning, marginal survivor. Read together they reveal how much the simile's contempt depends on point of view: the same dog is a threat to the hunted and a hero to the dispossessed.
**Verse 8**
הִנֵּה יַבִּיעוּן בְּפִיהֶם; חֲרָבוֹת בְּשִׂפְתוֹתֵיהֶם; כִּי־מִי שֹׁמֵעַ׃
A grotesque mixed metaphor: יַבִּיעוּן, "they gush / bubble forth," is a water-verb — used of flowing springs and of wisdom welling up, יַבִּיעַ אֹמֶר, "pours forth speech" (Ps. 19:3) — yoked to חֲרָבוֹת, "swords." Liquid and steel at once: mouths spouting blades like geysers. Why swords specifically, when the Psalter offers arrows (Ps. 64:4), razors (Ps. 52:4), and venom (Ps. 140:4) for weaponized speech? Arrows imply distance, poison stealth, razors sly cutting; swords are brazen, public, immediate. Their lips do not wound covertly — they brandish. The same body-parts, פֶּה and שָׂפָה, will reappear in v. 13 as the evidence that convicts them.
כִּי־מִי שֹׁמֵעַ, "for who hears?", is the psalm's pivot of dramatic irony, the stock voice of practical atheism (compare אָמַר בְּלִבּוֹ… לֹא רָאָה יָּהּ, "he says in his heart… the LORD does not see," Ps. 10:11). It is also the single denying כִּי that three affirming כִּי אֱלֹקִים clauses will overturn. Note too the acoustic near-touch: שֹׁמֵעַ ("hears," root שמע) sits a consonant away from the psalm's governing שמר ("watch") — ears beside eyes, both organs the enemies assume God is not using.
Sylvia Plath's late poem "Words" (*Ariel*, 1965), written weeks before her death, offers a striking analogue to the psalm's sense that speech does not vanish into air. She begins: "Axes / After whose stroke the wood rings, / And the echoes! / Echoes traveling / Off from the center like horses." Plath insists that once released, words keep moving outward, beyond recall, ringing long after the blow. The psalm's enemies imagine their swords-on-lips dissolve unheard; Plath, like the psalm's God in the next verse, knows the stroke does not stop where the speaker thinks it does.
**Verse 9**
וְאַתָּה ה׳ תִּשְׂחַק־לָמוֹ; תִּלְעַג לְכָל־גּוֹיִם׃
The second וְאַתָּה pivot answers v. 8's "who hears?" with two syllables of devastation: not only does God hear, God laughs. And where v. 6's pivot piled up four titles, this one uses a single name — the brevity is the rebuttal. One divine name suffices to answer the arrogant question. תִּשְׂחַק, "You laugh," with the archaic poetic suffix לָמוֹ, "at them," lifts the verb directly from the throne-room of Psalm 2: יוֹשֵׁב בַּשָּׁמַיִם יִשְׂחָק, אֲדֹנָי יִלְעַג־לָמוֹ, "He who sits in heaven laughs, the Lord mocks them" (2:4) — same two verbs (שחק, לעג), same suffix, same scene of nations conspiring. Meiri makes the link explicit: כְּעִנְיַן ה׳ יִלְעַג לָמוֹ, "as in 'the LORD mocks them.'" The divine laughter is not amusement but contempt — the absolute sovereign's response to an impotent threat. The LXX's ἐξουδενώσεις for תִּלְעַג ("treat as nothing") presses harder than mere mockery toward annihilation.
Milton heard this laughter and slowed it down. In *Paradise Lost* (1667), Book V, the Father regards the rebel angels: "Mighty Father, thou thy foes / Justly hast in derision, and secure / Laugh'st at thir vain designs and tumults vain." Milton's syntax delays the verb across three lines, so the laughter arrives calm, untouched, inevitable — the derision of a heaven that was never in danger. The contrast with the psalm is instructive: Milton's God laughs from celestial security, but the psalmist must summon that same laughter from inside immediate mortal peril, with the dogs still at the door. The psalm borrows heaven's serenity into a besieged house; that borrowing is the whole act of faith.
**Verse 10**
עֻזּוֹ אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמֹרָה; כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי׃
The first confidence refrain — and a genuine grammatical knot. עֻזּוֹ is "*his* strength" (third person), yet it is addressed to אֵלֶיךָ, "*You*" (second person). The mismatch invites three solutions. The LXX read עֻזִּי, "*my* strength" (κράτος μου), smoothing it. Ibn Ezra takes עֻזּוֹ as an appositive title for God — אַתָּה הָאֵל שֶׁהָעֹז לוֹ בְּעַצְמוֹ, "You, the God who has strength in Himself." Rashi and the plain-sense school keep the third person and refer it to the *enemy*: עֻזּוֹ וְתָקְפּוֹ שֶׁל אוֹיְבִי הֶחָזָק עָלַי, אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "the might of my enemy, who is stronger than I — toward You I keep watch." The cleanest reading honors the psalm's own design: the anomalous "his strength" of v. 10 is *meant* to be transformed into the "my strength" of v. 18. The shift from third person to first is itself the argument — power that belonged to another becomes the speaker's own.
אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "I will keep watch," is the שמר reversal completed. The same verb that named hostile surveillance in v. 1 now names faithful vigil toward God — a willed first-person form (cohortative, signaling resolve), the opposite vector of the same root. Ibn Ezra connects it to לֵיל שִׁמּוּרִים, the "night of watching," and back to וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת־הַבַּיִת. מִשְׂגַּבִּי, "my haven," is the one element of the refrain that will *not* change between v. 10 and v. 18: God-as-fortress stays fixed while everything around it — the possessive, the watching-verb — transforms.
Beckett knew this posture of waiting and drained it of its certainty. In *En attendant Godot* (1953), Vladimir and Estragon hang their lives on an arrival: VLADIMIR: Nous serons sauvés, "We'll be saved" — *if* he comes. The psalmist's אֶשְׁמֹרָה is vigil grounded in a haven already proven (כִּי, "for," God *is* my stronghold); Beckett keeps the bare structure of expectant watching and removes the ground beneath it, so that redemptive waiting curdles into reflex. The psalm watches *because* rescue is sure; Beckett's tramps watch because watching is all that is left.
**Verse 11**
אֱלֹקֵי (חַסְדּוֹ) [חַסְדִּי] יְקַדְּמֵנִי; אֱלֹקִים יַרְאֵנִי בְשֹׁרְרָי׃
A textual fork with real theological stakes. The Kethib (written) is חַסְדּוֹ, "*His* faithfulness"; the Qere (read) is חַסְדִּי, "*my* faithfulness." Radak lays it out exactly: הַכְּתִיב עַל עוֹשֶׂה הַחֶסֶד וְהוּא הָאֵל… וְהַקְּרִי עַל הַמְקַבְּלוֹ וְהוּא דָּוִד, "the written form refers to the doer of the kindness, God; the read form, to its recipient, David." The LXX supports the Kethib (ὁ ἔλεος αὐτοῦ, "his mercy"). The choice matters because if the Kethib is original, the psalm runs an arc — God's חסד meets me (v. 11) → my חסד with God (v. 18) — exactly paralleling the עֻזּוֹ → עֻזִּי transformation: a divine attribute that becomes the speaker's possession. The Qere harmonizes both verses into consistency but flattens the climb.
יְקַדְּמֵנִי, "will come to meet / anticipate me," from קדם (the root of "east," what lies ahead), makes חסד a vanguard — loyalty that arrives *before* the crisis. The LXX's προφθάσει, "will come first," catches the preemptive sense. Compare the courtly image of Ps. 89:15, חֶסֶד וֶאֱמֶת יְקַדְּמוּ פָנֶיךָ, "kindness and truth go before Your face."
יַרְאֵנִי בְשֹׁרְרָי, "He lets me look on my watchers," completes the inverted gaze. שֹׁרְרָי, "my watchers / lurkers," is a near-fixed term for the speaker's foes in this cluster (יָשִׁיב הָרַע לְשֹׁרְרָי, "He returns evil to my watchers," Ps. 54:7; cf. 56:3). The man who was watched to be killed (v. 1) now looks, with satisfaction, on the very watchers who hunted him. Three watching-roots converge in two verses — שמר (v. 10), ראה (v. 11), שרר (v. 11) — gaze running speaker-to-God, God-to-speaker, enemy-to-speaker, all at once.
**Verse 12**
אַל־תַּהַרְגֵם פֶּן־יִשְׁכְּחוּ עַמִּי; הֲנִיעֵמוֹ בְחֵילְךָ וְהוֹרִידֵמוֹ; מָגִנֵּנוּ אֲדֹנָי׃
The theologically startling request: in an imprecatory psalm, *do not kill them*. The logic is pedagogical, not merciful. Rashi nails the reasoning: שֶׁאֵין זוֹ נְקָמָה הַנִּכֶּרֶת… כָּל הַמֵּתִים מִשְׁתַּכְּחִים, "this is not a recognizable revenge — all the dead are forgotten." Living, wandering enemies are perpetual testimony; dead ones fade from memory. The forgetting-verb יִשְׁכְּחוּ (root שכח) carries Torah's weight — שכח is the standing spiritual danger of Deuteronomy (פֶּן־תִּשְׁכַּח אֶת־ה׳, "lest you forget the LORD," Deut. 8:11).
הֲנִיעֵמוֹ, "make them wander / totter," is the precise root of Cain's curse — נָע וָנָד תִּהְיֶה בָאָרֶץ, "a wanderer and fugitive you shall be on the earth" (Gen. 4:12). The punishment is un-creation of the settled home, and it will be fulfilled textually in v. 16's יְנִיעוּן, "they wander." The archaic suffix here, ־מוֹ (on הֲנִיעֵמוֹ and וְהוֹרִידֵמוֹ), is worth noticing: this poetic 3mp ending appears six times in the psalm, all inside the judgment block (vv. 9–14), and always attached to the enemies — never to God, never to the speaker. It is the morphological badge of the condemned, marking the party to whom things are done: laughed at, made to wander, brought low.
מָגִנֵּנוּ אֲדֹנָי, "our shield, O Lord," startles with a sudden communal plural in an otherwise singular lament — and a title-shift to אֲדֹנָי. The community materializes precisely where the enemies' punishment is being designed as a lesson for "my people." Ibn Ezra reads מָגִנֵּנוּ as a royal hint: הוּא מַלְכֵּנוּ בֶּאֱמֶת, "He is truly our king," set against the human king Saul who ordered the killing. The shield is the ancient covenant image — אָנֹכִי מָגֵן לָךְ, "I am a shield to you" (Gen. 15:1).
**Verse 13**
חַטַּאת־פִּימוֹ דְּבַר־שְׂפָתֵימוֹ; וְיִלָּכְדוּ בִגְאוֹנָם; וּמֵאָלָה וּמִכַּחַשׁ יְסַפֵּרוּ׃
The mouth comes back as evidence. The exact body-parts that mounted swords in v. 8 — פֶּה, שָׂפָה — now mount the indictment: חַטַּאת־פִּימוֹ, "the sin of their mouth." This is the very noun חַטָּאָה the speaker disowned in v. 4 (לֹא־חַטָּאתִי), now relocated onto the enemy's speech-organ. Malbim presses the irony hard: the enemies themselves will confess — הֵם בְּעַצְמָם יְדַבְּרוּ בְּשִׂפְתוֹתֵיהֶם וְיִתְוַדּוּ אֶת הַחֵטְא, "they themselves will speak with their lips and confess the sin." The weapon becomes the witness.
וְיִלָּכְדוּ בִגְאוֹנָם, "let them be caught in their pride," makes the snare self-set. גָּאוֹן, "majesty / pride," is usually a positive word — God's grandeur, the sea's swell — but here the arrogance that asked "who hears?" (v. 8) becomes the net. Ibn Ezra ties them together explicitly: וְיִלָּכְדוּ בִגְאוֹנָם — כְּנֶגֶד כִּי מִי שׁוֹמֵעַ, "answering 'for who hears?'" Sin functions as its own punishment-mechanism; no external trap is needed.
The closing phrase is productively ambiguous. וּמֵאָלָה וּמִכַּחַשׁ יְסַפֵּרוּ joins אָלָה, "curse / binding oath," and כַּחַשׁ, "lying / denial" (the two stand parallel in BDB's citation of this very verse). The subject of יְסַפֵּרוּ, "they recount," is uncertain: are the enemies *trafficking* in curses and lies, or will their curses and lies be *told* as testimony against them? Meiri leaves the syntax deliberately knotted. The ambiguity is the point: their lying speech speaks their own sentence.
**Verse 14**
כַּלֵּה בְחֵמָה כַּלֵּה וְאֵינֵמוֹ; וְיֵדְעוּ כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מֹשֵׁל בְּיַעֲקֹב; לְאַפְסֵי הָאָרֶץ סֶלָה׃
The imprecatory apex, and the famous tension with v. 12. There the prayer was "do not kill them"; here it is the doubled כַּלֵּה… כַּלֵּה, "make an end… make an end," until וְאֵינֵמוֹ, "they are no more." The commentators divide. Ibn Ezra harmonizes by speed: כַּלֵּה — פְּעָמִים מְעַט מְעַט וּבַסּוֹף וְאֵינֵמוֹ, "consume them little by little, and at the end they are gone" — wandering first, annihilation last, two stages of one judgment. The plainer reading lets the tension stand: the psalm is honest about the escalation of imprecatory desire, which begins wanting a useful lesson and ends wanting nothing left. The word וְאֵינֵמוֹ carries the condemned-suffix ־מוֹ for the last time — the morpheme dissolving along with its referents.
חֵמָה, "burning wrath," also means "venom" (BDB notes the dual sense, from a root meaning "heat"); the fury that consumes the enemies has a poison-edge that quietly answers their poison-lips.
מֹשֵׁל בְּיַעֲקֹב, "ruling in Jacob" — not Israel. The choice is pointed. Ibn Ezra glosses it against the nations' gods: אֱלֹקֵי הָעַמִּים אֱלִילִים וֵאלֹקֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל שׁוֹפֵט צֶדֶק, "the nations' gods are idols; Israel's God is a righteous judge." But there is a quieter resonance: Jacob was himself the archetypal wanderer — אֲרַמִּי אֹבֵד אָבִי, "a wandering Aramean was my father" (Deut. 26:5). The enemies condemned to wander (v. 12) are placed under the God who rules through and over a people that began in wandering.
לְאַפְסֵי הָאָרֶץ, "to the ends of the earth," is the scale's outer limit. אֶפֶס means "nothingness, extremity" — the uttermost margins where being meets void, more extreme than ordinary קְצֵה, "edge." The psalm's trajectory completes here: house (v. 1) → city (v. 7) → all nations (vv. 6, 9) → ends of the earth. And the second סֶלָה lands, like the first, on the line of maximal public scope, the two pauses sealing the cosmic frame.
**Verse 15**
וְיָשֻׁבוּ לָעֶרֶב; יֶהֱמוּ כַכָּלֶב; וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר׃
The refrain returns, all but verbatim — and the return is the whole formal argument. The dogs of v. 7 are repeated almost unchanged *after* the death-sentence of vv. 12–14 has been passed over them. The form is almost identical; the meaning is inverted by position. The one tiny shift, יָשׁוּבוּ (v. 7) → וְיָשֻׁבוּ (v. 15), adds a connective waw and a slightly altered vowel, perhaps nudging from pure description ("they keep returning") toward sequence ("and so they return"). Malbim reads the difference as the hinge between threat and pathos: now the same circling dog is כְּכֶלֶב הָרָעֵב שֶׁסּוֹבֵב בַּלַּיְלָה בָּעִיר לְבַקֵּשׁ אוֹכֶל, "like the hungry dog that prowls the city at night looking for food," driven not by malice but by an empty stomach.
The refrain's encircling motion — וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר, "they circle the city" — mimes its own behavior: the dogs circle, and the verse circles back to repeat itself. The structure enacts the content. Meiri marks the reversal cleanly: where before they were there לְרַגֵּל, "to spy," now they return דֶּרֶךְ קְלָלָה, "by way of curse," circling not to kill but to beg. Everything depends on what has happened between the two almost identical pictures: divine action has changed nothing about the words and everything about their weight.
**Verse 16**
הֵמָּה (יְנוֹעוּן) [יְנִיעוּן] לֶאֱכֹל; אִם־לֹא יִשְׂבְּעוּ וַיָּלִינוּ׃
The transformation is complete and the prayer is fulfilled inside its own poem. הֵמָּה יְנִיעוּן, "*they* wander," uses the exact root of v. 12's imprecation הֲנִיעֵמוֹ, "make them wander." The curse is enacted textually: what was asked is now described. And the fronted pronoun הֵמָּה, "they," stands one verse before the speaker's וַאֲנִי, "but I" (v. 17) — the sharpest "they / but I" antithesis in the poem. There is even a faint sonic chime: the demonstrative הֵמָּה (HĒM-MĀH) shares its consonants ה־מ with the howl-verb יֶהֱמוּ (ye-he-MŪ) of the refrain, so that the word naming the dogs half-echoes the noise they make, just as the simile is finally cashed in.
The governing grammar reaches its destination at אִם־לֹא יִשְׂבְּעוּ, "if they are not satisfied" — an imperfect verb of non-completion for an appetite that never closes. The enemies' final verb cannot finish, and neither can their hunger; aspect and fate coincide. The picture closely recalls Amos: וְנָעוּ… וְלֹא יִשְׂבָּעוּ, "they wandered… and were not satisfied" (Amos 4:8).
וַיָּלִינוּ holds two roots at once. לון I means "to spend the night" — homeless, lodging outside; לון II means "to murmur, grumble." Both are active: they lodge unsatisfied *and* they whine. The LXX's γογγύσουσιν, "they will grumble," chooses the second — and lands a sharp irony, for γογγύζω is the verb of Israel's own wilderness murmuring (וַיִּלֹּנוּ… עַל־מֹשֶׁה, "they grumbled against Moses," Exod. 16:2). The enemies end by replicating the complaint of God's own people in the desert.
Manuel Bandeira's "O Bicho" (1947), written amid the poverty of Rio, takes this exact image — the creature scavenging for food in filth — and detonates it. He describes an animal catando comida entre os detritos, "scavenging food among the debris," and then: O bicho não era um cão, / Não era um gato, / Não era um rato. / O bicho, meu Deus, era um homem, "The animal was not a dog, / not a cat, / not a rat. / The animal, my God, was a man." The psalm weaponizes the scavenger to *degrade* its enemies into dog-like need; Bandeira reverses the vector entirely, so the shock is the discovery that the scavenging beast is human, and animalization becomes an indictment of social cruelty rather than a curse upon foes. Set against the psalm, Bandeira exposes the moral cost the imprecation pays: to wish your enemy reduced to a starving dog is, Bandeira reminds us, to wish a man into the gutter.
**Verse 17**
וַאֲנִי אָשִׁיר עֻזֶּךָ; וַאֲרַנֵּן לַבֹּקֶר חַסְדֶּךָ; כִּי־הָיִיתָ מִשְׂגָּב לִי וּמָנוֹס בְּיוֹם צַר־לִי׃
The emphatic וַאֲנִי, "but I," is the poem's sharpest hinge, colliding directly with v. 16's הֵמָּה, "they." The pronoun is grammatically unnecessary; its presence is pure contrast (the classic lament marker — וַאֲנִי בְּחַסְדְּךָ בָטַחְתִּי, "but I have trusted in Your kindness," Ps. 13:6). Dogs whine at night; the speaker sings at morning. Two joyful verbs replace the enemies' single growl: אָשִׁיר, measured "song," and וַאֲרַנֵּן, the ecstatic jubilant outcry of רנן.
לַבֹּקֶר, "at morning," is the deliberate antithesis to the enemies' לָעֶרֶב, "at evening" (vv. 7, 15). Morning is the Psalter's fixed hour of reversal and renewed חסד — שַׂבְּעֵנוּ בַבֹּקֶר חַסְדֶּךָ, "satisfy us at morning with Your kindness" (Ps. 90:14); אֱלֹקִים… יַעְזְרֶהָ לִפְנוֹת בֹּקֶר, "God will help her at the turn of morning" (Ps. 46:6). חַסְדֶּךָ here is the first direct address of חסד to God, preparing v. 18's climactic אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי.
The verse closes with paired refuge-images: מִשְׂגָּב, the fixed inaccessible fortress, and מָנוֹס, "a place of flight," from נוס, "to flee." The two are complementary — one stands its ground above the danger, the other is the escape route out of it; God is both. The same pairing anchors the Davidic refuge-litany: מִשְׂגַּבִּי וּמְנוּסִי, "my haven and my refuge" (2 Sam. 22:3). The perfect tense הָיִיתָ, "You have been," grounds present song in past rescue: the singing rests on a deliverance already accomplished.
**Verse 18**
עֻזִּי אֵלֶיךָ אֲזַמֵּרָה; כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי׃
The refrain returns a final time, and three precise changes from v. 10 carry the whole psalm's argument. First, עֻזּוֹ, "*his* strength," becomes עֻזִּי, "*my* strength": the power that belonged to another (enemy or God, depending on how v. 10 is read) is now the speaker's own possession. Second, אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "I will keep watch," becomes אֲזַמֵּרָה, "I will sing." The two share neither root nor sense — שמר is anxious vigil, זמר is free praise — but they share a sound (esh-**MŌ**-rāh / a-zam-**MĒ**-rāh) and a form (both willed first-person cohortatives), so that the ear hears one verb mutate into the other. Three stages of a single trajectory: enemy surveillance (שמר, v. 1) → faithful vigil (שמר, v. 10) → free song (זמר, v. 18). Watching has become singing.
Third, the psalm adds two words it has been withholding: אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, "my faithful God / God of my faithfulness" — a phrase found nowhere else in the Psalter in exactly this form, and the last word of the poem. The genitive is productively double: subjectively, "the God to whom my faithfulness belongs"; objectively, "the God who shows faithfulness to me." If v. 11's Kethib (חַסְדּוֹ, "*His* kindness") is original, the arc is complete — God's חסד met me (v. 11), and now חסד is named as *mine* with God (v. 18) — the same dispossession that turned עֻזּוֹ into עֻזִּי. Malbim reads the whole transformation royally: עֻזִּי, הַיְינוּ שֶׁאַגִּיעַ לַמְּלוּכָה, "my strength — that I will attain the kingship," the fugitive's confidence that Saul's power is, in the end, watched-over and reserved for him.
One element does not change: מִשְׂגַּבִּי, "my haven," stands identical in v. 10 and v. 18. Everything else transforms — the possessive on עז, the watching that became song, the חסד now added — while God-as-fortress holds fixed. That is the psalm's quiet theology: God is the constant; what deepens is the singer's grasp of Him.
Solomon Ibn Gabirol, the eleventh-century Andalusian poet, built his most beloved piyyut directly on this verse's vocabulary. *Shaḥar Avakeshka* opens: שַׁחַר אֲבַקֶּשְׁךָ, צוּרִי וּמִשְׂגַּבִּי, אֶעֱרֹךְ לְפָנֶיךָ שַׁחְרִי וְגַם עַרְבִּי, "At dawn I seek You, my Rock and my haven; I set before You my morning and my evening." Ibn Gabirol lifts the psalm's מִשְׂגַּבִּי and its morning-song and does something the psalm could not: he turns one night's emergency gratitude into a *repeatable* discipline. Where the psalmist sings at the particular morning after a particular siege survived, Ibn Gabirol makes "morning and evening" a permanent liturgical rhythm — שַׁחְרִי וְגַם עַרְבִּי folding the enemies' evening and the speaker's morning into a single daily round of seeking. The psalm's once-only dawn becomes, in the piyyut, every dawn there will ever be.---
