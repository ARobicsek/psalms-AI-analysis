## Changes
*For exact before/after text, see [psalm_059_copy_edit_diff.md](psalm_059_copy_edit_diff.md).*

1. [7, 9(b)] **Introduction**: Replaced "the speaker waits in silence" with "the speaker directs his own words to God." — The psalmist is not silent in Psalm 59; he speaks throughout in petition. The original phrasing overstated the contrast and conflicted with the actual text.
2. [7] **Introduction — floor plan**: Changed "the dog-refrain repeated verbatim" to "repeated with slight variation." — Verse 15 is not strictly verbatim to v. 7; it adds a prefixed ו and a vowel shift in יָשֻׁבוּ / יָשׁוּבוּ.
3. [7, 9(c)] **Introduction — Selah paragraph**: Changed "quotes it almost exactly" to "echoes it" and softened the final framing to "cast as the war of the nations against the anointed." — Psalm 59:9 clearly echoes Psalm 2:4, but it is not an almost exact quotation; the theological conclusion needed slightly more cautious wording than the textual overlap alone could prove.
4. [7, 2] **Introduction — expansion/contraction paragraph**: Changed "identical words return" to "the same words return" and softened וַיָּלִינוּ to "can even be heard as a whine." — The refrain is not textually identical, and the later verse itself acknowledges that וַיָּלִינוּ is contested, so the introduction needed to stay consistent with that later nuance.
5. [7, 9(b)] **Introduction — power keyword paragraph**: Changed "differing only in their vowels" to "differing chiefly in their vowels" and softened the conclusion about "stolen strength." — עַזִּים and עֻזִּי are near-twins, but not identical except for vowels; the prior wording ignored the plural ending. The theological claim about transfer of strength was also stronger than the sound-play alone could establish.
6. [9(c)] **Introduction — Cain paragraph**: Changed "Psalm 59 cites that whole pattern" to "Psalm 59 echoes that pattern." — The link to Cain is thematic and lexical, but not a direct citation.
7. [9(f)] **Liturgical — Key verses and phrases**: Changed "has never been entirely obvious" to "is not entirely obvious." — The original attributed a judgment to unspecified readers or commentators without evidence; the revision keeps the point while avoiding an unsupported attribution.
8. [8] **Verse 2**: Removed the stem label "Piel" from תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי and the label "reflexive participle" from מִמִּתְקוֹמְמַי. — These grammar tags were not doing interpretive work beyond what the translations and explanations already conveyed, so they added jargon without illumination.
9. [9(c)] **Verse 6**: Changed "the enemies of the anointed are the rebellious nations" to "are treated as the rebellious nations." — The royal/theological framing is plausible, but the evidence supports poetic casting more securely than strict equivalence.
10. [7, 9(e)] **Verse 7**: Changed "the lions or wolves the Psalter usually reaches for" to "the lions or wolves the Psalter can also reach for," and softened the LXX conclusion to "focus on different aspects of the image." — The Psalter also uses dogs, so "usually" overstated the contrast; likewise, the MT/LXX difference does not justify the claim that they disagree about "what is most frightening."
11. [7, 9(c)] **Verse 9**: Changed "an almost verbatim lift" to "a clear echo" and softened the intertextual conclusion to "reads Saul's death squad in the same frame." — The verbal overlap with Psalm 2 is real but not nearly verbatim, and the relationship is better described as framing than as direct textual filing.
12. [9(b)] **Verse 10**: Added hedge to the reading of עֻזּוֹ as intentionally third-person and carried that hedge into the progression argument. — This is an interpretive solution to a grammatical difficulty, not a certainty; the text needed to present it as a plausible reading rather than established fact.
13. [7] **Verse 11**: Corrected the translation of Psalm 54:7 from "my watchful enemies" to "my foes." — The cited verse uses לְשֹׁרְרָי in the ordinary sense "foes"; "watchful enemies" imported the commentary's motif into a citation that does not itself make that point.
14. [9(c)] **Verse 12**: Changed "a quiet citation of Scripture's first model" to "a quiet echo of Scripture's first model." — The Cain connection is thematic and lexical, but not a direct citation.
15. [7, 9(b), 9(f)] **Verse 13**: Rewrote the discussion of יְסַפֵּרוּ to remove the unsupported alternate meaning "numbered/consumed" and the LXX claim built on it. — The original argument depended on a dubious semantic leap the text did not justify. The revised wording keeps the speech/indictment point and preserves Malbim's confessional reading in a clearer, text-based way.
16. [9(b), 7] **Verse 14**: Removed the claim that בְּיַעֲקֹב implies specially "immanent" rule and deleted the gloss on אֶפֶס as "zero, nothing." — The preposition ב in משל ב is standard for ruling over a people, so the original theological inference overread the grammar; the אפס gloss did not support the argument being made.
17. [9(b)] **Verse 16**: Changed "quietly concedes that they will not be filled" to "keeps the hunger open-ended and fits the curse of Isaiah 56:11." — The conditional clause does not itself prove that they will certainly remain unsated; the revised wording matches the evidence.
18. [7, 2] **Verse 17**: Corrected the claim that "the speaker never assigns himself a perfect-tense verb." — Earlier in the psalm the speaker uses first-person perfects in his protestation of innocence (לֹא־פִשְׁעִי וְלֹא־חַטָּאתִי, v. 4). The revision preserves the grammatical insight by focusing specifically on confidence grounded in God's perfect-tense action.
19. [2, 9(b)] **Verse 18**: Carried forward the hedge from v. 10 ("on that reading"), softened the claim that the enemies' fierceness is literally transferred, and changed "resolve the Qere" to "echo the Qere." — Once the progression from עֻזּוֹ to עֻזִּי is presented as interpretive, later restatements had to preserve that hedge; "resolve" also overstated what the closing phrase does with the v. 11 Qere.
