# Commentary on Psalm 59
---
## Introduction
A death squad has surrounded the house. The superscription tells us so with brutal economy: שָׁאוּל וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת־הַבַּיִת לַהֲמִיתוֹ, "Saul sent men to watch the house in order to put him to death." And yet, for a poem occasioned by armed assassins, Psalm 59 is strangely free of weapons. No spears fly, no swords are drawn — except one set of metaphorical blades that the enemies carry not in their hands but on their lips (v. 8). The menace here has been re-coded almost entirely as *sound*: the enemies bark, growl, rave, and "pour forth" speech, while the speaker directs his own words to God and, at the very end, answers them — not with a sword, but with a song. The poem's deepest claim is that the only thing that finally defeats a hostile noise is a different noise.
That claim is built, with a craftsman's patience, out of a single consonant. The root that opens the psalm is שׁמר, "to watch, guard": Saul's men *watch* (וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ) the house. In the first confidence-refrain, the speaker takes that exact verb and turns it inside out — עֻזּוֹ אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "for You I keep watch" (v. 10). The hunted man, the *object* of surveillance, becomes its *subject*: he watches no longer the door but God. And in the psalm's final word-cluster, the verb mutates one consonant further. אֶשְׁמֹרָה (`'esh-MŌ-rāh`) becomes אֲזַמֵּרָה (`'ă-zam-MĒ-rāh`), "I will sing" (v. 18). The two verbs are near-twins — both open with a sibilant, both keep the מ־ר skeleton, both are cohortatives ending in `-ārāh`. To the ear, *watching* does not so much get replaced by *singing* as get **transfigured into it**, one letter at a time. שׁמר → זמר. Surveillance becomes song.
Before tracing how the poet earns that transformation, here is the floor plan. The psalm divides cleanly into two parallel halves on a double-refrain spine:
- **v. 1** — superscription (Saul, the watched house).
- **vv. 2–6** — fourfold cry for rescue; protest of innocence; appeal to the cosmic Judge. (*Selah*, v. 6.)
- **vv. 7–10** — First cycle: the dog-refrain (7) → the enemies' raving speech (8) → God's laughter (9) → the confidence-refrain (10).
- **vv. 11–14** — God's חֶסֶד arrives; a prayer for *graduated*, pedagogical judgment. (*Selah*, v. 14.)
- **vv. 15–18** — Second cycle: the dog-refrain repeated with slight variation (15) → the dogs now starving (16) → morning praise (17) → the expanded confidence-refrain (18).
Notice what the *Selahs* do. A reader expects the musical pause to fall at the structural seams — the ends of the two refrains, vv. 10 and 18. It does not. Both *Selahs* land instead on the two verses that blow the conflict open to cosmic scale: לִפְקֹד כׇּל־הַגּוֹיִם, "to bring all nations to account" (v. 6), and לְאַפְסֵי הָאָרֶץ, "to the ends of the earth" (v. 14). The performance marks track the *theology*, not the architecture: the poem pauses to breathe precisely where Saul's local thugs are revealed as one instance of a universal rebellion that God judges. This is the same move Psalm 2 makes when יוֹשֵׁב בַּשָּׁמַיִם יִשְׂחָק, "He who sits in heaven laughs" (Ps 2:4) at the raging nations — and our psalmist echoes it in v. 9, וְאַתָּה ה׳ תִּשְׂחַק־לָמוֹ, "But You, O LORD, laugh at them." David's bedroom siege is, theologically, cast as the war of the nations against the anointed.
The genius of the poem is that this expansion of God's reach is matched, beat for beat, by a *contraction* of the enemy's. As the stage widens — house (v. 1) → city (vv. 7, 15) → all nations (vv. 6, 9) → ends of the earth (v. 14) — the dogs' radius collapses. In the first refrain they *circle the city* with predatory purpose: וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר, "they roam the city" (v. 7). After the imprecation, the same words return in v. 15 — but now the prayer of v. 12, הֲנִיעֵמוֹ, "make them wander," has done its work. By v. 16 the confident circuit has become aimless scavenging, יְנִיעוּן לֶאֱכֹל, "they wander in search of food," and the growl can even be heard as a whine, וַיָּלִינוּ, "they whimper." The refrain that returns *circles back exactly as the dogs circle* — the form enacts the content — but the second time we hear it, we already know it is hollow. God's reach grows; the dogs shrink; the two motions are one mechanism.
The poet runs the same dispossession through his keyword for power. The enemies are first introduced as עַזִּים, "fierce ones" (v. 4) — `ʿaz-ZIY-m`. By the end, that very sound has changed hands. עֻזּוֹ, "his strength" (v. 10, God's), becomes עֻזֶּךָ, "Your strength" (v. 17, addressed directly), becomes עֻזִּי, "my strength" (v. 18) — `ʿuz-ZIY`. The enemies' defining attribute, `ʿazzîm`, and the psalmist's final possession, `ʿuzzî`, are aural near-twins, differing chiefly in their vowels. One *hears* fierceness being re-vowelled, through God, into the property of the man it was meant to destroy. The poem quietly suggests that their ferocity was never finally theirs; strength belongs to God to redistribute.
This is why the most theologically startling verse in the psalm is not an imprecation but a *restraint*. In the middle of cursing his attackers, the speaker pulls back: אַל־תַּהַרְגֵם פֶּן־יִשְׁכְּחוּ עַמִּי, "Do not kill them, lest my people forget" (v. 12). Rashi catches the logic exactly: כִּי כָל הַמֵּתִים מִשְׁתַּכְּחִים, "because all the dead are forgotten." A corpse teaches nothing for long; a wandering beggar is a standing lesson. The verb chosen, הֲנִיעֵמוֹ, "make them totter," reaches back to the first sentence God ever passed on a murderer — נָע וָנָד תִּהְיֶה בָאָרֶץ, "a wanderer and a fugitive you shall be on the earth" (Gen 4:12). Cain, too, was spared death and made a perpetual, living sign. Psalm 59 echoes that pattern: not annihilation, but humiliation kept on display "to the ends of the earth."
The human experience underneath all this craft is the specific vertigo of being *watched* by people who mean to kill you — the collapse of one's own home into a trap. Lu Xun caught the same dissolution in his 1918 *Diary of a Madman*, where lucid observation curdles into paranoia: 然而须十分小心。不然，那赵家的狗，何以看我两眼呢？ — "I must be very careful. Otherwise, why did Zhao's dog look at me twice?" Lu Xun's narrator has lost the frame; he cannot tell threat from shadow. Psalm 59's speaker keeps the frame with terrible clarity — he knows exactly who is at the door — and his way through the terror is not to escape the watching but to *out-watch* it: to turn his own surveillance toward God until vigilance becomes melody.
That is the one thing to carry away. Psalm 59 does not pray for the noise to stop. It prays for the power to answer it — and the answer, when it comes, is the enemy's own verb of watching, mutated by a single consonant into praise.

#### Full psalm
Psalm 59 is not part of the fixed daily, Shabbat, or festival liturgy in any of the major rites. Its unfiltered imprecation — אַל־תָּחֹן כׇּל־בֹּגְדֵי אָוֶן, "have no mercy on any treacherous villain" (v. 6) — and its second-person curses make it awkward as communal prayer; one does not easily lead a congregation in "consume them, consume them" (v. 14). Its principal devotional home is *outside* the statutory service: it is the fifth of the ten psalms of Rebbe Nachman of Breslov's **Tikkun HaKlali** (the "General Remedy" — Pss 16, 32, 41, 42, 59, 77, 90, 105, 137, 150), recited by Breslov Hasidim (Nusach Sefard, i.e. the Hasidic rite) as a spiritual rectification. There the enemies who watch the house are read inwardly: Saul's brazen assault (עַזּוּת) maps onto intrusive impurity, and the psalmist's survival models spiritual resilience.
#### Key verses and phrases
Two windows give Psalm 59 voice in the printed liturgy. First, in **Perek Shirah**, the anthology of creation's "songs" printed as an appendix and recited as a voluntary devotion (especially in Hasidic communities after Shacharit), vv. 6 and 17 are placed in the mouths of non-human creatures: וְאַתָּה ה׳ אֱלֹקִים צְבָקוֹת... הָקִיצָה לִפְקֹד כָּל־הַגּוֹיִם, "You, LORD God of hosts... bestir Yourself to bring all nations to account," paired with וַאֲנִי אָשִׁיר עֻזֶּךָ, "But I will sing of Your strength." Lifted out of David's bedroom, the verses become a timeless voice in the cosmic choir — though why so fierce a petition belongs among the animals' praises is not entirely obvious.
Second, the **Rosh Hashanah Shacharit** machzor (Nusach Ashkenaz) embeds the final refrain in a piyyut expanding the chazzan's Kedushah. The prayer-leader, conscious of his unworthiness before the Day of Judgment, pleads: עֻזִּי אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמְרָה לְסַעֲדִי... כִּי אַתָּה מִשְׂגַּבִּי אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, "My strength — toward You I keep watch for my support... for You are my haven, my faithful God." The reworking is instructive: it borrows v. 18's frame (עֻזִּי, "my strength"; the closing מִשְׂגַּבִּי אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי) but grafts onto it v. 10's verb, אֶשְׁמְרָה, "I keep watch" — not v. 18's אֲזַמֵּרָה, "I sing." The liturgy thus fuses the watch-pole and the song-pole that the psalm so deliberately separates. The penitent on the Day of Judgment is not yet ready to sing; he is still, like David at the window, *keeping watch* — and the rite quietly reverses the psalm's signature transformation, holding the speaker at vigil rather than releasing him into praise.

## Verse-by-Verse Commentary
**Verse 1**
לַמְנַצֵּחַ אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת לְדָוִד מִכְתָּם; בִּשְׁלֹחַ שָׁאוּל, וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת־הַבַּיִת לַהֲמִיתוֹ׃
Three opaque technical terms crowd the first half-line, and their very opacity is part of the data. אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת, "do not destroy," recurs identically atop Psalms 57, 58, and 75 — almost certainly the name of a melody, perhaps the tune-tag of a known vintage song, since the phrase appears in Isaiah 65:8 of a cluster of grapes: אַל־תַּשְׁחִיתֵהוּ כִּי בְרָכָה בּוֹ, "do not destroy it, for a blessing is in it." Rashi, however, hears it as theology rather than musicology: כָּךְ קָרָא שֵׁם הַמִּזְמוֹר עַל שֵׁם שֶׁהָיָה קָרוֹב לָמוּת, "he named the psalm thus because he was close to dying and being destroyed." There is a quiet payoff to Rashi's reading: the body of the poem contains the speaker's own negated-destruction plea, אַל־תַּהַרְגֵם, "do not kill them" (v. 12). The title and the body may answer each other — the al-tashcheth psalm whose argument is, precisely, *do not destroy utterly*.
מִכְתָּם is genuinely unknown. BDB throws up its hands ("meaning unknown"); etymologies run from כֶּתֶם, "gold" (a "golden poem"), to a Syriac root for inscription. The Septuagint's στηλογραφία, "stele-inscription," supports the second — a poem to be engraved. What we can say is distributional: all six michtamim (Pss 16, 56–60) are Davidic and cluster around crisis. Whatever the word means, it smells of persecution.
The hinge of the verse is וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ, "and they watched." This is the seed of the whole psalm. The root שׁמר here is hostile, lethal, fixed on an object — the *house*. The infinitive of purpose seals the stakes with cold finality: לַהֲמִיתוֹ, "to put *him* to death." Note the third-person suffix in a superscription otherwise narrating about David — a flicker of narrative distance, the report of a death warrant, just before the first-person voice cries out. The narrative behind it (1 Sam 19:11) has Michal lowering David through a window and disguising a teraphim in the bed; Meiri cites it directly: וַתּוֹרֶד מִיכַל אֶת דָּוִד בְּעַד הַחַלּוֹן, "and Michal let David down through the window."
**Verse 2**
הַצִּילֵנִי מֵאֹיְבַי אֱלֹקָי; מִמִּתְקוֹמְמַי תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי׃
Two rescue verbs from two different roots frame the divine name. הַצִּילֵנִי (root נצל) is the verb of *snatching away* — pulling a body out of immediate peril. תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי (root שׂגב) is the opposite spatial logic: not extraction but *elevation*, being set so high the attacker cannot reach. Radak draws the picture: כְּאִלּוּ תְשִׂימֵנִי בְּמָקוֹם רָם וְנִשְׂגָּב שֶׁלֹּא יוּכַל אֵלַי, "as if You set me in a place high and lofty, where he cannot reach me." Between the two verbs sits אֱלֹקָי, "my God" — the personal possessive cradled inside the two requests, the still point the rescue verbs circle.
The choice of שׂגב matters beyond this verse, because it is a seed that will flower. Here it is a petition — *make me high*. It returns as the noun מִשְׂגָּב, "high refuge," in both confidence-refrains (vv. 10, 18) and as retrospective testimony in v. 17 (הָיִיתָ מִשְׂגָּב לִי, "You have *been* my refuge"). The keyword migrates from plea to confession to memory. The Septuagint flattens all of this with λυτρόω, "redeem," losing the verticality entirely — a reminder that "rescue" in Hebrew is a spectrum of distinct spatial pictures, not a single abstraction.
מִמִּתְקוֹמְמַי, "those who rise up against me," pictures the enemies as those who *raise themselves*. The pride is grammatical — built into the verb form — and it anticipates the trap of v. 13, where the wicked are caught בִגְאוֹנָם, "in their own pride." The doubled `mim-` opening gives the word an emphatic, almost stammering force.
**Verse 3**
הַצִּילֵנִי מִפֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן; וּמֵאַנְשֵׁי דָמִים הוֹשִׁיעֵנִי׃
The anaphora הַצִּילֵנִי / הַצִּילֵנִי, "rescue me / rescue me," heads both vv. 2 and 3, drumming urgency. But the third rescue verb, הוֹשִׁיעֵנִי (root ישׁע), adds yet a third register: not snatching (נצל), not elevating (שׂגב), but *granting victory* — the verb of deliverance in battle. Three rescue roots in two verses, comprehensive coverage of every modality of being saved.
The enemy descriptors escalate morally. פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן, "workers of iniquity," is a Psalter formula; compare Psalm 6:9, סוּרוּ מִמֶּנִּי כׇּל־פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן, "depart from me, all you workers of iniquity." The word אָוֶן carries a useful double charge — both *wickedness* and *emptiness, futility* — so their scheming is at once evil and doomed. Sigmund Mowinckel famously read this phrase across the Psalter as naming practitioners of malevolent sorcery, which would make the "swords on their lips" of v. 8 literal verbal magic; the reading is contested but it sharpens why this psalm fears *speech* so much.
אַנְשֵׁי דָמִים, "men of bloods," uses the plural דָּמִים — not abstract blood but accumulated acts of bloodshed. The phrase recurs at Psalm 55:24, אַנְשֵׁי דָמִים וּמִרְמָה לֹא־יֶחֱצוּ יְמֵיהֶם, "men of blood and deceit shall not live out half their days." There is a hard irony hovering here that the medievals do not name but the alert reader will: it is the very accusation Shimei later hurls at David himself — אִישׁ הַדָּמִים, "man of blood" (2 Sam 16:7). The hunted man pleads against being made the thing his enemies will one day call him.
**Verse 4**
כִּי הִנֵּה אָרְבוּ לְנַפְשִׁי; יָגוּרוּ עָלַי עַזִּים; לֹא־פִשְׁעִי וְלֹא־חַטָּאתִי ה׳׃
אָרְבוּ לְנַפְשִׁי, "they lie in ambush for my life," is military diction — the verb of the hidden raiding party (compare Psalm 142:4, טָמְנוּ פַח לִי, "they have hidden a trap for me"). The ambushers are also, paradoxically, loud public talkers (v. 8): covert in tactic, brazen in speech. יָגוּרוּ is ambiguous — the medievals read it as "they gather/assemble" (Radak: יֵאָסְפוּ וְיִתְקַבְּצוּ עָלַי); the root can also mean "attack" or even "dread." The assembly reading fits the siege image best.
עַזִּים, "fierce ones," is the first sounding of the עז note that the psalm will spend the next fourteen verses confiscating from the enemy. Malbim sees the whole moral case turning on this word: רַק עַזִּים אוֹרְבִים שֶׁלֹּא כַדִּין, "it is only *fierce men* lying in wait, against all justice" — fierceness without right.
The verse's second half launches a threefold protestation of innocence that runs into v. 5, and each term is a distinct technical category: פֶּשַׁע is willful rebellion, חַטָּאת is missing the mark, and עָוֺן (v. 5) is accumulated guilt — together the full legal-covenantal spectrum, denied exhaustively. This is the rhetoric of Job, who likewise covers every category to leave God no loophole. The Septuagint, characteristically, collapses the distinctions into the generic ἀνομία / ἁμαρτία, losing the legal precision the Hebrew is at pains to deploy.
**Verse 5**
בְּלִי־עָוֺן יְרֻצוּן וְיִכּוֹנָנוּ; עוּרָה לִקְרָאתִי וּרְאֵה׃
The verse pivots violently mid-line. The first half is third-person enemy description: יְרֻצוּן וְיִכּוֹנָנוּ, "they run and array themselves." (The archaic poetic ending `-ūn` on יְרֻצוּן recurs across the psalm — יַבִּיעוּן in v. 8, יְנִיעוּן in v. 16 — a consistent stylistic signature.) The verb יִכּוֹנָנוּ deserves a glance: its root כון means to *establish, make firm* — the enemies are setting themselves up as a stable fighting position, ironically appropriating the language of permanence that properly belongs to God's establishing of the righteous.
Then, without transition, the line lurches toward God in raw imperative: עוּרָה לִקְרָאתִי וּרְאֵה, "Rouse Yourself to meet me, and see!" This is bold theology — it implies divine dormancy. Ibn Ezra connects the wake-up call directly back to the title's surveillance: וְטַעַם עוּרָה כְּנֶגֶד וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת הַבַּיִת, "the sense of 'rouse Yourself' is *against* 'and they watched the house'" — the enemies are awake and watching, so God must wake too.
The choice of root is worth noting because the very next verse uses a *different* wake-up verb: עוּרָה (root עור, "stir from dormancy") here, then הָקִיצָה (root קיץ, "wake from sleep") in v. 6. Psalm 44:24 deploys both together — עוּרָה לָמָּה תִישַׁן... הָקִיצָה, "Awake! Why do You sleep?... Rouse Yourself!" — so the pairing is conventional. There is a sharp counter-image worth holding alongside it: Zechariah 13:7, חֶרֶב עוּרִי, "Sword, awake!" There a *weapon* is roused to strike; here, in a poem whose only weapons are on the enemies' lips, it is *God* who is roused to *see*. The psalm rouses sight, not steel.
**Verse 6**
וְאַתָּה ה׳־אֱלֹקִים צְבָקוֹת אֱלֹקֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, הָקִיצָה לִפְקֹד כׇּל־הַגּוֹיִם; אַל־תָּחֹן כׇּל־בֹּגְדֵי אָוֶן סֶלָה׃
The pivot-word וְאַתָּה, "But You," is the lament genre's classic vertical turn — from the horizontal crisis to the God above it. And the poet greets that turn with the most maximal divine title in the psalm: four designations stacked without the usual connecting syntax — ה׳, אֱלֹקִים, צְבָקוֹת, אֱלֹקֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. The accumulation is pointed. צְבָקוֹת means "hosts, armies." The God of *armies* is invoked against Saul's *military* squad — and, since Saul is Israel's own king, the God *of Israel's armies* is being summoned against Israel's army. Malbim reads the moment biographically: David, until now Israel's own field commander against the Philistines, here resigns his command into God's hands — מוֹסֵר צְבָקוֹתָיו אֶל ה', "he hands his armies over to the LORD."
The scale detonates. לִפְקֹד כׇּל־הַגּוֹיִם, "to bring all nations to account." The verb פקד is famously two-faced — it can mean a gracious visitation (God "visited" Sarah, Gen 21:1) or a punitive reckoning. The parallel clause settles it: אַל־תָּחֹן, "show no grace." We have leapt from a personal ambush (v. 4) to the judgment of every nation. Radak, troubled by the jump, reads the whole verse eschatologically — David, despairing of justice in his own corrupt generation, projects it to the end of days. But the simpler reading is the royal one: the enemies of the anointed are treated as the rebellious nations, the same equation Psalm 2 makes. The first *Selah* falls here — not at a structural seam but on this very universalizing of the conflict, the poem pausing to let the cosmic scope settle.
**Verse 7**
יָשׁוּבוּ לָעֶרֶב, יֶהֱמוּ כַכָּלֶב, וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר׃
Read this verse aloud. Three verbs roll by on long `ū`-vowels — yā-**SHŪ**-vū, ye-he-**MŪ**, wiy-**SŌ**-və-vū — and the whole line growls. יֶהֱמוּ (root המה) is itself an onomatopoeia for a low, tumultuous noise — used of the sea (Ps 46:4) and of roiling bowels (Jer 31:20). The verse *sounds like the dogs it describes*.
Why dogs, and not the lions or wolves the Psalter can also reach for? The choice is deliberate, and it is about *status*. Lions confer terror with dignity — פֶּן־יִטְרֹף כְּאַרְיֵה נַפְשִׁי, "lest he tear my life like a lion" (Ps 7:3); wolves are ravenous and martial — זְאֵבֵי עֶרֶב, "evening wolves" (Zeph 3:3), notably also an evening predator. But the dog in the ancient Near East is no pet. It is a pariah scavenger, unclean, death-associated, the lowest of insults: הֲכֶלֶב אָנֹכִי, "Am I a dog?" spits Goliath (1 Sam 17:43). The poet wants *menace without dignity* — danger you can also despise. Ibn Ezra hears the simile precisely: these are guard-dogs barking the night-watch, דִּמָּה אוֹתָם לִכְלָבִים שֶׁהֵם נוֹבְחִים בַּלַּיְלָה לִשְׁמוֹר הַמְּדִינָה, "he likened them to dogs that bark at night to guard the city."
לָעֶרֶב, "at evening," opens the psalm's great temporal antithesis: evening belongs to the dogs (vv. 7, 15), morning to the psalmist's song (v. 17). And the Septuagint preserves a road not taken: where MT has יֶהֱמוּ, "they growl," the Greek reads λιμώσσω, "they hunger" — either a different Hebrew text or an interpreter reading the dogs forward to their starvation in v. 16. The two traditions focus on different aspects of the image: the loud threat, or the desperate hunger.
**Verse 8**
הִנֵּה יַבִּיעוּן בְּפִיהֶם; חֲרָבוֹת בְּשִׂפְתוֹתֵיהֶם; כִּי־מִי שֹׁמֵעַ׃
יַבִּיעוּן is a grotesque verb to apply to speech. Its root, נבע, is the verb of *springs and fountains* gushing water — Proverbs 18:4, נַחַל נֹבֵעַ מְקוֹר חׇכְמָה, "a flowing stream, a fountain of wisdom." Here the mouths gush — and what gushes out is *swords*. The image is a deliberate collision: liquid eruption and edged metal, a fountain of blades. The poet wants you to flinch.
Weaponized speech is a Psalter signature, and this michtam cluster is saturated with it. Its twin, Psalm 57:5, has שִׁנֵּיהֶם חֲנִית וְחִצִּים וּלְשׁוֹנָם חֶרֶב חַדָּה, "their teeth are spears and arrows, their tongue a sharp sword." Psalm 64:4 sharpens the tongue into a sword and the bitter word into an arrow; Psalm 52:4 makes it a razor; Psalm 140:4 puts viper's venom under the lips. The choice of *sword* rather than *arrow* is telling — a sword is a close-quarters weapon. These enemies are not snipers at a distance; they are at the door, raving in your face.
The clincher is the rhetorical question כִּי־מִי שֹׁמֵעַ, "for who hears?" This is the Psalter's recurring profile of practical atheism — the assumption of divine blindness that licenses cruelty. Compare Psalm 94:7, וַיֹּאמְרוּ לֹא יִרְאֶה־יָּהּ, "they say, 'The LORD does not see.'" Note the acoustic mischief: שֹׁמֵעַ (`shō-MĒ-aʿ`, "hears," root שׁמע) sits one consonant from the surveillance root שׁמר that runs through the whole poem — different root, but the listening/watching field hums together. The enemies' question is about to be answered with devastating precision in the very next word.
**Verse 9**
וְאַתָּה ה׳ תִּשְׂחַק־לָמוֹ; תִּלְעַג לְכׇל־גּוֹיִם׃
"Who hears?" they ask. וְאַתָּה ה׳ — "But You, O LORD" — the second great pivot, demolishing the question. God not only hears; He *laughs*. תִּשְׂחַק־לָמוֹ, "You laugh at them," is a clear echo of Psalm 2:4, יוֹשֵׁב בַּשָּׁמַיִם יִשְׂחָק, "He who sits in heaven laughs." The intertext reframes everything: by echoing the enthronement psalm, our poet reads Saul's death squad in the same frame as the "raging nations against the anointed." Meiri makes the link explicit: כְּעִנְיַן ה' יִלְעַג לָמוֹ, "as in 'the LORD mocks them.'"
This laughter is not amusement; it is *fearless superiority*. Job 5:22 uses the same verb of the secure man: לְשֹׁד וּלְכָפָן תִּשְׂחָק, "at destruction and famine you shall laugh." And the second verb escalates: תִּלְעַג, "You deride," is more contemptuous than laughter — active scorn. Notice the archaic poetic suffix לָמוֹ for the expected לָהֶם; this `-mô` ending now clusters thickly through the imprecatory center (vv. 12, 13, 14), giving the enemy-section a distinctive sonic drone.
Milton heard this verse and slowed it down. In *Paradise Lost* (1667), Book V, the Father contemplates the rebel angels:
> Mighty Father, thou thy foes
> Justly hast in derision, and secure
> Laugh'st at thir vain designs and tumults vain...
Milton's syntax *delays* the laughter — "secure / Laugh'st" — so that the divine derision feels untroubled, inevitable, descending from a height nothing can reach. The psalmist's laughter does the same theological work but from a different vantage: Milton's God laughs from undisturbed heaven, while David must *invoke* that laughter from inside a surrounded house, betting his life that the One who sits above the siege finds it absurd.
**Verse 10**
עֻזּוֹ אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמֹרָה; כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי׃
The first confidence-refrain — and a real grammatical puzzle. עֻזּוֹ is "*his* strength" (third person), yet the line addresses God in the second person, אֵלֶיךָ, "toward *You*." One watches "his strength" toward "You." The commentators split. Rashi reads עֻזּוֹ as the *enemy's* strength: עֻזּוֹ וְתָקְפּוֹ שֶׁל אוֹיְבִי הֶחָזָק עָלַי, אֵלֶיךָ אֲצַפֶּה, "my strong enemy's might — toward You I look [for rescue]." Ibn Ezra reads it of God: הָאֵל שֶׁהָעֹז לוֹ בְּעַצְמוֹ, "the God whose strength is His own." Malbim, ingeniously, hears David guarding *Saul's kingship* in trust toward God who will transfer it to him.
Rather than emend עֻזּוֹ to עֻזִּי, "my strength," to match v. 18 — which would erase the very progression the psalm is building — the third-person distance may be intentional. On that reading, in v. 10 the strength is still external, viewed across a relational gap; by v. 18 it has become עֻזִּי, "*my* strength," personally possessed. The refrain encodes a journey, and v. 10 is its starting point: strength still at arm's length.
The verb is the spine of the poem. אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "I keep watch," is the *same root* as the title's וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ, "they watched" — now inverted from lethal surveillance of a house to faithful vigil toward God. Ibn Ezra hears it as a liturgical wakefulness: כְּטַעַם לֵיל שִׁמּוּרִים, "like 'a night of watching'" (Exod 12:42), set against the enemies' watch of v. 1. The cohortative form signals resolve, a vow. And מִשְׂגַּבִּי, "my high refuge," now arrives as a *noun* — the seed sown as a petition-verb in v. 2 (תְּשַׂגְּבֵנִי) has become a settled confession: God *is* my fortress.
**Verse 11**
אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי יְקַדְּמֵנִי; אֱלֹקִים יַרְאֵנִי בְשֹׁרְרָי׃
A celebrated textual crux opens the verse. The Ketiv (written text) is חַסְדּוֹ, "*his* faithfulness"; the Qere (read tradition) is חַסְדִּי, "*my* faithfulness." Radak explains the elegant logic: הַכְּתוּב עַל עוֹשֶׂה הַחֶסֶד... וְהַקְּרִי עַל הַמְקַבְּלוֹ, "the written form refers to the One who *does* the kindness [God]; the read form to the one who *receives* it [David]." The Septuagint's ὁ ἔλεος αὐτός supports the Ketiv. The theological stakes are real but the choice is not zero-sum: the Qere creates a clean bracket with the psalm's final three words, אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, "my faithful God" (v. 18), framing the whole second half. Either way, חֶסֶד — covenant-loyalty — surfaces here as the word the psalm has been reaching toward, and it will be its last word.
The verb יְקַדְּמֵנִי is a vivid spatial image: God's faithfulness "comes before me" — *meets me in advance*, like a military vanguard arriving ahead of the troops. The picture is rare and royal; Psalm 89:14 personifies the same dynamic, חֶסֶד וֶאֱמֶת יְקַדְּמוּ פָנֶיךָ, "steadfast love and faithfulness go before Your face." Divine protection here is not a static wall behind which one hides; it is a forward escort that reaches the danger *first*. The Septuagint's προφθάνω, "to arrive before, forestall," catches it exactly.
The last word, בְשֹׁרְרָי, is itself caught between two roots. The standard reading takes it from שׁרר, "be an enemy" — "my foes," as at Psalm 54:7, יָשִׁיב הָרַע לְשֹׁרְרָי, "He will requite evil to my foes." But the homonym שׁוּר means "to watch, spy." If the latter, this is a *fourth* watching-word in a psalm already drenched in surveillance (vv. 1, 8, 10). The ambiguity is almost too apt: in this poem, to be an enemy simply *is* to be a watcher. Rashi reads the line as fulfilled desire — יַרְאֵנִי בְשׁוֹרְרָי, מַה שֶּׁאֲנִי תָּאֵב לִרְאוֹת, "He will let me see, in my watchers, what I long to see."
**Verse 12**
אַל־תַּהַרְגֵם פֶּן־יִשְׁכְּחוּ עַמִּי; הֲנִיעֵמוֹ בְחֵילְךָ וְהוֹרִידֵמוֹ; מָגִנֵּנוּ אֲדֹנָי׃
The most theologically surprising verse in the psalm restrains its own curse. אַל־תַּהַרְגֵם, "do not kill them" — in the middle of an imprecation. The reasoning is pedagogical: פֶּן־יִשְׁכְּחוּ עַמִּי, "lest my people forget." Rashi spells out the cruel realism: שֶׁאֵין זוֹ נְקָמָה הַנִּכֶּרֶת... כִּי כָל הַמֵּתִים מִשְׁתַּכְּחִים, "this would not be a *recognizable* revenge... for all the dead are forgotten." A corpse is a quickly-closed file; a living, wandering beggar is an open lesson. John Calvin, reading this in the 1550s, drew from it a structural limit on Christian cursing: David asks for *prolonged* punishment not from private vindictiveness but so that the divine triumph remains visible and instructive.
The chosen verb, הֲנִיעֵמוֹ, "make them totter/wander," reaches back to Genesis 4. Cain's sentence was נָע וָנָד תִּהְיֶה בָאָרֶץ, "a wanderer and fugitive shall you be" (Gen 4:12) — the same root נוע. And the parallel runs deeper than the word: God spared Cain from death *and* made him wander *and* kept him alive as a marked, perpetual sign. Verse 12 echoes that whole sentence — spare-from-death, make-wander, leave-as-warning — making it a quiet echo of Scripture's first model of non-lethal, pedagogical judgment. The kinetic justice is exact: those who *circled the city* with purpose (v. 7) are made to *wander* without it.
Then a single anomaly: מָגִנֵּנוּ, "*our* shield" — the only first-person *plural* in a psalm of relentless "I." And it sits in the same verse as the only "my people," עַמִּי. The communal vocabulary surfaces precisely where the punishment is justified by a community's need to *remember*. The "we" and the "my people" are one thought: the individual lament cracks open to a collective exactly where the lesson must be witnessed. Ibn Ezra hears in מָגִנֵּנוּ a pointed allusion to Saul: הוּא מַלְכֵּנוּ בֶּאֱמֶת, "he is truly our king" — God the true shield against the king who would kill. The rare divine name אֲדֹנָי seals the verse.
**Verse 13**
חַטַּאת־פִּימוֹ דְּבַר־שְׂפָתֵימוֹ; וְיִלָּכְדוּ בִגְאוֹנָם; וּמֵאָלָה וּמִכַּחַשׁ יְסַפֵּרוּ׃
The poetic justice is built into the architecture. Recall v. 8: the swords gushed בְּפִיהֶם... בְּשִׂפְתוֹתֵיהֶם, "from their mouths... on their lips." Verse 13 returns to the very same organs — פִּימוֹ... שְׂפָתֵימוֹ, "their mouth... their lips" — but the function has flipped. What were *weapons* are now *evidence*: חַטַּאת־פִּימוֹ, "the sin of their mouth." The instrument of attack has become the indictment. The speech boomerangs.
וְיִלָּכְדוּ בִגְאוֹנָם, "let them be trapped in their own pride." The snare is not external; the pride *is* the trap — and it reaches back to v. 2's "those who *raise themselves up.*" Proverbs 11:6 states the principle baldly: וּבְהַוַּת בֹּגְדִים יִלָּכֵדוּ, "the treacherous are caught in their own malice." The arrogant "who hears?" of v. 8 is exactly the overconfidence that springs the trap shut.
The verb יְסַפֵּרוּ, "they recount," keeps the focus on speech: they go on telling curses and lies, and those very utterances are what condemn them. Malbim presses the verbal sense further: the enemies will be compelled to *confess* — שֶׁהֵם בְּעַצְמָם יְדַבְּרוּ בְּשִׂפְתוֹתֵיהֶם וְיִתְוַדּוּ אֶת הַחֵטְא, "that they themselves will speak with their lips and confess the sin." In either case, crimes *named* become the means of indictment.
**Verse 14**
כַּלֵּה בְחֵמָה כַּלֵּה וְאֵינֵמוֹ; וְיֵדְעוּ כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מֹשֵׁל בְּיַעֲקֹב, לְאַפְסֵי הָאָרֶץ סֶלָה׃
The psalm's most violent instant: כַּלֵּה... כַּלֵּה, "consume... consume," the imperative doubled around בְחֵמָה, "in fury." This is the bookend to the opening anaphora: the poem began in doubled *petition* (הַצִּילֵנִי / הַצִּילֵנִי, vv. 2–3) and now climaxes in doubled *imprecation*.
But does "consume them till they are no more" (וְאֵינֵמוֹ) contradict v. 12's "do not kill them"? The medievals resolve it as sequence. Ibn Ezra: כַּלֵּה פְּעָמִים מְעַט מְעַט וּבַסּוֹף וְאֵינֵמוֹ, "consume them little by little, and at the end — they are no more." First the slow, visible, pedagogical wandering; *then*, after the lesson has done its work, the final end. Mercy and severity are not in tension; they are in order.
And the moment the curse peaks, the purpose-clause swerves to doxology: וְיֵדְעוּ כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מֹשֵׁל בְּיַעֲקֹב, "that they may *know* God rules in Jacob." The participle מֹשֵׁל is present and continuous — ongoing sovereignty. The ancestral name יַעֲקֹב, not יִשְׂרָאֵל, reaches to the primal root. The whole destructive prayer is subservient to this single cognitive goal: knowledge, spread לְאַפְסֵי הָאָרֶץ, "to the ends of the earth." This is the psalm's geographic climax — house → city → nations → world's end — and the second *Selah* falls here, mirroring the first (v. 6), again marking not a seam but a universalizing.
Frederick Douglass reached for the same logic in 1852, in "What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?" — insisting that only catastrophic moral pressure exposes entrenched evil:
> For it is not light that is needed, but fire; it is not the gentle shower, but thunder. We need the storm, the whirlwind, and the earthquake.
The psalmist, too, asks for fury rather than gentleness — not for the joy of destruction but because only overwhelming judgment makes the moral truth *known* "to the ends of the earth." Both reject the soft answer; both believe certain truths arrive only by fire.
**Verse 15**
וְיָשֻׁבוּ לָעֶרֶב, יֶהֱמוּ כַכָּלֶב, וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר׃
The dog-refrain returns, near-verbatim from v. 7 (only a prefixed vav and a vowel-shift, יָשֻׁבוּ for יָשׁוּבוּ). And the repetition *is* the argument. Structurally, the refrain circles back exactly as the dogs circle the city — the form imitates the content. But everything between vv. 7 and 15 has changed the frame: God has been invoked (v. 6), declared sovereign over all nations (vv. 9, 14), and the judgment pronounced (vv. 12–14). The same words now land into a God-ruled universe. They *sound* identical and yet are already hollow — the menace nullified before v. 16 even spells out the deflation.
The medievals read the second occurrence as turned to a curse. Malbim: the dogs that first prowled to *kill a righteous man's life* will now prowl as *starving* dogs — רַק הֵמָּה יְנִיעוּן לֶאֱכֹל... כְּכֶלֶב הָרָעֵב שֶׁסּוֹבֵב בַּלַּיְלָה בָּעִיר לְבַקֵּשׁ אוֹכֶל, "but they shall wander to eat... like the hungry dog that roams the city at night seeking food." Meiri reads the whole repetition as poetic inversion: where they once circled to *spy*, they will now circle לְבַקֵּשׁ לֶחֶם, "to beg bread." The circling is unchanged; only its object has collapsed from a man's life to a scrap of food.
**Verse 16**
הֵמָּה יְנִיעוּן לֶאֱכֹל; אִם־לֹא יִשְׂבְּעוּ וַיָּלִינוּ׃
The prayer of v. 12 is here visibly answered. There the speaker asked God הֲנִיעֵמוֹ, "make them wander"; here the dogs do exactly that, יְנִיעוּן, "they wander" (same root נוע) — petition enacted as description, the curse already taking effect within the strophe. The kinetic reversal is complete: the *purposeful circuit* of v. 7 (וִיסוֹבְבוּ, deliberate) has become *aimless searching* for food.
And the sound degrades with the status. The threatening יֶהֱמוּ, "they growl/roar" (v. 7), gives way to וַיָּלִינוּ — and this word is itself contested. From one root (לין) it means "they lodge overnight [unsatisfied]"; from another (לון) it means "they grumble, whine." The Septuagint reads γογγύσουσιν, "they shall grumble" — and, strikingly, that is the very verb used of Israel's *wilderness murmuring* (Exod 16). If that resonance is heard, the enemies have been reduced to the desert-grumbling posture of complaining Israel: the predator has become a whiner. The psalm's threatening *noise* has degraded, within the dog-motif, from a roar to a whimper.
The conditional אִם־לֹא יִשְׂבְּעוּ, "if they are not satisfied," keeps the hunger open-ended and fits the curse of Isaiah 56:11, וְהַכְּלָבִים עַזֵּי־נֶפֶשׁ לֹא יָדְעוּ שָׂבְעָה, "the dogs are greedy, they never know fullness." Note that Isaiah's dogs are עַזֵּי־נֶפֶשׁ, "fierce of appetite" — the same עז root the psalm has been confiscating. The enemies' fierceness now expresses itself only as an insatiable, unmet hunger.
The Brazilian poet Manuel Bandeira inverts this same scavenger-image to devastating effect in "O Bicho" (1947), written amid the squalor of Rio:
> O bicho não era um cão,
> Não era um gato,
> Não era um rato.
> O bicho, meu Deus, era um homem.
> "The animal was not a dog, / was not a cat, / was not a rat. / The animal, my God, was a man."
The psalm *weaponizes* the hungry scavenger to degrade its enemies into dog-like need. Bandeira reverses the vector entirely: his shock is the discovery that the scavenging creature *is human*, turning animalization from a curse upon an enemy into an indictment of the society that starves a man. Reading the two together exposes what the psalm's curse costs — it consigns a human being to the dog's hunger — and reminds us that the same image can damn either the scavenger or the world that made him scavenge.
**Verse 17**
וַאֲנִי אָשִׁיר עֻזֶּךָ, וַאֲרַנֵּן לַבֹּקֶר חַסְדֶּךָ; כִּי־הָיִיתָ מִשְׂגָּב לִי, וּמָנוֹס בְּיוֹם צַר־לִי׃
The whole poem turns on two unnecessary letters: וַאֲנִי, "But I." Grammatically the pronoun is redundant — the verb already carries "I." Its presence is rhetorical force, the lament's classic contrastive marker (compare Ps 13:6). They whine, unsatisfied (v. 16); *I* — emphatic — sing. The contrast is staged as pure pronoun-against-verb.
And the verbs themselves climb: אָשִׁיר, "I will sing," then וַאֲרַנֵּן, "I will *exult*" — a more exuberant noise than mere singing. The content is עֻזֶּךָ, "*Your* strength" — the עז note now in the second person, addressed directly to God, one step short of the full possession עֻזִּי of v. 18. The temporal word completes the antithesis: לַבֹּקֶר, "at morning." The dogs own the evening (vv. 7, 15); the psalmist owns the dawn. This is creation's own order — darkness yields to light — and a Psalter commonplace: בָּעֶרֶב יָלִין בֶּכִי וְלַבֹּקֶר רִנָּה, "weeping lodges at evening, but joy comes at morning" (Ps 30:6). The psalm has, in fact, distributed that proverb's two halves across its two parties: the dogs got the evening-lodging (וַיָּלִינוּ, v. 16), the speaker takes the morning-shout (וַאֲרַנֵּן לַבֹּקֶר). What one sufferer holds in sequence in Psalm 30 is here split between enemy and self.
Most arresting is the perfect tense: הָיִיתָ מִשְׂגָּב לִי, "You *have been* a fortress to me" — past, completed, certain — while the dogs are *still prowling* in vv. 15–16. This is faith spoken as accomplished fact before it is visible. And notice the grammar of the claim: when the psalm grounds its confidence, the perfect-tense verb belongs to God. The speaker's own response remains petition, resolve, and song still on the way.
**Verse 18**
עֻזִּי אֵלֶיךָ אֲזַמֵּרָה; כִּי־אֱלֹקִים מִשְׂגַּבִּי, אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי׃
The refrain returns one last time, and three small changes from v. 10 carry the psalm's entire argument. First, עֻזּוֹ, "*his* strength" (distant, third person), has become עֻזִּי, "*my* strength" — on that reading, the four-stage migration עַזִּים → עֻזּוֹ → עֻזֶּךָ → עֻזִּי reaches its end. What was first heard in the enemies' fierceness is now heard in the psalmist's own possession; recall that עַזִּים (`ʿaz-ZIY-m`) and עֻזִּי (`ʿuz-ZIY`) are aural near-twins, so the ear literally hears the attribute change hands by a turn of the vowels.
Second, and decisively, אֶשְׁמֹרָה, "I keep watch" (v. 10), has become אֲזַמֵּרָה, "I sing." Same cohortative form — same vow, same resolve — but the root has shifted from שׁמר (vigilance, the defensive crouch) to זמר (song, the secure overflow). And the two words are almost the same word: `'esh-MŌ-rāh` and `'ă-zam-MĒ-rāh` share their sibilant onset, their מ־ר core, their final `-ārāh`. The transformation that the entire poem has labored to earn is, in the end, the distance of a single consonant. Watching becomes singing. The hunted watcher at the window has become a singer at dawn. Ibn Ezra ties it back to the title's surveillance with one phrase: עֻזּוֹ אֵלֶיךָ אֶשְׁמֹרָה... כְּנֶגֶד וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ אֶת הַבַּיִת, "'for You I keep watch'... *against* 'and they watched the house.'"
Third, the addition. The psalm does not end on power-imagery. It ends on relationship: אֱלֹקֵי חַסְדִּי, "my faithful God" — three words that echo the Qere of v. 11 and frame the whole second half in an inclusio of covenant-loyalty. The deepest security, the poem's last word insists, is not עֹז (strength) but חֶסֶד (faithful love), and the possessive makes it personal — not a theological abstraction but *mine*.
Solomon Ibn Gabirol heard this ending and built a dawn-prayer on it. In eleventh-century Spain he opens his piyyut "Shaḥar Avakeshkha" with the psalm's own vocabulary of refuge and morning:
> שַׁחַר אֲבַקֶּשְׁךָ, צוּרִי וּמִשְׂגַּבִּי,
> אֶעֱרֹךְ לְפָנֶיךָ שַׁחְרִי וְגַם עַרְבִּי.
> "At dawn I seek You, my Rock and my *haven* (מִשְׂגַּבִּי); / I set before You my morning and also my evening."
Ibn Gabirol takes the two poles the psalm so carefully separates — the dogs' *evening*, the singer's *morning* — and folds them into a single repeatable discipline (שַׁחְרִי וְגַם עַרְבִּי, "my morning and also my evening"). What Psalm 59 sings once, after a single night's siege survived, the piyyut turns into a daily liturgy of seeking — emergency gratitude domesticated into devotion. And in doing so it preserves the psalm's deepest claim intact: that the proper response to being watched through the night is, at last, to sing.---
