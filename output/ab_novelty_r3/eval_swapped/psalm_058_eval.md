## Failure audit

### Commentary A
**OVERREACH**
- "Ancient Near Eastern curse texts include the wish that violators dissolve 'like a snail' — the same image as verse 9, drawn from the same repertoire of treaty-curses." The standard treaty-curse image is *wax/salt*; a "snail" treaty-curse is not well attested and is asserted as established fact. Likely overreach.
- "The verb is the one used of prophets beholding a vision" (חזה) — true that חזה is the prophetic-vision verb, but its application to plain seeing here is leaned on as if it must carry prophetic freight; mildly overstated (recurs in vv9, 11).

**FALSE PROFUNDITY**
- "This is what injustice feels like from the inside: a closed system in which the perpetrators are the only ones doing anything, and heaven says nothing." — vivid, but restates the grammatical observation already made.
- "Evil here is meticulous. It keeps good books." — epigrammatic restatement of the תְּפַלֵּסוּן gloss.

**LOST HEDGES**
- The "mute/deaf" reading of אֵלֶם is the author's preferred construal ("I find the 'mute' reading the most productive") yet the verse-by-verse later treats the silence/deafness link as if textually settled; the disputed lexical base should stay foregrounded.

### Commentary B
**OVERREACH**
- Hurston's title "Their Eyes Were Watching God" tied to the v10 whirlwind is a charming resonance but the connection to the psalm's imagery is thematic at best; the pairing's force leans on the coincidence of phrase. Borderline overreach (lowest-scoring item).

**FALSE PROFUNDITY**
- "The same patch of ground is problem in verse 3 and resolution in verse 12." — neat, but only restates the inclusio.
- "True speech restored, but spoken by no one in particular, by everyone." — cadence over content; repeats the no-plaintiff point.

**LOST HEDGES**
- The Bonhoeffer claim ("Psalm 58 answers before the question is asked") is interpretively bold; it is well-anchored historically but the leap from "speaker never claims to be righteous" to a deliberate answer to the innocence problem could be hedged.
- Generally B hedges better: the al-tashḥet/Isa 65:8 reading is explicitly marked "This is conjecture," which is exactly the calibration the rubric rewards.

## Coverage spot-check

**v3** — Both treat תְּפַלֵּסוּן richly with Ibn Ezra/Radak. A adds the sound-bridge and the יְדֵיכֶם bracket; B adds the precise Isaiah 26:7 co-occurrence of פלס + מֵישָׁרִים, which is the sharper, more checkable intertext. Edge B.

**v6** — Both strong on חוֹבֵר חֲבָרִים and the expert-charmer's failure. B's "gentlest whisper against a sealed ear" and its Radak citation ("they will not heed the voice of the righteous at law") tie the verse forward to the petition more tightly. Roughly even.

**v10** — Both honestly flag the crux. A and B both canvass pot/thorn readings. B adds the יָבִינוּ↔מְחֻכָּם irony (the "expert" swept off "before he understands"); A adds the second-person-suffix bracket. Both genuinely engaged; neither pads.

**v12 (late verse)** — Both excellent. A handles the plural אֱלֹקִים שֹׁפְטִים and the v2↔v12 reversal well. B does the same *and* closes the Solomon frame begun in the introduction (the psalm answers 1 Kgs 8:27 on its own terms) — a payoff A leaves on the table. Edge B.

Neither commentary visibly under-treats verses or pads routine glosses to feign depth. Both are dense and verse-attentive. The micro-difference is that B more consistently makes each verse serve a single governing thesis.
