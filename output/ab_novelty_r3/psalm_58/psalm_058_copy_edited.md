# Commentary on Psalm 58
---
## Introduction
Read the five psalms around it and you notice what Psalm 58 is missing. Its neighbors — 56, 57, 59, 60 — are saturated with a suffering "I: חׇנֵּנִי אֱלֹקִים, be gracious to me, O God (57:2); הַצִּילֵנִי מֵאֹיְבַי, deliver me from my enemies" (59:2). They beg, they bleed, they name their pursuers. Psalm 58 does none of this. Search its body — verses 2 through 12 — and you will not find a single first-person form: no אֲנִי, no "my enemies," not one verb in the first person singular. The speaker has vanished. Everything is third-person observation (רְשָׁעִים, "the wicked; צַדִּיק, the righteous one"), second-person accusation (תְּדַבֵּרוּן, "you speak; יְדֵיכֶם, your hands"), and exactly one turn upward to God (v. 7). In a cluster defined by personal persecution, this is the one psalm with no victim in it.
That single grammatical fact governs everything, and it is the argument of this essay: **Psalm 58 is a lawsuit with no plaintiff — and that absence is precisely what converts a complaint into a theodicy.**
The architecture is forensic, a courtroom proceeding in five movements. A superscription (v. 1). An indictment of corrupt judges, opened by a prosecutor's question (vv. 2–3). The presentation of evidence — the wicked's congenital depravity, sealed by the image of the deaf snake (vv. 4–6). The sentence: a seven-fold curse of dissolution, water and snail and stillbirth and whirlwind (vv. 7–10). And the verdict's aftermath, in which the righteous and then all humanity acknowledge that justice has been done (vv. 11–12). Binding the whole is a single repeated word, בָּאָרֶץ, "in the land / on the earth": in verse 3 it is the scene of the crime — חֲמַס יְדֵיכֶם, "the violence of your hands"; in verse 12 it is the seat of the court — אֱלֹקִים שֹׁפְטִים בָּאָרֶץ, "God judging on earth." The same patch of ground is problem in verse 3 and resolution in verse 12.
Now watch what the missing "I" accomplishes. Because no one in the poem claims to be the wronged party, the case never becomes *my* grievance; it becomes *the* grievance. The corruption is structural — judges who weigh out brutality with the very scales of justice — and the prosecutor refuses to personalize it. This is why the traditional commentators are forced to import a speaker from next door. Rashi, Radak, Metzudat David, and Meiri all anchor the psalm to the scene in 1 Samuel 26, where David lifts Saul's spear and water-jug and shouts across the valley, הֲלוֹא תַעֲנֶה אַבְנֵר, "Will you not answer, Abner?" — David rebuking the silent courtiers who let Saul hunt an innocent man. The instinct is sound: the psalm has a hole where its "I" should be, and the narrative fills it. But the hole is deliberate. Twentieth-century readers felt its force from the opposite direction. Dietrich Bonhoeffer, preaching this psalm at Finkenwalde in 1937 under the shadow of the Reich, wrestled with what he called the problem of innocence: who is clean enough to pray a curse like this? Psalm 58 answers before the question is asked. The speaker never claims to be the righteous one. צַדִּיק, "the righteous one," is a third party whom God vindicates in verse 11 — not the voice we have been listening to.
What, then, does the voice we *have* been listening to actually do? It speaks — and the whole poem turns out to be organized around speech and its failures. The judges "speak" justice they do not practice (v. 2). The wicked are דֹּבְרֵי כָזָב, "speakers of lies," from the womb (v. 4). Then comes the hinge image: a פֶּתֶן חֵרֵשׁ, "a deaf cobra," that יַאְטֵם אָזְנוֹ, "stops its ear," against the most skilled charmer alive (vv. 5–6). Corrupt speech has hardened into willed deafness — evil that will not *hear*. The sentence that follows (vv. 7–10) is, read this way, a program of enforced silence: smash the teeth, melt them like water, sweep them off before they can even understand. And the last word goes to וְיֹאמַר אָדָם, "humanity will say" (v. 12) — true speech restored, but spoken by no one in particular, by everyone. The poem moves from corrupt speech through willed deafness through enforced silence to honest confession, and the speaker who began the prosecution gracefully exits before the verdict, ceding the closing line to the human race.
There is a deeper resonance in the word that opens the whole thing. הַאֻמְנָם, "is it really true?", is rare. Its most famous home is Solomon's prayer at the dedication of the Temple: הַאֻמְנָם יֵשֵׁב אֱלֹקִים עַל־הָאָרֶץ, "Will God really dwell on the earth?" (1 Kgs 8:27; cf. 2 Chr 6:18, which adds אֶת־הָאָדָם, "with man"). Solomon used it to voice doubt about God's earthly presence. Psalm 58 opens with the same skeptical particle and closes by answering it in the same terms — אֱלֹקִים, "God; אָדָם, man; בָּאָרֶץ, on the earth." The poem that begins "Is it really so?" ends אַךְ יֵשׁ־אֱלֹקִים שֹׁפְטִים בָּאָרֶץ, "there is, indeed, a God judging on earth." The בָּאָרֶץ inclusio is not merely an internal frame; it is the refutation of an ancient doubt. Does God govern down here, where the courts are crooked and the violent prosper? The psalm spends ten verses making the doubt feel unbearable, then plants the answer on the very ground where the crime occurred.
Two final craft choices seal the reading. First, the divine names. They appear exactly three times, all at the two hinges: both אֱלֹקִים and ה׳ at the pivot to petition in verse 7, and אֱלֹקִים again in the closing verdict of verse 12. Across the curse-cascade (vv. 8–10), where God is the likely unnamed "he" who treads the bow and whirls the wicked away, the executor is never named. The Name commissions the destruction and pronounces the verdict but does not soil itself in the act. Second — and this is what makes the psalm impossible to un-see once noticed — the poet withholds his own face for the same reason he withholds God's hands during the violence. Vengeance is handed *upward*; the human keeps clear of it.
That is the single thing to carry away. Psalm 58 is the rarest kind of curse: an imprecation with no first-person voice, prosecuted by a speaker who refuses to call himself the victim — which is exactly why, three thousand years on, anyone bewildered by injustice can step into its silence and find the case already made.

#### Full psalm
Psalm 58 has no place in the fixed daily, Shabbat, or festival liturgy of any major rite. Its unrelenting second-person indictment and its seven-fold curse have made it a poor fit for communal prayer — one does not easily worship in the mode of הַאֻמְנָם אֵלֶם צֶדֶק תְּדַבֵּרוּן, "do you really decree what is just?" Its liturgical afterlife is therefore almost entirely fragmentary, and where the whole psalm does surface, it is in para-liturgical and magical settings rather than statutory ones.
The most striking later reuse is in **Perek Shirah**, the devotional "Song of Creation" printed at the back of many siddurim (including the Hasidic Nusach Sefard) and recited, where observed at all, as a private supplement after the weekday Shacharit. There verse 9 is assigned to the snail as its "song: שַׁבְּלוּל אוֹמֵר כְּמוֹ שַׁבְּלוּל תֶּמֶס יַהֲלֹךְ נֵפֶל אֵשֶׁת בַּל חָזוּ שָׁמֶשׁ, The snail says: 'Like a snail that melts away as it moves; like a stillbirth, may they never see the sun.'" Why a curse should be a creature's hymn of praise looks inexplicable until one recalls that שַׁבְּלוּל, "snail," is an absolute hapax: this is the *only* verse in the entire Hebrew Bible that contains the word. Perek Shirah pairs each creature with a verse that names it (the ant beside it gets לֵךְ אֶל נְמָלָה, "go to the ant"); the snail had no other verse to be given.
#### Key verses and phrases
Verse 7's הֲרֹס שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ, "smash their teeth in their mouth," enters the **Ashkenazi Yom Kippur Shacharit**, embedded in a selichah within the fourth blessing of the Amidah, punctuated by the refrain חָטָאנוּ, "we have sinned." The payyetan reworks the verse — הֲרֹס שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ וְיֻדְמָמוּ, "smash their teeth in their mouth and let them be silenced" — adding the verb וְיֻדְמָמוּ for rhyme. The addition, ostensibly metrical, in fact reads the psalm's own logic correctly: to destroy the mouth's weapon is to silence the mouth. The Day of Atonement redirects the biblical curse against generic רְשָׁעִים into a plea against those who לָקַחַת נַפְשִׁי זָמָמוּ, "plotted to seize my soul," even as the worshipper confesses that his own sin helped empower them.
Beyond fixed prayer, the medieval **Shimmush Tehillim** prescribes the whole psalm apotropaically — against attacking dogs, and (drawing on נֵפֶל, "stillbirth," in v. 9) as an amulet against miscarriage, a literalizing reversal that turns the curse of failed birth into a charm for safe birth.

## Psalm 58

1. לַמְנַצֵּ֥חַ אַל־תַּשְׁחֵ֗ת לְדָוִ֥ד מִכְתָּֽם׃‎		For the leader; al tashḥeth. Of David. A michtam.
2. הַאֻמְנָ֗ם אֵ֣לֶם צֶ֭דֶק תְּדַבֵּר֑וּן מֵישָׁרִ֥ים תִּ֝שְׁפְּט֗וּ בְּנֵ֣י אָדָֽם׃‎		O mighty ones,-a do you really decree what is just?
Do you judge mankind with equity?
3. אַף־בְּלֵב֮ עוֹלֹ֢ת תִּפְעָ֫ל֥וּן בָּאָ֡רֶץ חֲמַ֥ס יְ֝דֵיכֶ֗ם תְּפַלֵּסֽוּן׃‎		In your minds you devise wrongdoing in the land;
with your hands you deal out lawlessness.-a
4. זֹ֣רוּ רְשָׁעִ֣ים מֵרָ֑חֶם תָּע֥וּ מִ֝בֶּ֗טֶן דֹּבְרֵ֥י כָזָֽב׃‎		The wicked are defiant from birth;
the liars go astray from the womb.

a deaf viper that stops its ears
6. אֲשֶׁ֣ר לֹֽא־יִ֭שְׁמַע לְק֣וֹל מְלַחֲשִׁ֑ים חוֹבֵ֖ר חֲבָרִ֣ים מְחֻכָּֽם׃‎		so as not to hear the voice of charmers
or the expert mutterer of spells.
7. אֱֽלֹקִ֗ים הֲרׇס־שִׁנֵּ֥ימֽוֹ בְּפִ֑ימוֹ מַלְתְּע֥וֹת כְּ֝פִירִ֗ים נְתֹ֣ץ ׀ ה׳׃‎		O God, smash their teeth in their mouth;
shatter the fangs of lions, O LORD;
8. יִמָּאֲס֣וּ כְמוֹ־מַ֭יִם יִתְהַלְּכוּ־לָ֑מוֹ יִדְרֹ֥ךְ חִ֝צָּ֗ו כְּמ֣וֹ יִתְמֹלָֽלוּ׃‎		let them melt, let them vanish like water;
let Him aim His arrows that they be cut down;
9. כְּמ֣וֹ שַׁ֭בְּלוּל תֶּ֣מֶס יַהֲלֹ֑ךְ נֵ֥פֶל אֵ֝֗שֶׁת בַּל־חָ֥זוּ שָֽׁמֶשׁ׃‎		like a snail that melts away as it moves;-a

there is, indeed, divine justice on earth.”

## Verse-by-Verse Commentary
**Verse 1**
לַמְנַצֵּ֥חַ אַל־תַּשְׁחֵ֗ת לְדָוִ֥ד מִכְתָּֽם׃
Two opaque terms frame a psalm of pure transparency of feeling. The tune-name אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת, "do not destroy," heads a poem whose entire body petitions for destruction — a juxtaposition no reader can miss. The phrase is shared with Psalms 57, 59, and 75, all crisis psalms, which suggests an editorial cluster. But the words have a richer biblical home. In Isaiah 65:8 the vintner says of a cluster of grapes, אַל־תַּשְׁחִיתֵהוּ כִּי בְרָכָה בּוֹ, "do not destroy it, for a blessing is within it." If our superscription echoes that, the tension is not merely ironic; it may be answered three verses later. The Isaianic restraint applies wherever there is "a blessing within" worth preserving — and verse 4 will declare these wicked corrupt מֵרָחֶם, "from the womb." Perhaps the one case where "do not destroy" yields to total destruction is the entity with no blessing within it to spare. This is conjecture, since the Isaiah echo is itself uncertain, but it would make the superscription not a contradiction but the psalm's opening legal premise.
מִכְתָּם, "michtam," is genuinely unknown, occurring only in Psalms 16 and 56–60. The candidates: כֶּתֶם, "gold" (a golden poem); כתב, "to write" (an inscription); or an Akkadian root for covering and atonement. The Septuagint chose the writing tradition decisively — εἰς στηλογραφίαν, "for a stele-inscription" — reading the term as a monument, a permanent public testimony engraved in stone. Rashi, characteristically, declines the etymological gamble and calls it simply שֵׁם נְעִימוּת שִׁיר, "the name of the melody of a song." The LXX reading is the more suggestive: a psalm that ends by having all humanity confess justice "on earth" is, fittingly, the kind of utterance one would carve in stone.
**Verse 2**
הַאֻמְנָ֗ם אֵ֣לֶם צֶ֭דֶק תְּדַבֵּר֑וּן; מֵישָׁרִ֥ים תִּ֝שְׁפְּט֗וּ בְּנֵ֣י אָדָֽם׃
The opening word is a prosecutor's snare. הַאֻמְנָם, "is it really true?", is a rare interrogative, and it often appears in skeptical or challenging questions — Balak's sarcasm to Balaam (Num 22:37), Solomon's doubt at the Temple (1 Kgs 8:27). It expects the answer "no," and verse 3 supplies it with אַף, "indeed!" The question is a trap that opens the floor of the indictment.
The crux of the whole psalm sits in the second word, אֵלֶם. The consonantal text supports three readings, and they reshape the entire register. Read אֵלִם, "gods / mighty ones," and the addressees are a corrupt divine council, as in Psalm 82 — cosmic register. BDB, with characteristic caution, lists the word under אֵלֶם, "silence," reading the line as an accusation that the *silent* ones speak corruption. The Septuagint did something stranger: it dropped the word entirely and recast the line as straightforward second-person address — εἰ ἀληθῶς ἄρα δικαιοσύνην λαλεῖτε, "if truly, then, you speak righteousness." The translator either had a different Hebrew text or found the word untranslatable and rebuilt the sentence.
I commit to the "silence" reading, because it launches the poem's master arc. If the charge is that mute ones nonetheless *speak* (תְּדַבֵּרוּן) corruption, then verse 2 plants the seed of the speech-and-silence trajectory that organizes everything after it. The emendation to "gods" gives a grander cosmos but loses the paradox. The Talmud, mining the same ambiguity, hears in אֵלֶם a counsel of conduct: מַה אוּמְנוּתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה? יָשִׂים עַצְמוֹ כְּאִלֵּם, "what is a person's craft in this world? Let him make himself like a mute" — yet, the Gemara adds, not regarding words of Torah, for the verse continues צֶדֶק תְּדַבֵּרוּן, "you shall speak righteousness" (Chullin 89a). Speech withheld and speech required: the rabbinic ear already heard the tension the poet built.
The vocabulary of the second colon is the language of ideal governance — מֵישָׁרִים, "equity," and שׁפט, "judge" — deployed to frame its inversion. The bitterness is the point: the standard exists; these judges merely refuse to apply it.
**Verse 3**
אַף־בְּלֵב֮ עוֹלֹ֢ת תִּפְעָ֫ל֥וּן; בָּאָ֡רֶץ חֲמַ֥ס יְ֝דֵיכֶ֗ם תְּפַלֵּסֽוּן׃
The verse moves from heart to hand — בְּלֵב, "in the heart," to יְדֵיכֶם, "your hands" — tracing injustice from conception to execution. Radak captures the anatomy precisely: though the heart only thinks and does not act, כָּל כָּךְ יַחְשׁוֹב לְבַבְכֶם הָעָוֶל תָּמִיד כְּאִילוּ הוּא פּוֹעֵל אוֹתוֹ, "your heart so constantly devises wrong that it is as if it were performing it." Corruption is cognitive before it is kinetic.
The detonating word is תְּפַלֵּסוּן, "you weigh out." The image belongs to the world of measured precision — the noun פֶּלֶס is a balance (Isa 40:12), and the verb can mean to level a path (Prov 4:26; Isa 26:7). Psalm 58 perverts the whole field: these judges weigh out *violence* with the meticulous care one would bring to weighing out justice. Ibn Ezra reads the irony exactly — תִּשְׁקְלוּ אוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁלֹּא יַרְגִּישׁ אָדָם בֶּחָמָס, "you weigh it so finely that no one even feels the violence being done." This is not careless corruption; it is expert calibration.
There is a sharper target. The verb פלס and the noun מֵישָׁרִים of verse 2 occur *together* in one verse elsewhere: Isaiah 26:7, אֹרַח לַצַּדִּיק מֵישָׁרִים יָשָׁר מַעְגַּל צַדִּיק תְּפַלֵּס, "the path of the righteous is level; You make smooth the track of the righteous." Isaiah's image is God grading the road for the righteous to walk. Psalm 58 takes both of Isaiah's words and turns them inside out: the judges who should judge with מֵישָׁרִים instead תְּפַלֵּסוּן not a righteous path but חָמָס, "violence."
W. H. Auden caught this exact hypocrisy — justice defined by its own circular authority — in "Law Like Love" (1940), written as Europe slid into war and law itself seemed up for grabs:
> Law, says the judge as he looks down his nose,
> Speaking clearly and most severely,
> Law is as I've told you before, ...
> Law is The Law.
Auden's judge weaponizes the *form* of jurisprudence — the severe tone, the tautology — exactly as the psalm's rulers weaponize the scale. The shared move is the perversion of justice's own instruments by the very people sworn to wield them. The difference: Auden's satire is resigned, a shrug at human nature; the psalm's is a formal charge, the first count in a trial that ends in conviction.
The phrase חֲמַס, "violence," also opens a sound-bridge: its ḥ-m cluster will return in verse 5 as חֲמַת, "venom." The poet is preparing to argue, by sound, that judicial violence *is* poison.
**Verse 4**
זֹ֣רוּ רְשָׁעִ֣ים מֵרָ֑חֶם; תָּע֥וּ מִ֝בֶּ֗טֶן דֹּבְרֵ֥י כָזָֽב׃
The evidence phase opens with an ontological claim: the wicked are wrong from the start. The two halves are synonymous parallelism intensifying toward origin — מֵרָחֶם, "from the womb," matched by מִבֶּטֶן, "from the belly." This opens a birth-imagery arc: those born from the womb here (v. 4) will be wished into the condition of the never-born stillbirth in verse 9.
The opening verb זֹרוּ is ambiguous. It can derive from זוּר, "to be estranged," or from זָרָה, "to scatter." The Septuagint resolved it decisively — ἀπηλλοτριώθησαν, "they were alienated" — making a claim about being, not behavior: the wicked are estranged *creatures*, not merely actors gone wrong. Rashi presses the theology, reading them as estranged from God in the womb כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁעָשָׂה עֵשָׂו, "in the way Esau did," already struggling against Jacob in Rebekah's belly.
Malbim draws the sharpest distinction, and it darkens the verse considerably. Open sinners, he says, are זֹרוּ מֵרָחֶם — corrupted *after* birth, by bad company and teachers, for human nature inclines to justice. But the דֹּבְרֵי כָזָב, "speakers of lies," are תָּעוּ מִבֶּטֶן — astray even before emerging, because רְעָתָם אֵינוֹ דָּבָר מִקְרִי רַק עַצְמִי וְטִבְעִי לָהֶם, "their evil is not accidental but essential and natural to them." This is the psalm's hardest assertion: a corruption that precedes choice.
Xunzi, the Confucian philosopher writing around 300 BCE, made the same anthropological diagnosis but drew the opposite conclusion:
> 人之性惡，其善者偽也
> "Human nature is evil; its goodness derives from conscious activity."
For Xunzi this is the universal human default, and the remedy is education — ritual and law bend the crooked timber straight. The psalm refuses that hope for *these* wicked. Their depravity is not a condition to be cultivated out of them but a fact to be eradicated, which is why the next move is not pedagogy but the deaf snake of verse 5: there is no charming this serpent.
**Verse 5**
חֲמַת־לָ֗מוֹ כִּדְמ֥וּת חֲמַת־נָחָ֑שׁ; כְּמוֹ־פֶ֥תֶן חֵ֝רֵ֗שׁ יַאְטֵ֥ם אׇזְנֽוֹ׃
The verse runs on a double meaning. חֲמַת means both "venom" and "wrath / heat" (from חֵמָה). Both senses fire at once: the judges' rage *is* their poison. And the word completes the sound-bridge from verse 3 — חָמָס, "violence," now sounds as חֲמַת, "venom." The ḥ-m cluster fuses the two by ear, arguing that judicial violence is a toxin in the social body. Psalm 140 makes the same equation explicit: חֲמַת עַכְשׁוּב תַּחַת שְׂפָתֵימוֹ, "the venom of an adder is under their lips" (140:4).
The comparison particle is oddly elaborate. The poet writes not the simple כְּמוֹ, "like," but כִּדְמוּת, "like the likeness of." The redundancy — "their venom is like the likeness of snake venom" — keeps the image slightly distanced, as though the speaker were reaching for the nearest available comparison rather than equating the two flatly.
Then the verse pivots from the expected danger to the surprising one. Not the venom but the *deafness* is the point: a פֶּתֶן חֵרֵשׁ, "deaf cobra," that יַאְטֵם אָזְנוֹ, "stops its ear." The verb אטם is active and deliberate — used for sealing channels and stopping ears against the cry of the poor (Prov 21:13). This snake does not passively fail to hear; it plugs its own ear. Rashi preserves an old naturalist lore: the aging snake goes deaf in one ear and presses the other into the dust שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁמַע אֶת הַלַּחַשׁ, "so as not to hear the incantation" of the charmer adjuring it to do no harm. The theology is chilling: this evil is not merely dangerous but uncorrectable, immune to every word of persuasion. Jeremiah supplies the exact image of judgment-by-uncharmable-snake: צִפְעֹנִים אֲשֶׁר אֵין־לָהֶם לָחַשׁ, "adders that cannot be charmed" (Jer 8:17).
There is a quieter biblical irony beneath the snake. The very phrase אֹטֵם אָזְנוֹ, "stopping his ear," describes the *righteous* man in Isaiah 33:15 — the one who אֹטֵם אָזְנוֹ מִשְּׁמֹעַ דָּמִים, "stops his ear from hearing of bloodshed." The sage's virtuous deafness and the snake's vicious deafness use the same gesture. Verse 5 exploits exactly that ambivalence: the wicked's refusal to hear *looks* like the saint's, and is its perversion.
**Verse 6**
אֲשֶׁ֣ר לֹֽא־יִ֭שְׁמַע לְק֣וֹל מְלַחֲשִׁ֑ים; חוֹבֵ֖ר חֲבָרִ֣ים מְחֻכָּֽם׃
The verse completes the snake portrait and, with it, the evidence phase. מְלַחֲשִׁים, "whisperers," are snake-charmers, named for the hiss-like whisper (לחש) of their craft. The acoustic irony is fine-tuned: the charmer deploys the quietest, most intimate sound — a whisper — against an ear that has sealed itself to all sound. Even the gentlest, most refined attempt at persuasion is refused.
The second colon is built on an emphatic construction. חוֹבֵר חֲבָרִים is a cognate accusative — literally "a binder of bindings," one who ties magic knots — the same forbidden practitioner listed in Deuteronomy 18:11, חֹבֵר חָבֶר. And מְחֻכָּם, "expert," is a passive participle: not naturally clever but *made* skilled, a mastered craft. The poet stacks every available marker of maximum competence — and deploys them precisely at the moment of total failure. This is the structural argument of the whole evidence section, and the commentators see it. Metzudat David: even if the charmer הוּא מְחוּכָּם מְאֹד בִּדְבַר הַלַּחַשׁ, "is very expert in the matter of charming, עַכֵּז לֹא יִשְׁמַע, still it will not hear." Radak draws the conclusion that licenses verse 7: כִּי אֵלֶּה הָאֲנָשִׁים לֹא יִשְׁמְעוּ לְקוֹל הַצַּדִּיק בַּמִּשְׁפָּט, "for these men will not heed the voice of the righteous one at law." The peak of human wisdom is exhausted against willful deafness. Only God can act now.
The Septuagint took the charmer in a different direction, rendering the line with φάρμακα language, associated with potions and spells, rather than pure acoustic incantation — re-importing the poison vocabulary of verse 5 and shifting the magic from sound to substance.
The ancient Mediterranean knew the successful version of this scene, which makes the psalm's failure register all the more. In Apollonius of Rhodes' *Argonautica* (3rd c. BCE), Medea charms the sleepless dragon guarding the fleece:
> ἡ δέ μιν... γλυκερῇ ἐνοπῇ θέλγε
> "She charmed him with her sweet voice... and sleep, cast upon him by her melodies, relaxed his dark head."
Medea's whisper *works*: human mystical skill masters natural chaos, and the monster's eyes close. The psalm summons exactly this trope of the serpent-tamer — and then introduces its terrifying anomaly. The snake that stops its own ears cannot be sung to sleep. Where Medea's art represents the triumph of human technique over danger, the deaf cobra represents an evil so absolute that it has unplugged itself from the very mechanism of control. The charmer is presented as maximally expert; it makes no difference at all.
**Verse 7**
אֱֽלֹקִ֗ים הֲרׇס־שִׁנֵּ֥ימֽוֹ בְּפִ֑ימוֹ; מַלְתְּע֥וֹת כְּ֝פִירִ֗ים נְתֹ֣ץ ׀ ה׳׃
This is the pivot. Every prior verse addressed human beings; here, for the first time, the poem turns upward. Both divine names appear in a single verse — אֱלֹקִים, "God," opening, and ה׳, the covenant name, closing — God as universal power and covenant partner summoned at once. Nils Lund long ago noticed the strict chiasm: the two violent verbs, הֲרֹס, "smash," and נְתֹץ, "shatter," sit bracketed *inside* the divine names, with the lion's fangs at the center. The structure makes a theological claim by shape alone: the destruction is enclosed within God, framed as strictly His prerogative.
But the distribution runs wider than verse 7. The divine names occur only three times in the whole psalm — twice here, once in verse 12. Across the curse that follows (vv. 8–10), where God is the likely actor who treads the bow and whirls the wicked away, the executor is never named. The Name commissions the violence at verse 7 and pronounces the verdict at verse 12, but goes unnamed throughout the act itself.
The verb הֲרֹס is architectural demolition vocabulary — used for tearing down walls and ramparts (Isa 22:19). Applied to teeth, it treats the mouth as a fortress to be razed. The phrase שִׁנֵּימוֹ בְּפִימוֹ, "their teeth in their mouth," looks redundant — where else are teeth? — but the in-situ specification matters: destroy the weapon at the very site of its use, the mouth that produced the corrupt speech of verses 2–4. Malbim makes the predation explicit: God should shatter the fangs בַּל יוּכְלוּ לֶאֱחֹז אֶת הַטֶּרֶף, "so they cannot seize the prey." The zoology escalates even as the curse begins: the snake of verses 5–6 becomes a כְּפִיר, a young lion, the fiercest and hungriest of predators. The image of broken predator-teeth as rescue is biblical convention — Psalm 3:8, שִׁנֵּי רְשָׁעִים שִׁבַּרְתָּ, "You have broken the teeth of the wicked," and Job 29:17, where breaking the jaws of the unrighteous means וּמִשִּׁנָּיו אַשְׁלִיךְ טָרֶף, "I cast the prey from his teeth." To disarm the mouth is to free the victim.
**Verse 8**
יִמָּאֲס֣וּ כְמוֹ־מַ֭יִם יִתְהַלְּכוּ־לָ֑מוֹ; יִדְרֹ֥ךְ חִ֝צָּ֗ו כְּמ֣וֹ יִתְמֹלָֽלוּ׃
The seven-fold dissolution begins, and the first image is the loss of form itself: יִמָּאֲסוּ כְמוֹ־מַיִם, "let them melt like water." The verb is usually מָאַס, "be rejected"; here the medieval grammarians read it as a variant of מסס, "melt" — Radak: כְּמוֹ יִמָּסְסוּ, the wicked rendered as nothing. The water יִתְהַלְּכוּ, "walks away," disappearing downstream and leaving no trace. This launches a quiet semantic move that the next verse completes: the root הלך, ordinarily "to go," becomes the psalm's verb of *vanishing*. To proceed is to drain away.
The second colon is one of the psalm's hard knots. יִדְרֹךְ חִצָּו, "he treads his arrows," omits the expected object — one normally "treads" (draws) a bow, not arrows. And כְּמוֹ, "like," stands before a finite verb (יִתְמֹלָלוּ, "they wither / are cut off"), which is irregular Hebrew. The commentators split on the archer's identity. Rashi and Metzudat David read God: הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְנֶגְדָּם, "the Holy One aims at them, so that they be cut off." Radak demythologizes — the arrows are the wicked's own evil words, the bow their mouth: הַקֶּשֶׁת הוּא הַפֶּה. The context favors God as archer: the surrounding verbs are all judgment falling *upon* the wicked.
The water image cuts against the grain of its usual biblical force. Water is normally the vehicle of *overwhelming* power — He comes כַנָּהָר צָר, "like a pent-up stream" (Isa 59:19). Psalm 58 inverts it: here water signifies not force but the loss of all force, cohesion draining away. Rashi adds a poignant gloss, reading the melting as the wicked dissolving in their own dread, וּכְמוֹ בַמַּיִם יִתְהַלָּכוּ, like the knees that "go to water" with terror in Ezekiel 7:17.
**Verse 9**
כְּמ֣וֹ שַׁ֭בְּלוּל תֶּ֣מֶס יַהֲלֹ֑ךְ; נֵ֥פֶל אֵ֝֗שֶׁת בַּל־חָ֥זוּ שָֽׁמֶשׁ׃
Two images of coming-to-nothing, one biological and one human. שַׁבְּלוּל, "snail," is an absolute hapax — the only occurrence in the Bible — and the ancient folk-belief was that the snail dissolves into its own slime-trail as it crawls. תֶּמֶס יַהֲלֹךְ, "it melts as it goes," completes the move begun in verse 8: forward motion *is* the means of disappearance, the snail consumed in the very act of proceeding. The Septuagint, faced with the unknown creature, substituted κηρός, "wax" — either a different Hebrew text or a translator reaching for a familiar dissolution-image, as in the wicked who perish כְּהִמֵּס דּוֹנַג מִפְּנֵי־אֵשׁ, "as wax melts before fire" (Ps 68:3).
The hapax also solves a small liturgical puzzle. Perek Shirah, the devotional bestiary, assigns this grim verse to the snail as its hymn — which looks bizarre until one realizes that, since this is the only verse in Scripture naming a snail, the snail had no other verse to sing. The curse is incidental to a forced lexical match.
The second image is נֵפֶל אֵשֶׁת, "a woman's stillbirth," that בַּל־חָזוּ שָׁמֶשׁ, "never saw the sun." This closes the birth-imagery arc with verse 4: those born wicked from the womb are now wished into the state of the never-born. The verb חָזוּ, "saw," is the visionary חזה, not ordinary רָאָה — and it sets up an axis with verse 11. The stillbirth never *sees* (חזה) the light; the righteous will *behold* (חזה) vengeance. Entry into the moral order is figured as the capacity to see; erasure is to never see at all.
The verse has gathered a remarkable poetic afterlife on its stillbirth image, and the moderns turn its violence into elegy. Gwendolyn Brooks' "the mother" (1945) anchors the unborn in permanent ghostly presence:
> Abortions will not let you forget.
> You remember the children you got that you did not get,
> The damp small pulps with a little or with no hair,
> The singers and workers that never handled the air.
Brooks' heavy spondees — "children you got that you did not get" — hold the unborn in agonized remembrance, mourning exactly those who "never handled the air." The psalmist reaches for the identical biological tragedy — the one who "never saw the sun" — but weaponizes it as triumphant curse. Where Brooks grieves the failure to enter life as irreplaceable loss, the psalmist *desires* it for the wicked: complete, retroactive un-creation. The same image, the same not-seeing — and a chasm between the poet who would give anything to undo the erasure and the poet who prays for it. The difference measures the distance between elegy and imprecation.
**Verse 10**
בְּטֶ֤רֶם ׀ יָבִ֣ינוּ סִּירֹתֵכֶ֣ם אָטָ֑ד; כְּמוֹ־חַ֥י כְּמוֹ־חָ֝ר֗וֹן יִשְׂעָרֶֽנּוּ׃
Widely regarded as the most difficult verse in the Psalter, and the translations diverge wildly. The crux is סִּירֹתֵכֶם, which can mean "your thorns" or "your cooking-pots." On the thorn reading (Rashi): בְּטֶרֶם יֵדְעוּ קוֹצִים רַכִּים שֶׁלָּכֶם לִהְיוֹת אֲטָדִים קָשִׁים, "before your tender briars know to become hard brambles" — the wicked cut down before they mature. On the pot reading (Malbim, Radak), the image is culinary: thorns crackle as fuel under a pot, and judgment strikes בְּטֶרֶם יָבִינוּ, "before they perceive it" — before the pot even feels the heat, while the meat is still raw, the storm sweeps it away. The pot reading likely glances at Ecclesiastes 7:6, כְּקוֹל הַסִּירִים תַּחַת הַסִּיר, "like the crackling of thorns under a pot," the emblem of brief, loud, pointless combustion.
Either way, the irony bites: the verb יָבִינוּ, "understand," recalls the wicked who were מְחֻכָּם, "expert" (v. 6), and who calibrated their injustice with such precision (v. 3). All that intelligence avails nothing; they are swept off בְּטֶרֶם יָבִינוּ, before they grasp what is happening.
The verse then shifts person mid-stream — from second-person "your thorns" to a third-person singular object in יִשְׂעָרֶנּוּ, "he whirls *him* away." The commentators take this in stride; Meiri simply notes דֶּרֶךְ הַנְּבוּאוֹת לְדַבֵּר פַּעַם לָשׁוֹן יָחִיד פַּעַם לָשׁוֹן רַבִּים, "it is the way of prophecy to speak now in singular, now in plural." The verb סער is storm-theophany vocabulary — the LORD whose way is בְּסוּפָה וּבִשְׂעָרָה, "in whirlwind and storm" (Nah 1:3). Rabbinic readers also linked אָטָד with גֹּרֶן הָאָטָד, "the threshing-floor of Atad" in Genesis 50:10, helping draw the next verse into the aggadah of Esau's death at Jacob's burial.
Zora Neale Hurston gave the verse's whirlwind its most concentrated modern form in *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (1937), in the hurricane that levels the Everglades:
> The wind came back with triple fury, and put out the light for the last time... their eyes were watching God.
Hurston's "triple fury" personifies the storm as overwhelming divine force exposing the fragility of all human power — exactly the psalm's whirlwind that uproots the wicked כְּמוֹ חָרוֹן, "in fury," before their thorns can take hold. Both make the sudden storm the cosmic equalizer, wrenching authority back from corrupt human hierarchies to the untamable judgment of God. The difference is direction: Hurston's characters cower *under* the wind, watching to see if it means them; the psalm's wicked are its target, weeded out of the ground before they understand they were planted.
**Verse 11**
יִשְׂמַ֣ח צַ֭דִּיק כִּי־חָזָ֣ה נָקָ֑ם; פְּעָמָ֥יו יִ֝רְחַ֗ץ בְּדַ֣ם הָרָשָֽׁע׃
The verdict's aftermath. The צַדִּיק, "righteous one," appears for the first time — and crucially, as a third party. He is not the speaker; he is the one God has been vindicating. Ibn Ezra ties him precisely back to the indictment: יִשְׂמַח צַדִּיק כְּנֶגֶד אֵלֶם צֶדֶק, "the righteous rejoices, answering the silenced justice" of verse 2. The justice that the mute judges refused to speak is now seen.
The verb is again חָזָה, the prophetic "behold" of Isaiah, Micah, Amos — not ordinary seeing. The righteous perceives נָקָם, "vengeance," as a prophetic event, divinely ordained and now witnessed. This completes the vision-axis: the stillbirth בַּל־חָזוּ שָׁמֶשׁ, "never saw the sun" (v. 9); the righteous כִּי־חָזָה נָקָם, "has seen vengeance." The same faculty, allotted in opposite directions. And נָקָם is covenantal, not personal — the legal vengeance God reserves to Himself (Deut 32:35), not private vendetta.
The second image is deliberately shocking: פְּעָמָיו יִרְחַץ בְּדַם הָרָשָׁע, "he bathes his feet in the blood of the wicked." This is ANE victory-iconography, wading through the blood of the defeated — the same convention as Isaiah's blood-stained warrior (Isa 63:1–3). Its closest mythic parallel is the Ugaritic warrior-goddess Anat (KTU 1.3 II), who wades knee-deep in the gore of her enemies. The psalm appropriates the gore but strips the human of any active role: the righteous merely *observes* and bathes in the aftermath. God executes; the righteous witnesses. The Septuagint, perhaps uncomfortable, reads χεῖρας, "hands," for "feet" — possibly aligning with clean-hands imagery (Ps 24:4). The Talmud attaches the verse to the grim scene of Hushim son of Dan beheading Esau at Jacob's burial, so that נָתְרָן עֵינֵיהּ וְנָפְלוּ אַכַּרְעָא דְיַעֲקֹב, "his eyes fell out and dropped at Jacob's feet," and Jacob opened his eyes and smiled — בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה נִתְקַיְּימָה נְבוּאָתָהּ שֶׁל רִבְקָה, fulfilling the verse (Sotah 13a).
The most powerful modern response refuses the verse entirely. Haim Nachman Bialik, writing "On the Slaughter" (1903) in the wake of the Kishinev pogrom, turns the imprecatory mode against itself:
> וְאָרוּר הָאוֹמֵר: נְקֹם!
> נְקָמָה כָזֹאת, נִקְמַת דַּם יֶלֶד קָטָן
> עוֹד לֹא-בָרָא הַשָּׂטָן
> "And cursed be he who says: Avenge! Such vengeance, vengeance for the blood of a small child, Satan himself has not yet created."
Bialik takes the psalm's own vocabulary — נָקָם, דָם — and curses the very impulse the psalm promises will bring the righteous joy. Where the psalmist guarantees emotional closure (יִשְׂמַח צַדִּיק, "the righteous will rejoice") in the blood of the wicked, Bialik insists that for the blood of a murdered child no vengeance is even conceivable, leaving only the cry piercing the abyss. The contrast is not that one is moral and the other not; it is that the psalm's confidence in vengeance-as-resolution and Bialik's despair at vengeance-as-impossibility are two Jewish responses to the same unbearable injustice, three millennia apart.
**Verse 12**
וְיֹאמַ֣ר אָ֭דָם אַךְ־פְּרִ֣י לַצַּדִּ֑יק; אַ֥ךְ יֵשׁ־אֱ֝לֹקִ֗ים שֹׁפְטִ֥ים בָּאָֽרֶץ׃
The verdict, spoken not by the psalmist but by אָדָם, "humanity" — generic, anonymous, everyone. The speaker who prosecuted the case cedes the closing line to the human race. And the speech-and-silence arc resolves: the corrupt speakers of verse 2 and the willfully deaf snake of verse 5 are silenced, and ordinary humanity is restored to honest confession. The same human sphere named in verse 2 — בְּנֵי אָדָם, "human beings" — is now the speaker of true judgment.
The double אַךְ, "surely... surely," delivers an emphatic cadence that answers the skeptical הַאֻמְנָם, "is it really?", of verse 2. The poem that opened with a doubt closes with a doubled certainty. אַךְ־פְּרִי לַצַּדִּיק, "surely there is fruit for the righteous" — agricultural recompense, righteousness bearing yield, answering the wicked who merely תִּפְעָלוּן, "produce," wrong (v. 3). As Jeremiah frames divine recompense, לָתֵת לְאִישׁ כִּדְרָכָיו כִּפְרִי מַעֲלָלָיו, "to give each according to his ways, according to the fruit of his deeds" (Jer 17:10).
The final clause is theologically loaded: אֱלֹקִים שֹׁפְטִים — a plural participle with אֱלֹקִים. Grammatically possible, though not the usual agreement when אֱלֹקִים refers to the God of Israel; and here the plural leaves room for several resonances. Standard monotheism hears, "God judges." A divine-council reading hears an echo of "gods who judge," answering Psalm 82. And the ironic reversal remains in any case: the corrupt שֹׁפְטִים, "judges," of verse 2 are supplanted by true שֹׁפְטִים in verse 12 — same participle, opposite referent. Ibn Ezra and Radak take the plural as angels, מְשַׁלְּחֵי הָאֵל, executing God's decrees on earth. The participle's ongoing aspect matters: not a single past verdict but continuous judging.
And the last word closes the frame: בָּאָרֶץ, "on the earth." The same ground that was the scene of violence in verse 3 is now the seat of justice. This is the answer to the Solomonic doubt the psalm opened with — הַאֻמְנָם יֵשֵׁב אֱלֹקִים עַל־הָאָרֶץ, "will God really dwell on earth?" (1 Kgs 8:27). The psalm spends ten verses making the doubt unbearable and plants its reply on the very spot where the crime occurred. Calvin built his reading on exactly this word, insisting against those who confine God's government to heaven that verse 12 vindicates active divine administration *on earth*.
Faiz Ahmed Faiz gave the same earthly insistence its modern revolutionary form in "Hum Dekhenge" (1979), written under Zia-ul-Haq's martial law:
> جب ظلم و ستم کے کوہِ گراں / روئی کی طرح اُڑ جائیں گے
> "When the heavy mountains of tyranny and oppression blow away like cotton..."
Faiz's prophetic rhythm envisions oppression's sudden physical collapse — mountains blown like cotton. Like Psalm 58, the poem moves toward a communal declaration that justice will manifest not in some deferred afterlife but visibly, physically, here. The psalm and the qawwali share the same stubborn conviction: that the answer to "is there justice on earth?" must finally be given *on earth*, and spoken — as Faiz's title insists, *we shall see it* — by the ordinary people who were its victims.---
