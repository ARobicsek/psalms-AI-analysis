# Commentary on Psalm 60
---
## Introduction
Read the superscription of Psalm 60 and then read its third verse, and you will feel the floor tilt. The heading records a triumph: David fighting Aram-Naharaim and Aram-Zobah, Joab returning to cut down twelve thousand Edomites in the Valley of Salt. Then the poem opens its mouth and says אֱלֹקִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ, "O God, You have rejected us." A victory is announced; a defeat is sung. The Septuagint sharpens the strangeness by translating the obscure title-word מִכְתָּם as στηλογραφία, "stele-inscription" — the engraved text of a public monument. Psalm 60 is built like a triumph-monument, and the text someone has carved on it is a record of the army falling apart. The heading also calls it לְלַמֵּד, "to teach" — the only psalm in the Psalter labeled as instruction. The question this essay pursues is what, exactly, a monument to defeat is trying to teach.
The architecture is clean. Verses 1–2 are the superscription, rubric plus historical anchor. Verses 3–5 are the complaint, an unbroken chain of accusations with God as the grammatical subject of every disaster. Verses 6–7 turn toward petition — a banner for the faithful, then a plea to be answered. Verses 8–10 are the hinge: a first-person divine oracle in which God claims the whole land and reduces the surrounding nations to household images of contempt. Verses 11–12 collapse back into lament — *who will bring me to Edom?* — and verses 13–14 close on confidence: human help is worthless; with God we will do valiantly. So the poem moves complaint → petition → oracle → relapse → confidence, and the oracle of total sovereignty sits exactly in the middle.
Here is the first thing the casual reader misses. That central oracle is sealed inside a bracket of rejection. The complaint opens at verse 3 with זְנַחְתָּנוּ, "You have rejected us," and the lament returns at verse 12 with the identical word, הֲלֹא־אַתָּה אֱלֹקִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ, "Have You not, O God, rejected us?" The divine land-grant is framed on both sides by the same complaint, and the complaint is not resolved by the promise it surrounds. One sign that this is deliberate is that the poet's own material was reused elsewhere without the bracket. Psalm 108 stitches together the second half of Psalm 57 and the second half of Psalm 60 (vv. 7–14), and in doing so it keeps only the *back* bracket — Ps 108:12 has one זְנַחְתָּנוּ, opening with praise rather than the verse-3 wound. The editor of Psalm 108 dissolved precisely the front bracket that makes Psalm 60 ache. The same oracle becomes triumphant there and anguished here, not by changing a word of the oracle but by keeping or breaking the seal of rejection in front of it. Psalm 60's refusal of cheap victory is a structural decision, and it is unique to Psalm 60.
The deeper engine, though, is the grammar — and once you hear it you cannot un-hear it. Sort the verbs by form. The acts of destruction that open the poem stand in the perfect, the conjugation of completed action: זְנַחְתָּנוּ, "You have rejected; פְרַצְתָּנוּ, You have breached; אָנַפְתָּ, You have been angry; הִרְעַשְׁתָּה, You have made quake; פְּצַמְתָּהּ, You have torn open; הִרְאִיתָ, You have made us see; הִשְׁקִיתָנוּ, You have made us drink." Seven finished blows. The pleas for rescue are imperatives — a demand, not a report: תְּשׁוֹבֵב, "restore; רְפָה, heal; הוֹשִׁיעָה, save; הָבָה, grant." And the psalm's statements of confidence — the oracle's exultant claims and the closing vow alike — are cohortative or imperfect, the mood of the not-yet: אֶעְלֹזָה, "let me exult; אֲחַלְּקָה, let me divide; אֲמַדֵּד, let me measure; נַעֲשֶׂה, we will do valiantly; יָבוּס, He will trample." Even the one positive perfect, נָתַתָּה לִּירֵאֶיךָ נֵּס, gives not completed deliverance but a rallying banner. What has happened to the people is the breaking. Deliverance is wished, demanded, foretold — but never recorded as done.
This is why the poem ends on יָבוּס, "He will trample" — future, not finished. A casual reader expects a lament to climax in a completed reversal; this one climaxes in a verb that points past its own last word. The confidence is real, but it is the confidence of a people still inside the straits, betting on a victory the grammar will not yet let them claim. The poet has encoded the unresolved tension not in mood alone but in the verb system itself.
And now the heading makes sense. A victory-stele normally carves the triumph in stone — past tense, accomplished, eternal. This stele carves the rejection in the perfect and leaves the rescue in the imperfect. That inversion is the לְלַמֵּד, the lesson: what is permanent and recorded is the experience of being breached; what remains open, demanded, and trusted is the deliverance. The monument teaches by refusing to lie about which one has happened yet.
The human situation here is exact and unglamorous. It is the condition of holding a promise you believe and a present that contradicts it — of having heard, in your bones, that the land is yours, while standing locked out of the one fortified city you cannot reach (v. 11, עִיר מָצוֹר, often identified with Edomite Sela/Petra). Nizar Qabbani caught the same collision in 1967, writing after the Naksa: إذا خسرنا الحربَ لا غرابهْ, "If we lost the war, it is no surprise" — because, he says, the nation went to battle armed with بالعنترياتِ التي ما قتلت ذبابهْ, "with hollow heroics that never killed a fly." Qabbani blames the gap between the banner and the wound on the banner's emptiness. Psalm 60 does something braver: it lets the boast of the superscription stand, and then sings the wound underneath it, and insists that both are true at once — the victory in the record, the breach in the body, the rescue still in the imperative mood, waiting to be answered.
That is the thing that makes this psalm impossible to read the same way again: it is a monument on which the only event carved in finished, irreversible stone is the disaster, while every word of salvation is left grammatically unfinished — a triumph-inscription whose triumph has not yet been allowed to happen.

#### Full psalm
Psalm 60 is not part of the fixed daily, Shabbat, or festival psalm cycle in any of the major rites — its accusatory opening (זְנַחְתָּנוּ, "You have rejected us") and its military particularity make it awkward as routine communal praise. The one recurring custom is its reported recitation on **Shushan Purim**, the fifteenth of Adar kept by walled cities, drawn from the title-word שׁוּשַׁן. This is overdetermined: שׁוּשַׁן is at once "lily" and the proper name Susa, the archetypal walled city of Esther, and the body of the psalm fixes its longing on an עִיר מָצוֹר, "a fortified city" (v. 11) — so both the heading and the poem point toward the festival of the walled town.

## Verse-by-Verse Commentary
**Verse 7** — לְמַעַן יֵחָלְצוּן יְדִידֶיךָ הוֹשִׁיעָה יְמִינְךָ וַעֲנֵנִי, "That those whom You love may be rescued, deliver with Your right hand and answer me." In **Nusach Sefard (the Hasidic rite)** and across **Edot HaMizrach**, this verse is appended to the close of the Amidah, after the expansion of *Elohai Netzor* and before יִהְיוּ לְרָצוֹן, following the sequence עֲשֵׂה לְמַעַן שְׁמֶךָ … עֲשֵׂה לְמַעַן קְדֻשָּׁתֶךָ. The national-military plea of David becomes a personal coda — one more biblically-rooted cry for "Your beloved" at the seam between the statutory blessings and the worshipper's withdrawal from direct address. Notably, the verse also serves as the model *name-verse* segulah for those whose name begins with ל and ends with י — a custom that silently depends on the Qere וַעֲנֵנִי (ending in yod), not the Ketiv וַעֲנֵנוּ (ending in vav). Lived prayer thus quietly votes for the singular, royal reading of the textual crux.
**Verse 13** — הָבָה־לָּנוּ עֶזְרָת מִצָּר, "Grant us aid against the foe." In the **Edot HaMizrach** Neilah liturgy for Yom Kippur, this line is woven into the closing selichot at the "shutting of the gates," set among יוֹשֵׁב הַכְּרוּבִים הוֹפִיעָה … הֲיַד ה׳ תִּקְצָר, "Enthroned on the cherubim, shine forth … Is the hand of the LORD too short?" The psalm's original "help against the *military* foe" is reread as rescue from sin and exile at the year's most charged petitionary moment.
**Verse 6** — נֵס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס, "a banner to be raised." This phrase is recast in the **Ashkenazi** Rosh Hashanah Musaf piyyutim as a cosmic standard gathering עֶלְיוֹנִים וְתַחְתּוֹנִים, "the upper and lower realms," at the opening of the heavenly books — the rallying-banner of a defeated army transposed into the ensign of universal judgment.

**Verse 1**
לַמְנַצֵּחַ עַל־שׁוּשַׁן עֵדוּת; מִכְתָּם לְדָוִד לְלַמֵּד׃
Three terms here were already opaque to antiquity. שׁוּשַׁן עֵדוּת, literally "lily of testimony," may name a melody; Ibn Ezra shrugs that it is simply עַל טַעַם פִּיּוּט תְּחִלָּתוֹ שׁוּשַׁן עֵדוּת, "the tune of a poem beginning *shushan eduth*," while noting others took שׁוּשַׁן as an instrument. The Septuagint, reading a שׁנה root, rendered it ἀλλοιόω, "alter, change" — a wholly different word, a sign that the phrase had gone dark before the translators reached it.
מִכְתָּם is the richer puzzle. It clusters only at Psalms 16 and 56–60, suggesting a small discrete collection. The roots compete: כֶּתֶם, "gold" (so Rashi: a דָּבָר חָמוּד, "a precious thing," and Metzudat David, "beloved to him like fine gold"), against כתב, "to write." The Septuagint's στηλογραφία, "stele-inscription," takes the second path and names a form — public inscription on a stele. That reading matters for the whole psalm: if Psalm 60 was conceived as a monumental royal text, then its lamenting content is engraved where one expects a boast. A related form sits over Hezekiah's poem of illness and recovery in Isaiah 38:9, מִכְתָּב לְחִזְקִיָּהוּ — a royal personal text wrung from crisis, exactly the register here.
לְלַמֵּד, "to teach," is the unique element — no other psalm-heading carries it. Its closest scriptural cousin is 2 Samuel 1:18, where David commands his lament over Saul וַיֹּאמֶר לְלַמֵּד בְּנֵי־יְהוּדָה קָשֶׁת, "to teach the sons of Judah the bow." That pairing is suggestive, because the very crux of verse 6 hangs on whether the word there is קֹשֶׁט, "truth," or קֶשֶׁת, "bow." A heading that already flags a Davidic "teach … the bow" frame may explain why a tradition would later hear "bow" in verse 6. Meiri reads לְלַמֵּד as לְעוֹרֵר הַלְּבָבוֹת, "to awaken hearts" to the truth that all human affairs are ordered by Providence — which is precisely the lesson a defeat-monument would carry. Metzudat David puts it flatly: עָשׂוּי לְלַמֵּד דַּעַת אֶת הָעָם אֵיךְ הַכֹּל בָּא בְּהַשְׁגָּחָה, "made to teach the people that everything comes by divine oversight."
**Verse 2**
בְּהַצּוֹתוֹ אֶת־אֲרַם נַהֲרַיִם וְאֶת־אֲרַם צוֹבָה; וַיָּשָׁב יוֹאָב וַיַּךְ אֶת־אֱדוֹם בְּגֵיא־מֶלַח שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר אָלֶף׃
The longest historical superscription in the Psalter immediately generates problems. The verb בְּהַצּוֹתוֹ Rashi derives from נצה, "to strive," comparing בְּהַצּוֹתָם עַל ה׳ (Num 26:9), "when they quarreled against the LORD" — hence "when he fought." The Septuagint instead read a fire-root, rendering ἐμπυρίζω, "to set ablaze," which would describe a separate incendiary campaign against Aram before Joab's Edom action. The two readings are not trivially close; they describe different operations.
Then the casualty figures and commanders refuse to agree. Here Joab kills 12,000; 2 Samuel 8:13 credits David with 18,000; 1 Chronicles 18:12 credits Abishai with 18,000. Rashi harmonizes by phasing the battle — אֲבִישַׁי … שֵׁשֶׁת אֲלָפִים, וְיוֹאָב שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר אֶלֶף, "Abishai six thousand the first time, Joab twelve thousand on his return" — but the very need to harmonize tells the truth: the psalm preserves one of three divergent memories of the war. The toponym carries its own undertow. גֵּיא־מֶלַח, the "Valley of Salt," ties this victory to a chronic wound — Amaziah fights the same Edomites in the same valley generations later (2 Kgs 14:7) — and מֶלַח, salt, belongs to the vocabulary of desolation and curse (sowing a razed city with salt, Judg 9:45). Even the named triumph hums with the word for ruin, quietly preparing the cursed land-imagery of verses 4–5.
**Verse 3**
אֱלֹקִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ פְרַצְתָּנוּ; אָנַפְתָּ תְּשׁוֹבֵב לָנוּ׃
Three accusations land like blows, and then one imperative changes the direction of the whole verse. זְנַחְתָּנוּ is the strong word for rejection — not absence but deliberate repudiation. The concordance shows it is the lament-word *par excellence*: לָמָה זְנַחְתָּנִי (Ps 43:2), "Why have You rejected me?"; לָמָה אֱלֹקִים זָנַחְתָּ לָנֶצַח (Ps 74:1), "Why, O God, have You rejected forever?" What is theologically startling, as Ibn Ezra notes, is the absence of any confessed sin — the verb simply makes God the subject and Israel the object, with no stated cause. (Rashi supplies one prophetically, reading David foreseeing Edom's future dominion over Israel.)
פְרַצְתָּנוּ, "You have breached us," inverts a famous victory. At Baal-Perazim God פָּרַץ ה׳ אֶת־אֹיְבַי לְפָנַי כְּפֶרֶץ מָיִם (2 Sam 5:20), "burst through my enemies like a bursting of water." The same verb now has God bursting through *His own people's* wall — the besieging force is divine. Metzudat David draws the picture: כְּמוֹ עִיר שֶׁנִּפְרַץ חוֹמָתָהּ, "like a city whose wall is breached, so that attackers pour in from every side."
Then תְּשׁוֹבֵב, "restore us!" — and it is the rare Polel of שוב rather than the expected Hiphil הָשֵׁב. The intensive stem asks not for a simple return but for thorough reconstitution: the same hand that broke must comprehensively rebuild. The Septuagint, oddly, softened it to οἰκτείρω, "have pity," shifting from structural restoration toward emotional compassion. Malbim hears in the whole sequence the logic of Isaiah's אוֹדְךָ ה׳ כִּי אָנַפְתָּ בִּי, "I thank You, LORD, for You were angry with me" — the anger spent so that restoration may follow.
**Verse 4**
הִרְעַשְׁתָּה אֶרֶץ פְּצַמְתָּהּ; רְפָה שְׁבָרֶיהָ כִי־מָטָה׃
The metaphor lurches mid-verse from earthquake to operating table. First the land quakes and is torn; then comes רְפָה שְׁבָרֶיהָ, "heal its fractures" — a *medical* verb (רפא) applied to geology, where one expects חַזֵּק, "strengthen," or בָּנָה, "rebuild." The land has become a wounded body. This fuses two registers that Scripture usually keeps apart: national-political collapse and bodily trauma. Jeremiah does the same with אוֹי לִי עַל־שִׁבְרִי נַחְלָה מַכָּתִי (Jer 10:19), "Woe is me for my fracture; my wound is grievous," and the comfort comes in the same vocabulary: כִּי אַעֲלֶה אֲרֻכָה לָךְ וּמִמַּכּוֹתַיִךְ אֶרְפָּאֵךְ (Jer 30:17), "I will bring you healing and heal you of your wounds." The rhetorical logic is tight: You broke it; therefore You must heal it.
פְּצַמְתָּהּ is a genuine hapax — the verb פצם, "split open," occurs nowhere else in the Bible. Rashi confesses the difficulty, citing Dunash that it is Arabic without translating it, then settling on "tear," comparing the Targum of Jeremiah 22:14. The poet had common synonyms available — בָּקַע, פָּקַע, פָּצַח — and chose the strange one. The choice may carry a dark sound-irony: פָּצַם sits one consonant from פָּצַח, which in the prophets is almost a technical verb for *bursting into joyful song* — פִּצְחוּ הָרִים רִנָּה (Isa 44:23), "break forth, mountains, into song." The cry of fracture shadows the cry of jubilation.
The closing כִי־מָטָה, "for it is collapsing," uses מוט for instability rather than completed ruin. The land is not pictured as already fallen but as falling: this is a motivation-clause, the urgency that justifies the imperative. Du Fu, surveying the ruins of Chang'an after the An Lushan rebellion in 757, wrote 國破山河在, "The nation is shattered, but mountains and rivers remain" — and the contrast with our verse is precise. Du Fu finds bleak comfort in a landscape that outlasts the state; the psalmist is denied even that, for in his vision the very ground is splitting beneath the political collapse. Where the Chinese poet separates the body politic from the indifferent earth, the Hebrew poet fuses them — the state's fall *is* an earthquake, an ontological crisis reaching down to the dirt.
**Verse 5**
הִרְאִיתָ עַמְּךָ קָשָׁה; הִשְׁקִיתָנוּ יַיִן תַּרְעֵלָה׃
הִרְאִיתָ, "You have made [Your people] *see*," adds a cruel psychological turn: the suffering is not merely endured but witnessed, watched. And קָשָׁה, "hard things," carries an Egyptian undertone, the adjective of bondage in עֲבֹדָה קָשָׁה (Exod 1:14) — defeat as a renewed slavery.
The second colon delivers one of Scripture's signature images: יַיִן תַּרְעֵלָה, "wine of reeling." The exact phrase appears nowhere else, but תַּרְעֵלָה anchors the cup-of-wrath tradition: כּוֹס הַתַּרְעֵלָה (Isa 51:17), "the cup of reeling," and the bowl of staggering set before the nations in Zechariah 12:2. The motif's full machinery runs through Jeremiah 25:15, קַח אֶת־כּוֹס הַיַּיִן הַחֵמָה הַזֹּאת מִיָּדִי וְהִשְׁקִיתָה אֹתוֹ אֶת־כָּל־הַגּוֹיִם, "Take this cup of the wine of wrath from My hand and make all the nations drink." The sharp twist in Psalm 60 is that Israel drinks *first* — the cup ordinarily reserved for Babylon and Edom is pressed on God's own people. Rashi reads רַעַל as numbing envelopment, the wine הָאוֹטֵם אֶת הַלֵּב, "that stops up the heart and wraps it," comparing the "shawled" women of the Mishnah. The Septuagint's κατάνυξις, "stupor," intensifies further: not a tipsy sway but total anesthesia.
Wilfred Owen secularized exactly this image in *Dulce et Decorum Est*, drafted in the trenches in 1917: soldiers "Drunk with fatigue; deaf even to the hoots / Of gas-shells dropping softly behind." Owen's intoxication arises from sheer human abandonment, the staggering of men marched past endurance; the psalmist's is poured deliberately from God's hand. Both reach for drunkenness to name the same thing — the loss of bodily command, the inability to stand straight under overwhelming force — but the psalm keeps an agent behind the cup, which is what makes its terror theological rather than merely physical.
**Verse 6**
נָתַתָּה לִּירֵאֶיךָ נֵּס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס; מִפְּנֵי קֹשֶׁט סֶלָה׃
The verse pairs a noun with its own cognate verb — נֵס, "banner," and לְהִתְנוֹסֵס, "to be raised as a banner" — a figure that gathers force from repetition: a banner *bannering itself*. The banner is the right image at this turn precisely because it does not defend; it gathers. Unlike a shield or fortress, a נֵס identifies and rallies scattered forces — which is what a breached, reeling army (vv. 3–5) needs first. The motif runs from ה׳ נִסִּי (Exod 17:15), "the LORD is my banner," to the root of Jesse who עֹמֵד לְנֵס עַמִּים (Isa 11:10), "stands as a banner for peoples."
But נֵס לְהִתְנוֹסֵס can also be heard against the sound of נוס, "to flee." For an army just described as breached, a rally-standard that can be heard as flight is grimly apt. This is not only a modern ear: the Septuagint read the line from that flight-sense, rendering it τοῦ φυγεῖν ἀπὸ προσώπου τόξου, "to flee from before the bow."
That "bow" is the famous crux. The Masoretic מִפְּנֵי קֹשֶׁט, "because of truth," rests on a rare word (קֹשֶׁט, secured only by Prov 22:21 and the Aramaic of Daniel); the Septuagint read קֶשֶׁת, "bow," a single-letter difference (ט for ת). So the line is either theological — a banner raised *for the sake of truth* — or tactical — a banner *to flee the archers*. Syriac and Symmachus back the Hebrew; the heading's own לְלַמֵּד … קֶשֶׁת echo of 2 Samuel 1:18 may explain why the Greek tradition heard "bow." Rashi, reading קֹשֶׁט as "truth," gives it a moral twist: when God prospers the faithful, the nations will not impugn His justice but יְקַשְּׁטוּ דִּינֶיךָ, "will adorn Your judgments" as just. The Selah, marking a liturgical pause, falls exactly on this interpretive fork.
**Verse 7**
לְמַעַן יֵחָלְצוּן יְדִידֶיךָ; הוֹשִׁיעָה יְמִינְךָ וַעֲנֵנִי׃
The relational vocabulary climbs across three verses: עַמְּךָ, "Your people" (v. 5), then יְרֵאֶיךָ, "those who fear You" (v. 6), now יְדִידֶיךָ, "Your beloved." The last is the most intimate register, the language of love-poetry — the word behind Solomon's name יְדִידְיָה (2 Sam 12:25) and Benjamin's blessing יְדִיד ה׳ (Deut 33:12). As Derek Kidner observed, this is the vocabulary of ardor: rescue is asked not on grounds of duty but of love.
The verse's grammatical seam is the Ketiv/Qere. The written text is וַעֲנֵנוּ, "answer *us*" (plural, communal); the read tradition is וַעֲנֵנִי, "answer *me*" (singular, royal). A single "I" punctures the corporate "we," and the surrounding plurals (יֵחָלְצוּן, יְדִידֶיךָ) make the singular jar. The same variant survives in the parallel at Psalm 108:7, which means the uncertainty is old, not a copyist's slip — a seam where an original royal petition shows through its communal adaptation, or vice versa. The Septuagint's singular εἰσάκουσόν μου supports the Qere. And as noted in the liturgical section, the later segulah custom — fixing this verse for worshippers whose name runs ל…י — can only work with the yod-final Qere, so lived prayer quietly settled the question.
**Verse 8**
אֱלֹקִים דִּבֶּר בְּקׇדְשׁוֹ אֶעְלֹזָה; אֲחַלְּקָה שְׁכֶם וְעֵמֶק סֻכּוֹת אֲמַדֵּד׃
Here the poem pivots from petition to oracle, and the pivot-phrase דִּבֶּר בְּקׇדְשׁוֹ is deliberately ambiguous: "in His holiness" (an oath sworn on the divine nature, as in נִשְׁבַּע בְּקׇדְשׁוֹ, Ps 89:36) or "in His sanctuary" (an oracle delivered in the Temple). The first grounds the promise in God's very being and so makes it irrevocable; the medievals lean toward the prophetic, Radak glossing בְּרוּחַ קׇדְשׁוֹ, "by His holy spirit … that the kingdom of Israel should be mine."
Then come three first-person verbs — אֶעְלֹזָה, "let me exult; אֲחַלְּקָה, let me apportion; אֲמַדֵּד, let me measure out" — and the speaker is genuinely ambiguous. Is God still speaking, exulting over His own land-grant? Or has the king picked up the divine word and made it his own voice? The fusion may be the point: the royal and divine voices merge in the act of claiming the land. The verbs are a surveyor's: חלק and מדד are the vocabulary of Joshua's allotment (Josh 18–19). After the land *quaked and split* in verse 4, God now *measures and divides* it — the same earth, first convulsed, now precisely parceled. Isaiah makes measurement the very emblem of divine mastery, מִי־מָדַד בְּשָׁעֳלוֹ מַיִם (Isa 40:12), "Who has measured the waters in the hollow of his hand?" Shechem (west of the Jordan) and the Valley of Sukkoth (east) together mark territory on both sides of the river — the patriarchal heartland reclaimed by cartography.
**Verse 9**
לִי גִלְעָד וְלִי מְנַשֶּׁה; וְאֶפְרַיִם מָעוֹז רֹאשִׁי יְהוּדָה מְחֹקְקִי׃
The repeated possessive לִי … וְלִי, "mine … and mine," is the grammar of ownership, hammered. Then the tribes become parts of a warrior's body. אֶפְרַיִם מָעוֹז רֹאשִׁי, "Ephraim the stronghold of my head," is a helmet-metaphor — the dominant northern tribe as the war-god's headgear; the Septuagint's κραταίωσις τῆς κεφαλῆς, "fortification of the head," confirms the protective sense. The image belongs to the armory of holy war, where God Himself wears כּוֹבַע יְשׁוּעָה בְּרֹאשׁוֹ (Isa 59:17), "a helmet of salvation on His head."
יְהוּדָה מְחֹקְקִי, "Judah my scepter," reaches straight back to Jacob's blessing: לֹא־יָסוּר שֵׁבֶט מִיהוּדָה וּמְחֹקֵק מִבֵּין רַגְלָיו (Gen 49:10), "the scepter shall not depart from Judah, nor the ruler's-staff from between his feet." The root חקק means "to engrave, decree," so מְחֹקְקִי is the law-giving authority; the Septuagint amplified to βασιλεύς μου, "my king." The pairing maps a whole theology of the united monarchy onto two body-parts: Ephraim the fighting strength (helmet), Judah the dynastic authority (scepter) — north supplies the army, south the throne.
**Verse 10**
מוֹאָב סִיר רַחְצִי עַל־אֱדוֹם אַשְׁלִיךְ נַעֲלִי; עָלַי פְּלֶשֶׁת הִתְרוֹעָעִי׃
Three enemy nations, three postures of contempt, each more pointed. Moab is סִיר רַחְצִי, "my washbasin" — and the degradation is precise. Radak specifies that it is not a cooking-pot but a washbasin — the basin for filthy foot-rinse, the most menial object in the house. A proud nation (Jeremiah's haughty Moab) reduced to a servant's slop-bucket. The Septuagint here diverges wildly, reading λέβης τῆς ἐλπίδος μου, "cauldron of my hope," for which there is no good explanation from the Hebrew — a corrupted Vorlage, most likely; the humiliation-reading is firmly supported by context and by the parallel in Psalm 108.
On Edom, אַשְׁלִיךְ נַעֲלִי, "I cast my shoe" — a legal property-gesture. In Ruth 4:7 the transfer of a sandal seals a conveyance, שָׁלַף אִישׁ נַעֲלוֹ וְנָתַן לְרֵעֵהוּ, "a man drew off his sandal and gave it to his fellow"; in Deuteronomy 25:9 the removed sandal releases a claim. To throw one's shoe *onto* Edom can suggest planting a deed of ownership. Malbim reads it as the mark of servitude itself: חֲלִיצַת נַעַל רַבּוֹ, "removing the master's sandal" is the slave's task.
The verb to Philistia, הִתְרוֹעָעִי, is genuinely two-faced: "raise a shout *for* me" (vassal acclamation) or "shout *against* me" (enemy war-cry, taken with contempt — *go ahead, try it*). Metzudat David takes the first, בַּעֲבוּרִי יָרִיעוּ פְלִשְׁתִּים תְּרוּעַת מֶלֶךְ, "for my sake the Philistines shall sound the king's acclamation." The Septuagint forecloses the ambiguity toward submission, ἐμοὶ ἀλλόφυλοι ὑπετάγησαν, "the foreigners are made subject to me." Either way Philistia is demoted — whether obedient vassal or impotent taunter, the outcome is the same.
**Verse 11**
מִי יֹבִלֵנִי עִיר מָצוֹר; מִי נָחַנִי עַד־אֱדוֹם׃
The irony could not be sharper. One verse ago God cast His sandal *onto* Edom as His own property; now the psalmist cannot even get *to* Edom. עִיר מָצוֹר, "the fortified city" — which Radak and many moderns identify with Edom's rock-capital Sela/Petra, naturally impregnable — is the very territory just claimed by oracle, and it is humanly unreachable. The divine promise and lived experience stand back to back in raw tension. This is the psalm's tension in miniature: the deed is signed; the door is barred.
The double מִי, "who?", is the rhetoric of dependence — the implied answer being "God alone." But the two verbs sit in different tenses, and the dissonance is meaningful. יֹבִלֵנִי, "who will bring me," is imperfect (future desire); נָחַנִי, "who has led me," is perfect (past act). Ibn Ezra resolves it as memory turning to hope: הָיִיתִי מְחַשֵּׁב מִי יוֹבִילֵנִי … יַעֲשֶׂה זֶה הַשֵּׁם שֶׁנָּחַנִי עַד אֱדוֹם, "I would think: who will bring me? — and answer myself: the God who [once] led me as far as Edom." The God who *was* the guide is the only one who can be the guide again — which is exactly why His withdrawal in the next verse is unbearable.
**Verses 12–13**
הֲלֹא־אַתָּה אֱלֹקִים זְנַחְתָּנוּ; וְלֹא־תֵצֵא אֱלֹקִים בְּצִבְקוֹתֵינוּ׃
הָבָה־לָּנוּ עֶזְרָת מִצָּר; וְשָׁוְא תְּשׁוּעַת אָדָם׃
The verbatim return of זְנַחְתָּנוּ from verse 3 snaps the bracket shut around the oracle. The interrogative הֲלֹא expects a yes — it is rhetorical affirmation, not real question: "Indeed You have rejected us." The promise of verses 8–10 has not erased the rejection that frames it.
The second colon of verse 12 contains a buried irony of the divine name. The complaint is that God will not march בְּצִבְקוֹתֵינוּ, "with our armies" — and צְבָקוֹת, "hosts," is the very word in the war-title ה׳ צְבָקוֹת, "LORD of Hosts," the God who by definition goes out with armies. That answering title is structurally barred from this psalm: Psalm 60 sits in the Elohistic Psalter, where the Tetragrammaton is characteristically replaced with אֱלֹקִים. So the people voice their abandonment using the very noun that forms the war-title the psalm does not use — the absent war-God and the absent title mirroring each other.
Verse 13 opens with the archaic imperative הָבָה, "grant!" — an old form (Gen 11:3, 7) more urgent than the standard תֵּן, signaling a primal, foundational appeal. Then the antithesis lands with inverted word order, throwing the weight onto its first word: וְשָׁוְא תְּשׁוּעַת אָדָם, "and *worthless* — is human deliverance." שָׁוְא is the word of the Third Commandment, לֹא תִשָּׂא … לַשָּׁוְא, "you shall not take [the name] in vain." Human salvation is filed in the same drawer as empty, false things — the strongest possible devaluation. Malbim draws the whole theodicy together: the early rejection was itself הַהֲכָנָה אֶל הָעֵזֶר, "the preparation for the help," staged precisely so Israel would learn שָׁוְא תְּשׁוּעַת אָדָם — that without divine aid there is no rescue at all.
**Verse 14**
בֵּאלֹקִים נַעֲשֶׂה־חָיִל; וְהוּא יָבוּס צָרֵינוּ׃
The close is confident — and unfinished. נַעֲשֶׂה־חָיִל, "we will do valiantly," uses an idiom of triumph that the concordance scatters across blessing-contexts: וַעֲשֵׂה־חַיִל בְּאֶפְרָתָה (Ruth 4:11), "do valiantly in Ephrathah," and the temple-cry יְמִין ה׳ עֹשָׂה חָיִל (Ps 118:15), "the right hand of the LORD does valiantly." The preposition בֵּאלֹקִים, "*with*/by God," distributes the agency exactly: humans act, but only in God's company. Spurgeon caught the balance — divine working "is not an argument for human inaction, but… the best excitement for courageous effort." The first-person plural נַעֲשֶׂה also reasserts the communal "we" against the isolated "me" of verse 11.
And the final verb is יָבוּס, "He will trample" — visceral, not abstract. Rashi glosses it plainly, יִרְמוֹס, "he will tread down"; Radak ties it to בּוּסוּ בַּטִּיט חוּצוֹת, "trample in the mud of the streets." The image answers the whole psalm: a people that began *breached and shaking* ends with its enemies *underfoot*. But the verb is imperfect — future, not accomplished. The Septuagint blunted this to ἐξουδενώσει, "he will bring to nothing," losing the kinetic trampling while leaving the tense's open edge intact. Ibn Ezra names the mood exactly: מִתְנַבֵּא בְּרוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ שֶׁהוּא יְנַצֵּחַ בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה, "he prophesies by the holy spirit that [God] will be victorious *in the end*." The psalm does not close on a victory won. It closes on a victory promised, in the grammar of the not-yet — the only honest ending for a monument whose single accomplished event was the breaking, and whose every word of rescue still waits, in the imperative and the imperfect, to be answered.---
