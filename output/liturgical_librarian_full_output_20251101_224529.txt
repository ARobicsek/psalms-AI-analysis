# Scholarly Commentary: וְדֶרֶךְ רְשָׁעִים in Jewish Liturgy

The phrase וְדֶרֶךְ רְשָׁעִים (the way of the wicked) from Psalm 1:6 appears consistently throughout the morning liturgy across all Jewish traditions as part of the recitation of Psalm 146, which comprises the concluding section of Pesukei Dezimra (Verses of Praise). This phrase is integrated into the liturgical context immediately following Ashrei (Psalm 145) and preceding the final Halleluyah psalms, appearing within a broader passage that emphasizes God's protective care for the righteous and vulnerable: יְהֹוָה אֹהֵב צַדִּיקִים יְהֹוָה שֹׁמֵר אֶת־גֵּרִים יָתוֹם וְאַלְמָנָה יְעוֹדֵד וְדֶרֶךְ רְשָׁעִים יְעַוֵּת (The Lord loves the righteous; the Lord guards the strangers, sustains the orphan and widow, and makes crooked the way of the wicked). The phrase recurs identically across multiple liturgical traditions—Ashkenaz, Sefard, and Edot HaMizrach—appearing in both weekday and Shabbat services, as well as in the High Holiday Machzor for Yom Kippur, demonstrating its universal significance in Jewish prayer. The consistent placement of this phrase within Pesukei Dezimra, the preparatory section of morning prayer designed to attune the worshiper's heart before the formal Amidah, suggests its function as a theological affirmation that precedes the central petitions of the service. The phrase's appearance across all occasions—weekday, Shabbat, and festival—underscores the fundamental Jewish conviction that divine justice operates continuously, rendering the path of wickedness ultimately futile while protecting those who pursue righteousness.

In the Mussaf Amidah for the Three Pilgrimage Festivals, the verse is woven into the festival petitions as part of a broader liturgical meditation on divine blessing and sustenance. More prominently, the complete verse appears in the Rosh Hashanah night Kiddush ceremony, where it is recited within an extended acrostic poem following the preliminary kabbalistic supplications. In this domestic ritual context, the liturgy states: "אָנָּא יְהֹוָה הוֹשִׁיעָה נָּא, אָנָּא יְהֹוָה הַצְלִיחָה נָא: כַּכָּתוּב: יִשָּׂא בְרָכָה מֵאֵת יְהֹוָה יאהדונהי, וּצְדָקָה מֵאֱלֹהֵי יִשְׁעוֹ: וְהָיָה כְּעֵץ שָׁתוּל עַל־פַּלְגֵי מָיִם, אֲשֶׁר פִּרְיוֹ יִתֵּן בְּעִתּוֹ וְעָלֵהוּ לֹא־יִבּוֹל, וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר־יַעֲשֶׂה יַצְלִיחַ" ("Please, O Lord, save us; please, O Lord, grant us success: as it is written, may one receive blessing from the Lord and righteousness from the God of salvation. The placement of this Davidic image within the Rosh Hashanah Kiddush—a ceremony that inaugurates the new year and petitions for divine favor—transforms the botanical metaphor into a prayer for the community's flourishing and success throughout the coming year. The verse's appearance in both festival and High Holiday contexts reflects its theological significance as a symbol of sustained blessing and righteous prosperity in Jewish liturgical imagination.

# Scholarly Commentary on Psalm 1:3 in Jewish Liturgy

The phrase כְּעֵץ שָׁתוּל עַל־פַּלְגֵי־מָיִם (like a tree planted by streams of water) from Psalm 1:3 appears in Jewish liturgy exclusively within the Tefillat Geshem (Prayer for Rain), the special piyyutim recited during the Chazzan's repetition of the Mussaf Amidah on Shemini Atzeret across both Ashkenazi and Sefardi traditions. This acrostic poem, which begins with the invocation "אֱלהֵינוּ וֵאלהֵי אֲבותֵינוּ" (Our God and God of our fathers), integrates the psalmist's botanical imagery into a petition for divine blessing, as evidenced in the liturgical context: "בֵּרַכְתּו כְּעֵץ שָׁתוּל עַל פַּלְגֵי מַיִם. גְּנַנְתּו הִצַּלְתּוֹ מֵאֵשׁ וּמִמַּיִם" (You blessed him like a tree planted by streams of water; You sheltered him, saved him from fire and from water). The citation of Psalm 1:3 is strategically positioned within the Gevurot blessing, immediately preceding the seasonal insertion of "Mashiv HaRuach U'Morid HaGeshem" (He causes the wind to blow and the rain to fall), thereby establishing a theological connection between the righteous individual's flourishing through water and the community's need for rain during the winter season. The phrase's appearance in this singular liturgical context—the most solemn petition for rain in the Jewish calendar—underscores how biblical imagery of botanical prosperity becomes a metaphor for collective sustenance and divine providence. This usage demonstrates the rabbinic hermeneutical principle of applying individual psalmist wisdom to communal supplication, transforming personal piety into national petition.

# Scholarly Commentary: לֹא עָמַד in Jewish Liturgy

The phrase לֹא עָמַד (lit. "he/none did not stand") appears in Jewish liturgy primarily within two distinct commemorative and penitential contexts that span the liturgical calendar. The phrase first occurs in the Sefard tradition's Kriat HaMegillah (Reading of the Megillah) on Purim, where it appears as part of the narrative account from the Book of Esther describing the miraculous deliverance of the Jewish people. In this context, the liturgy recites: "לִשְׁלֹחַ יָד בִּמְבַקְשֵׁי רָעָתָם וְאִישׁ לֹא עָמַד לִפְנֵיהֶם כִּי נָפַל פַּחְדָּם עַל כָּל הָעַמִּים" ("to lay hands upon those who sought their harm, and no one stood before them, for fear of them fell upon all the peoples"), emphasizing the reversal of Jewish vulnerability into triumph through divine providence. The second occurrence appears in the Ashkenazi Machzor for Yom Kippur, specifically within the Seder HaAvodah—the elaborate liturgical poem recited during the Mussaf service that reconstructs the High Priest's Temple service. Here, the phrase functions within a broader lamentation about the present state of exile and spiritual diminishment, as the liturgy mourns: "לֹא נָבִיא וְלֹא חָזוֹן בָּֽנוּ. כְּעִוְרִים נְגַשֵּׁשׁ וְנֵלֵךְ" ("we have neither prophet nor seer among us; like the blind we grope and walk"), expressing the absence and loss characteristic of the post-Temple era. Thus, while both liturgical instances employ the phrase לֹא עָמַד, they operate in fundamentally different registers: one celebrating historical deliverance during a joyous festival, the other lamenting spiritual absence during the most solemn day of the Jewish year.
