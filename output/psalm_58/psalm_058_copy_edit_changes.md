## Changes
*For exact before/after text, see [psalm_058_copy_edit_diff.md](psalm_058_copy_edit_diff.md).*

1. [7, 9] **Introduction**: Added “cut them down with arrows” to the opening list of destructions. The original sentence claimed seven modes of destruction but listed only six; the psalm’s sequence includes the arrow image in (v. 8), so the count needed to match the evidence.

2. [2, 9] **Introduction**: Changed “The only ‘gods’ in sight are the corrupt אֵלֶם” to “The only possible ‘gods’ in sight are the suspect אֵלֶם.” The commentary later prefers the “mute ones” reading of אֵלֶם, so the earlier wording overstated one disputed reading and created internal tension.

3. [7] **Introduction**: Changed “He draws His arrows” to “He aims His arrows.” The Hebrew יִדְרֹךְ חִצָּו refers to aiming or bending the bow with arrows, not drawing arrows from a quiver.

4. [9] **Introduction**: Replaced “reassembled almost entirely … with every word flipped” with “reuses key words.” The original claim overstated the lexical overlap between the opening and closing verses; the correspondence is real but more limited.

5. [2, 9] **Introduction**: Changed “The corrupt plural ‘gods’ of the opening are replaced…” to “The suspect opening אֵלֶם are answered…” The original phrasing conflicted with the later preference for reading אֵלֶם as “mute ones,” not settled “gods.”

6. [6, 9] **Introduction**: Removed the Tom Waits comparison and recast the paragraph without it. The parallel depended mainly on a surface theme of divine absence and did not illuminate Psalm 58 as specifically as the surrounding analysis required.

7. [7] **Verse 1**: Changed “David’s name binds them to the persecution narratives” to “David’s name binds them together in the superscriptions.” Psalm 16 is one of the six מִכְתָּם psalms but is not tied to a persecution narrative in its heading, so the original claim was too broad.

8. [7] **Verse 2**: Removed the Balak reference from the discussion of הַאֻמְנָם. The citation was not securely supported by the biblical text as stated, so the sentence was tightened to a verifiable example.

9. [9] **Verse 4**: Replaced “where the sheep wander by accident” with “but here that wandering is traced back to birth.” Isaiah 53:6 does not present wandering merely as accidental; the original contrast weakened the argument by oversimplifying the parallel text.

10. [7] **Verse 4**: Changed “Xunzi’s verse” to “Xunzi’s claim.” The quoted material comes from a philosophical argument, not a verse, so the original label was factually inexact.

11. [7, 9] **Verse 6**: Changed “The repetition of sound performs what it describes” to “The repetition of sound reinforces what it describes.” The original wording slightly overstated the form-meaning claim; the effect comes from audible repetition supporting the image, not literally enacting it.

12. [7] **Verse 7**: Changed “the identical rare word מַלְתְּעוֹת” to “the related rare word מְתַלְּעוֹת.” Job 29:17 uses a cognate form, not the identical form cited in Psalm 58.

13. [9] **Verse 8**: Rewrote the discussion of יִמָּאֲסוּ to present it as a difficult form often read as יִמַּסּוּ. The original argument that the poet deliberately conflated מאס (“reject”) and מסס (“melt”) went beyond the evidence provided.

14. [7] **Verse 8**: Changed “He draws His arrows” to “He aims His arrows.” As in the introduction, the Hebrew points to aiming/bending the bow rather than drawing arrows out.

15. [7] **Verse 9**: Removed the sentence claiming that the Targum reads אֵשֶׁת as an “abortive mole.” That description was not textually secure as stated and risked misleading readers.

16. [6] **Verse 9**: Replaced the Donne and Gwendolyn Brooks comparisons with a brief note on *Perek Shirah*. Those modern parallels shared only a broad theme of unmaking or unborn life and required too much qualification to illuminate the psalm clearly.

17. [7] **Verse 10**: Generalized the claim about the Esarhaddon treaty to “Ancient Near Eastern curse texts.” The original specific citation was too precise for the evidence presented; the broader formulation preserves the substantive point without overclaiming.

18. [6] **Verse 10**: Removed the Zora Neale Hurston comparison and retained the interpretive point about the whirlwind. The literary parallel added little beyond a shared storm image and obscured the psalm’s own logic.

19. [6, 9] **Verse 11**: Changed “The psalm borrows the image” from Anat to “The psalm uses an image known elsewhere in the ancient Near East.” The original wording implied a specific borrowing relationship that the shared motif alone does not prove.

20. [9] **Verse 11**: Removed “fulfilling Rebekah’s old fear” from the Torah Temimah sentence. That conclusion depended on background the paragraph did not explain, making the logic opaque for first-time readers.

21. [9, 2] **Verse 12**: Replaced “assembled out of the indictment’s own vocabulary, every word flipped” with “reuses key words.” The original claim overstated the degree of lexical repetition and echoed the same overclaim made earlier in the introduction.

22. [2, 9] **Verse 12**: Changed “the corrupt ‘gods’ have been replaced” to “the corrupt judges have been replaced.” This avoids contradicting the commentary’s earlier preference for the “mute ones/judges” reading of verse 2.

23. [2, 9] **Verse 12**: Made the plural-answer argument conditional: “if verse 2 is heard as אֵלִים, ‘mighty ones,’ the plural here may deliberately answer that opening plural.” The original stated a reading-dependent conclusion too absolutely, despite the commentary’s earlier preference for a different reading of אֵלֶם.
