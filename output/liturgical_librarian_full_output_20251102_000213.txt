# Psalm 1 in Jewish Liturgy

Psalm 1 appears in Jewish liturgy as an introductory supplement within two significant prayer contexts, both recited in their entirety across Ashkenazic and Edot HaMizrach traditions. On Yom Kippur during Maariv (evening service), Psalm 1 is recited as a prelude to Aleinu, the concluding prayer of the service, establishing a thematic transition from the day's confessional prayers to the affirmation of God's sovereignty. Additionally, Psalm 1 is incorporated as an opening element before Shir HaYichud and Anim Zemirot, liturgical poems celebrating divine unity and mystical communion that appear in the High Holiday liturgy. The psalm's opening declaration—"Blessed is the one who does not walk in the counsel of the wicked"—serves as a fitting introduction to these devotional contexts, particularly on the Day of Atonement when the community seeks to align itself with righteous conduct and divine will. The consistent placement of the complete psalm across both traditions and occasions suggests its function as a liturgical gateway, preparing worshippers for the more elaborate prayers and confessions that follow.

# Scholarly Commentary

The phrase וְדֶרֶךְ רְשָׁעִים (the way of the wicked) from Psalm 1:6 appears consistently throughout Jewish liturgy as part of the concluding verses of Psalm 145 (Ashrei) within Pesukei Dezimra—the section of praise and thanksgiving that opens the Shacharit service across all major liturgical traditions. This phrase is recited daily on weekdays and Shabbat, as well as on the High Holidays, appearing in the Ashkenazi, Sefard, and Edot HaMizrach rites. The liturgical context preserves the full verse in its traditional form, as exemplified in the Siddur Sefard: "יְהֹוָה אֹהֵב צַדִּיקִים יְהֹוָה שֹׁמֵר אֶת־גֵּרִים יָתוֹם וְאַלְמָנָה יְעוֹדֵד וְדֶרֶךְ רְשָׁעִים יְעַוֵּת" (The Lord loves the righteous; the Lord guards the strangers, sustains the orphan and widow, and perverts the way of the wicked). The phrase functions theologically within Pesukei Dezimra as part of the morning's affirmation of divine justice and providence, appearing immediately before the transition to the Halleluyah psalms (Psalms 146-150) and the concluding Yishtabach blessing. Its consistent inclusion across all traditions and occasions underscores the liturgical importance of Psalm 145's declaration that God sustains the righteous while ensuring the ultimate failure of wickedness—a theme that resonates throughout the daily invocation of praise that precedes the Amidah.

# Scholarly Commentary

The phrase from Psalm 1:3 describing the righteous person as "a tree planted by streams of water, that yields its fruit in its season, and its leaf does not wither, and all that he does prospers" (וְֽהָיָ֗ה כְּעֵץ֮ שָׁת֢וּל עַֽל־פַּלְגֵ֫י־מָ֥יִם אֲשֶׁ֤ר פִּרְי֨וֹ ׀ יִתֵּ֬ן בְּעִתּ֗וֹ וְעָלֵ֥הוּ לֹֽא־יִבּ֑וֹל וְכֹ֖ל אֲשֶׁר־יַעֲשֶׂ֣ה יַצְלִֽיחַ) appears within the Edot HaMizrach liturgical tradition in two significant contexts that frame the High Holiday season. In the Rosh Hashanah night Kiddush ceremony, the verse is recited within a lengthy acrostic poem ("Shanah Zo...") that comprises the introductory kabbalistic supplications preceding the core blessings; the liturgy incorporates it as part of a petitionary sequence: "אָנָּא יְהֹוָה הוֹשִׁיעָה נָּא, אָנָּא יְהֹוָה הַצְלִיחָה נָא... וְהָיָה כְּעֵץ שָׁתוּל עַל־פַּלְגֵי מָיִם, אֲשֶׁר פִּרְיוֹ יִתֵּן בְּעִתּוֹ וְעָלֵהוּ לֹא־יִבּוֹל, וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר־יַעֲשֶׂה יַצְלִיחַ" (Please, O Lord, save us; please, O Lord, make us prosper... and he shall be like a tree planted by streams of water...). The phrase also appears within the Mussaf Amidah for the Three Pilgrimage Festivals in the Edot HaMizrach rite, where it functions as scriptural support within a broader liturgical invocation of divine blessing and prosperity. This dual usage reflects the theological function of Psalm 1 in Jewish liturgy as a paradigm for blessing and flourishing, particularly invoked during moments of communal petition for divine favor on festive occasions.

# Scholarly Narrative

The phrase כְּעֵץ שָׁתוּל עַל־פַּלְגֵי־מָיִם (like a tree planted by streams of water) from Psalm 1:3 appears in Jewish liturgy exclusively within the Tefillat Geshem (Prayer for Rain), the special piyyutim recited during the Chazzan's repetition of the Mussaf Amidah on Shemini Atzeret across both Ashkenazi and Sefardi traditions. This acrostic poem, which begins with the words זְכוֹר אָב נִמְשַׁךְ אַחֲרֶיךָ כַּמַּיִם (Remember the father drawn after You like water), incorporates the Psalmist's botanical metaphor to petition for divine blessing and sustenance through rainfall: בֵּרַכְתּוֹ כְּעֵץ שָׁתוּל עַל פַּלְגֵי מַיִם. גְּנַנְתּוֹ הִצַּלְתּוֹ מֵאֵשׁ וּמִמַּיִם (You blessed him like a tree planted by streams of water; You protected him, saved him from fire and from water). The liturgical context transforms the Psalm's description of the righteous person's flourishing into a poetic meditation on Israel's dependence upon God's providential care through water and rain, making the arboreal imagery particularly resonant during the season when Jews formally petition for winter precipitation. This phrase appears only as a component of the piyyutim inserted into the Gevurot blessing, rather than as an independent liturgical unit, thereby embedding the Psalmist's wisdom within the expanded festival liturgy of the closing festival.

# Scholarly Commentary: לֹא עָמַד in Jewish Liturgy

The phrase לֹא עָמַד (he did not stand) appears in Jewish liturgy in two significant but distinct liturgical contexts, each reflecting different theological purposes within the Jewish calendar. The phrase first appears in the Megillah reading on Purim, where it is embedded within the biblical text of the Book of Esther as recited in the Siddur Sefard. In this context, the liturgy presents the dramatic moment: "לִשְׁלֹחַ יָד בִּמְבַקְשֵׁי רָעָתָם וְאִישׁ לֹא עָמַד לִפְנֵיהֶם כִּי נָפַל פַּחְדָּם עַל כָּל הָעַמִּים" (to lay hands upon those who sought their harm, and no one stood before them, for the fear of them fell upon all the peoples)—capturing the moment when the Jewish people's enemies could not resist them, a central theme of Purim's celebration of divine deliverance. The phrase appears again in the Machzor for Yom Kippur in the Ashkenazi tradition, specifically within the Seder HaAvodah (the liturgical poem describing the High Priest's Temple service), where it contributes to the elegiac tone reflecting on the absence of prophecy and divine vision in exile. These two occurrences demonstrate how the same biblical phrase serves contrasting liturgical functions: on Purim, it emphasizes victorious resistance and the triumph of the Jewish people, while on Yom Kippur, it underscores spiritual desolation and the longing for restoration, thereby enriching the theological texture of these distinct seasonal observances.

# Scholarly Commentary

The phrase בַּעֲצַת רְשָׁעִים (in the counsel of the wicked) from Psalm 1:1 appears in the Sefard liturgical tradition during the Hakafot (circuits) of Simchat Torah, performed on both the evening and morning services following Shemini Atzeret. Within this joyous celebration of the Torah, the phrase is incorporated into a Yehi Ratzon (petition) prayer that accompanies one of the seven circuits around the synagogue, where the community seeks divine protection and moral guidance. The liturgical context reads: וּתְזַכֵּנוּ שֶׁלֹּא נֵלֵךְ בַּעֲצַת רְשָׁעִים וְלֹא נִהְיֶה מֵהוֹלְכֵי רָכִיל וּלְמַעַן זְכוּת אַהֲרֹן קְדוֹשׁ יְהֹוָה הֶחָתוּם ("and merit us that we do not walk in the counsel of the wicked and are not among those who bear tales, and for the merit of Aaron the holy one, O Lord, the sealed one"). By drawing upon the opening verse of Psalms during the Hakafot, the liturgy weaves together the themes of Torah celebration with ethical petition, asking that the community be preserved from wickedness as they rejoice in the completion and renewal of the Torah cycle. This integration reflects the rabbinic principle of combining festive commemoration with moral aspiration, ensuring that the joy of Simchat Torah remains grounded in righteous conduct and spiritual integrity.
