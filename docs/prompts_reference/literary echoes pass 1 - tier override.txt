=== WHAT MAKES A GOOD ECHO ===

A good echo makes the reader say "oh!" — either because:
- The similarity is uncanny (two poets 2000 years apart reaching for the same image)
- The difference is illuminating (same artistic problem, opposite solution)
- The quoted passage is itself beautiful, moving, or funny
- The connection reveals something about the psalm's CRAFT (not just its theme)

BAD: "Both texts explore the theme of suffering and betrayal." (Thematic label, not insight.)
BAD: Comparing any betrayal psalm to Dante's Judas in Inferno XXXIV. (Banned anyway, but also: every commentary does this.)
GOOD: Comparing a psalm's false visitor to Bob Dylan's "Positively 4th Street" — because the specific mechanism (grinning friend visiting your sickbed to gloat) is identical, but the tone (Dylan's acid sarcasm vs. the psalmist's wounded paranoia) reveals something about each poet's rhetorical strategy. (Note: Dylan is now an Earned Canonical Slot — use only if the mechanism is this specific.)

=== ANALYSIS RULES ===

For each comparison, write EXACTLY 2-3 sentences of analysis. These sentences must:
- Name a SPECIFIC formal or rhetorical feature (chiastic structure, shift from third to second person, jussive mood, enjambment across stanza break, anaphoric repetition, etc.)
- Explain what the juxtaposition REVEALS about the psalm's craft or meaning
- Identify what is DIFFERENT between the two texts and why that difference matters

Do NOT:
- Summarize what the psalm verse means (the reader already knows)
- Summarize what the comparison text means (that's what the quotation is for)
- Write more than 3 sentences of analysis per comparison
- Use phrases like "both texts explore," "this theme is universal," "similarly, X also..."
