## Tier 1 — true house wit (sampler candidates)

Each passage stays flat and serious while the content does something quietly
absurd; each carries the analytical point rather than decorating it.

1. > Psalm 58 carries, in its very first line, an instruction it spends the
   > next eleven verses cheerfully ignoring.
   — Ps 58, A/B r1 essay, opening line. *The move:* deadpan accuracy; the
   whole superscription problem stated as a comic fact. "Cheerfully" is the
   one unexpected adjective doing all the work.

2. > The oddly redundant "in their mouth" — where else would teeth be? —
   > flags the target precisely.
   — Ps 58, A/B r1, v. 7. *The move:* the scholar voices the naive question
   out loud and then makes it the philological point. Self-implicating,
   argument-carrying.

3. > Evil here is meticulous. It keeps good books.
   — Ps 58, production, v. 3 (on תְּפַלֵּסוּן, the wicked "weighing out"
   violence). *The move:* one homely image (bookkeeping) after a short blunt
   sentence; lands Ibn Ezra's reading (calibrated, unnoticeable corruption)
   in six words.

4. > By borrowing that laughter, Psalm 59 quietly files Saul's surveillance
   > squad under the same heading as a global rebellion: petty thugs become a
   > specimen of cosmic insolence, and the cosmic answer fits the petty crime.
   — Ps 59, production essay (variant in A/B r1: "quietly upgrades Saul's
   neighborhood death-squad into the rebellion of 'all the nations'").
   *The move:* the faintly bureaucratic verb ("files under") applied
   straight-faced to the intertext with Ps 2; the wit IS the analysis of the
   allusion's function.

5. > A corpse instructs no one.
   — Ps 59, A/B r1, on אַל־תַּהַרְגֵם ("do not kill them, lest my people
   forget"; variant in r3: "A corpse teaches nothing for long; a wandering
   beggar is a standing lesson."). *The move:* Rashi's logic (כל המתים
   משתכחים) compressed to a four-word flat sentence. Survives being said
   flatly because it IS said flatly.

6. > Rashi reads לָנִים בְּבֵיתִי לְשָׁמְרֵנִי, "they lodge against me to
   > guard me," hearing the assault as a kind of grim houseguest.
   — Ps 59, production, v. 4. *The move:* one homely word ("houseguest")
   renders a philological observation (גור = sojourn/assail) memorable.

7. > The causative הִשְׁקִיתָנוּ preserves God as the active bartender: not
   > "we drank" but "You made us drink."
   — Ps 60, production, v. 5 (cup-of-wrath). *The move:* faintly domestic
   frame with a straight face; the grammar point (causative stem) is the
   payload. Riskiest of the set — register brushes irreverence toward God
   rather than toward the scholar's trade; keep only with that caveat.

8. > The heading promises a parade.
   — Ps 60, A/B r1 essay, opening line. *The move:* four flat words that
   set up the whole superscription-vs-body collision. Not a joke; a
   compression whose bluntness smiles.

9. > The tidy solution is that אַל־תַּשְׁחֵת is merely a tune name … no more
   > meaningful than "to the tune of Greensleeves."
   — Ps 58, A/B r2 essay. *The move:* the homely modern comparison used to
   characterize (and gently deflate) the scholarly consensus before pivoting
   to the rabbinic reading. Punches at the guild, not the text.

10. > There is a quiet aptness in using an unprecedented word for an
    > unprecedented rupture: the lexical novelty matches the geological one.
    — Ps 60, production, v. 4 (on the hapax פְּצַמְתָּהּ). *The move:* the
    smile is entirely inside the observation; nothing is performed.

## Tier 1 additions — Session 364, Pss 61/62 (Session-359 A/B writer-compare outputs)

11. > Set on the balance, men are בְּמֹאזְנַיִם לַעֲלוֹת, "on the scales, to
    > rise." The pan does not sink. It floats up. … Rank does not add a
    > single gram.
    — Ps 62 cmp, essay ("The buried metrology," closing the Book-of-the-Dead
    contrast). *The move:* a physical fact stated flatly until the last five
    words land the social verdict. The author's favorite of the set.

12. > תְּהוֹתְתוּ is a hapax — it occurs nowhere else — and the lexicons
    > throw up their hands ("text dubious," says BDB).
    — Ps 62 cmp, v. 4. *The move:* the guild personified in one gesture;
    the citation ("says BDB") is the punchline's paperwork.

13. > Sanctuary-access is the estate God deeds to the landless.
    — Ps 61 cmp, essay ("The guest who never leaves," resolving the
    גֵּר/יְרֻשָּׁה paradox after Radak). *The move:* legal vocabulary
    ("estate," "deeds") applied straight-faced; the wit IS the resolution
    of the crux.

14. > …you cannot speak of "forever" while you are still fainting at the end
    > of the earth — and when you finally can, the word for it is *tomorrow,
    > and the day after that.*
    — Ps 61 cmp, essay closing line. *The move:* the grand abstraction
    cashed out as the humblest possible phrase; doubles as a proto-affective
    landing.

15. > Horace's emperor shines; the psalmist's king is merely kept.
    — Ps 61 cmp, v. 7 (closing the Odes IV.5 comparison). *The move:*
    six-word antithesis carrying the whole theology-of-kingship contrast;
    "merely kept" does the work.

Also verified, second rank: "a fact worth stating plainly rather than
padding" (Ps 62 cmp, liturgical §, on the thin record — honest-scholar
register); "We have lost the tune; we are left with the stage direction"
(Ps 61 cmp, v. 1, on נְגִינַת).

## Tier 1 additions — Session 364, Pss 63–66 (post-359 production finals)

16. > There is no battle here, no equal cosmic rival, no narrative of war.
    > There is a participle.
    — Ps 65 prod, v. 8 (vs. Enuma Elish on מַשְׁבִּיחַ). *The move:*
    grammar as theology; the anticlimax IS the argument that God's
    sea-stilling needs no combat myth. Best single moment in the corpus.

17. > You cannot photograph an answered prayer. You can only be told.
    — Ps 66 prod, essay ("Seeing, then hearing"). *The move:* one homely
    modern noun (photograph) carries the whole seen-vs-heard epistemology.

18. > The shark at least shows its teeth. The slanderer, like Macheath,
    > smiles.
    — Ps 64 prod, v. 5 (closing the Threepenny Opera comparison). *The
    move:* the comparison's residue compressed to two flat sentences; the
    second lands the moral asymmetry.

19. > Their assault required a quiver; His answer requires one shot.
    — Ps 64 prod, v. 8 (on plural חִצָּם vs singular חֵץ). *The move:*
    the grammatical number observation as a statement of divine economy.

20. > A psalm that opens with thirst in a waterless desert ought, sooner or
    > later, to offer the thirsty man a drink. Psalm 63 never does.
    — Ps 63 prod, essay opening. *The move:* deadpan expectation-and-refusal;
    same family as exemplars 1 and 8 (essay-opening setup) — do not put more
    than one of that family in any sampler.

21. > Moral order, cosmic order, and agricultural order are not three
    > departments of a busy deity. They are one continuous grace…
    — Ps 65 prod, essay. *The move:* the bureaucratic frame ("departments,"
    "busy deity") deflates a scholarly compartmentalization; punches at how
    WE organize God, not at God.

22. > The worshiper *eats* the holy portion; the enemy *becomes* one.
    — Ps 63 prod, v. 11 (on מְנָת שֻׁעָלִים vs the חֵלֶב of v. 6). *The
    move:* cultic vocabulary's dark symmetry in eight words.

Second rank from 63-66: "Somewhere between the cosmos and the larynx, the

## Anti-patterns observed (do NOT enter a sampler)

- **The "One does not …" template.** It recurs across independent runs —
  "One does not often encounter a demolition order filed under a melody named
  'preserve'" (Ps 58 prod); "One does not easily reconcile a title that
  forbids ruin with a body that pleads for it" (Ps 58 A/B r2) — the same
  syntactic mold Session 359 already identified as over-copied (the Ps 52
  "one does not easily worship…" gold example, since deleted from the
  prompt). The content is fine; the mold is a tell. Any sampler must exclude
  it, or the model will re-anchor on it.
- **The pun that announces itself.** "A reader expecting a war-song gets a
  war-*wound*" (Ps 60 A/B r2) — the italics telegraph the punchline; RULE 13
  failure mode ("a quip that announces itself").
- **Balanced epigrams without new content** — e.g. "the change of one suffix
  is the change of the whole world" (Ps 59 A/B r1): cadence exceeds claim
  (RULE 7b).

Session 364 status check: the "One does not …" template appears in ZERO of
the seven post-58/59/60 finals (61-67, both eras) — the Session-359 deletion
worked; keep it excluded from any sampler anyway. One NEW family to watch:
the essay-opening deadpan setup ("Psalm X carries/opens/promises … and then
doesn't") now has at least four members (exemplars 1, 8, 20, and Ps 65's
"recommends that you keep quiet"). Each is individually good; a sampler
should include at most one, or the model will template essay openings.
